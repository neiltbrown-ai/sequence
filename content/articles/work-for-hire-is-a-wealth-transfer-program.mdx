<DropCap>
There is a clause in the standard contract that most creative professionals sign without negotiation. It appears in different forms — sometimes on page two, sometimes buried on page twelve — but the economic function is identical across industries, disciplines, and decades: you make it, they own it, you get paid once.
</DropCap>

It's called work-for-hire. And it is the single most efficient mechanism for transferring wealth from the people who create value to the institutions that capture it.

This isn't hyperbole. Work-for-hire is the default deal structure across the creative economy — advertising, design, film production, music, publishing, software development, photography, architecture. It is so pervasive that most creatives don't recognize it as a *choice*. They experience it as gravity. The contract arrives. The clause is in it. They sign. They move on to the next project. The value they created compounds — somewhere else, for someone else, forever.

<Subhead>The Five Clauses That Cost You</Subhead>

Work-for-hire contracts are not all identical, but the value-extracting mechanisms are consistent. Five clauses — or their functional equivalents — appear in the vast majority of creative contracts, and each one transfers a specific category of value from the creator to the institution.

<SmallSubhead>1. "All Work Product Shall Be Considered Work Made for Hire"</SmallSubhead>

This is the foundational clause. Under U.S. copyright law, when a work is "made for hire," the employer or commissioning party — not the creator — is considered the legal author. The creator has no copyright interest. No reversion rights. No future participation. The work belongs to the other party as if they had made it themselves.

<SmallSubhead>2. "Including All Derivative Works, Adaptations, and Modifications"</SmallSubhead>

This extends ownership beyond the original deliverable to anything derived from it. The logo you designed becomes the basis for a product line, a franchise, an animated character. The derivative value — which is often orders of magnitude larger than the original value — belongs entirely to the commissioning party.

<SmallSubhead>3. "In Perpetuity, Throughout the Universe, in All Media Now Known or Hereafter Devised"</SmallSubhead>

The temporal and geographic scope clause. "In perpetuity" means forever. "All media now known or hereafter devised" means every distribution technology that exists today and every one that will be invented tomorrow. When streaming was invented, rights holders who had "hereafter devised" clauses automatically captured streaming revenue from work created decades earlier. The creators who made that work received nothing additional.

<SmallSubhead>4. "Creator Hereby Assigns All Right, Title, and Interest"</SmallSubhead>

The assignment clause. Even if the work doesn't technically qualify as work-for-hire under the Copyright Act, this clause transfers ownership through assignment. It's the backup mechanism — belt and suspenders, both designed to ensure the creator retains nothing.

<SmallSubhead>5. "Creator Shall Have No Right to Additional Compensation"</SmallSubhead>

The finality clause. No royalties. No backend. No participation in future revenue, regardless of how much the work generates. The flat fee is the total compensation. If the work generates $100 or $100 million, the creator's payment is identical.

Together, these five clauses create a structure where the creator bears the creative risk, contributes the scarce input (judgment, taste, vision), and captures a fixed, typically small fraction of the value produced. The institution bears the financial risk and captures the entirety of the upside — in perpetuity, throughout the universe, in all media now known or hereafter devised.

<Subhead>What the Alternatives Look Like</Subhead>

Maggie Rogers was twenty-two years old when every major record label called. Most artists in that position sign the standard deal — the label owns the masters, the artist gets an advance and a royalty rate, and the recordings belong to the label essentially forever.

Rogers didn't sign that deal. She'd spent the previous summer transcribing interviews for a book about the New York music scene — hours of conversations with musicians who'd been chewed up by exactly this kind of arrangement. When the labels came calling, she showed up with stories from their own histories.

The result: she signed with Capitol Records through her own imprint, Debay Sounds. Capitol distributes the music. Debay Sounds owns the masters. When the licensing term expires, the rights revert. Rogers can release independently whenever she chooses — and she has.

Sylvan Esso took it further. After a decade releasing music through other people's labels, Amelia Meath and Nick Sanborn launched Psychic Hotline in 2021. The label's founding statement includes a line that no traditional record label would write: "We do not seek to own intellectual property under any circumstance."

Every artist on Psychic Hotline's roster retains full ownership of their masters. The label provides distribution, marketing, manufacturing, and creative support. They play Madison Square Garden. And they never take the IP.

<Subhead>The 34 Structures You've Never Seen</Subhead>

Work-for-hire is Structure #0 — the default, the one everybody knows, the one that ships with the standard contract. The In Sequence library documents thirty-five structures in total. Thirty-four of them give the creator more participation in the value they produce than work-for-hire does.

<SmallSubhead>Conservative (lower risk, immediate income)</SmallSubhead>

Non-Exclusive Licensing (#27) — license the same work to multiple clients, retain ownership. Exclusive Licensing (#28) — grant exclusive use for a defined period at a premium, rights revert at term end. Rights Reversion Clauses (#29) — rights automatically return if the licensee doesn't use them within a timeframe. Royalty Structures (#25) — per-unit or per-use payments that produce ongoing income from past work.

<SmallSubhead>Moderate (shared risk, shared upside)</SmallSubhead>

Revenue Share Partnership (#24) — percentage of top-line revenue, cleaner and harder to manipulate than profit participation. Hybrid Fee + Backend (#26) — guaranteed upfront payment plus percentage of revenue or profit. Equity-for-Services (#17) — reduced cash fee in exchange for ownership stake.

<SmallSubhead>Aggressive (higher risk, ownership upside)</SmallSubhead>

Subsidiary Rights Retention (#30) — retain rights to sequels, derivatives, adaptations. This is the structure George Lucas used to trade a salary cut for merchandise and sequel rights. Co-Creation Joint Venture (#5) — form a new entity with shared ownership. Holding Company Model (#9) — build a parent company that owns multiple businesses and creative assets.

<Subhead>Why This Persists</Subhead>

If work-for-hire is so economically disadvantageous for creators, why does it remain the default? Three structural reasons.

**First: information asymmetry.** Most creative professionals have never seen the alternatives. They've never heard of IP licensing with performance escalators, or revenue share partnerships, or subsidiary rights retention. The creative majority — the 60% earning $75K-$500K — negotiates without this information.

**Second: leverage imbalance.** Even when creatives know alternatives exist, they often lack the leverage to negotiate for them. The client has other options. The deadline is pressing. The rent is due. Work-for-hire is the path of least resistance.

**Third: institutional inertia.** Companies use work-for-hire because their legal departments template it, their procurement processes assume it, and nobody in the organization has thought about whether alternative structures might actually produce better creative outcomes. The irony is that work-for-hire often produces *worse* creative output — because the talent has no economic reason to push beyond acceptable.

<Subhead>The Shift That's Already Happening</Subhead>

The shift is visible across creative industries. Musicians increasingly reject traditional label structures in favor of licensing deals. Designers are exploring equity-for-services arrangements. Filmmakers are accepting reduced fees in exchange for backend participation. The structures that existed only in entertainment law textbooks are entering mainstream creative practice — because the information is spreading, the leverage is shifting, and the institutions that insist on work-for-hire are finding that the best talent is choosing to work with someone else.

The deal structure is the difference. Not talent. Not effort. Not your network. The structure determines who captures the value.

There are thirty-five structures. You've probably only ever signed one.
