## The Problem

Creators make the things culture is made of. They write the songs, design the brands, shoot the films, build the products, write the books, draft the buildings, perform the shows. And then, almost without exception, somebody else owns it.

This isn't a story about bad luck or bad negotiating. It's a story about defaults — a quiet, century-old set of contractual norms that have trained an entire profession to mistake fees for fortune, credit for ownership, and presence for power.

A film composer writes a score that lives in a beloved trilogy for fifty years and never sees a publishing check. A graphic designer launches the visual identity of a unicorn and walks away with an invoice. An advertising creative builds the campaign that defines a generation of a brand and gets a year-end bonus. A photographer shoots the image that comes to define a movement and signs the rights away on a one-page release. A novelist writes the book a streamer turns into a billion-dollar franchise and is paid an option fee she could have made waiting tables for a month.

The structural reasons aren't mysterious:

1. **The defaults are extractive.** Standard creative contracts trade upside for predictability. The institution wants the asset; the creator wants to eat. The deal that gets signed is the deal that was offered.
2. **Nobody teaches the alternatives.** Most creators have never seen the full menu — royalty trusts, holding companies, advisor equity, profit-share waterfalls, IP escrow, brand licensing, creative collectives. They negotiate inside whatever version was put in front of them.
3. **The advice industry doesn't fit them.** "Build a personal brand" / "go viral" / "hustle harder" — none of that addresses the question of who actually owns the work when the dust settles.
4. **The compounding window is silent.** Decisions made early — what entity, what split, what license, what assignment — compound for forty years. Most creators only realize what those decisions cost when the catalog is too entangled to restructure.

The result is a generation of brilliant creators who shaped culture but didn't capture it — and a deep, uncomfortable gap between the people who *make* creative value and the people who *own* it.

That gap isn't inevitable. It's a question of literacy and infrastructure. Sequence is built to close it.

## What Sequence Is

Sequence is a membership platform built on a simple premise: **a creator's career is, structurally, a business — whether they treat it that way or not.** The work they're doing today is shaping a portfolio of intellectual property, relationships, and reputational equity that will be worth something significant in twenty years. The only question is who that value flows to.

Sequence gives creators four things they don't currently have:

1. **A real picture of who they are**, structurally — a creative-identity profile that places them on a stage of value capture (Stage 1 → 4) and identifies one of eight archetypes that shapes how they should think about ownership at this point in their career. Not a Myers-Briggs. A diagnostic of how they actually negotiate, what they actually own, and what's actually possible from where they're standing.
2. **A real picture of what they have**, financially — a portfolio analysis that takes their actual projects, contracts, and revenue streams and surfaces an estimated value, a leverage score, the drivers of that valuation, and the risks dragging it down. Most creators have never had anyone do this for them. The number, when it appears, is often the first time they've seen their career as something with a balance sheet.
3. **A plan for what to do next**, structurally — a strategic roadmap with three concrete actions calibrated to where they are. Not "post more on LinkedIn." *Form this entity. Restructure that deal. Ask for these terms next time. Protect this IP before the project ships.* The kind of advice that, twenty years from now, will be the reason their catalog is worth what it's worth.
4. **A reference library and a thinking partner** — 35 codified deal structures, 100+ deeply researched case studies of creators who built ownership the right way (and the cautionary cases of those who didn't), and an AI advisor with full context on the member's profile and portfolio that they can ask anything.

Underneath all of it is one loop: **understand who you are → see what you have → know what to do next → run real deals through it → update everything.**

## How a Member Uses It

A new member's first thirty minutes:

1. **Creative Identity** — A 5-section conversational wizard. The member answers questions about their discipline, sub-discipline, work mode (maker, performer, builder, service, hybrid, transition), revenue, current ownership posture, and how they make decisions. The system places them on Stage 1–4 and assigns one of 8 archetypes. From this point forward, every recommendation across the platform is personalized to who they actually are — not a generic creator persona.
2. **Portfolio Analysis** — They add their assets: projects, IP, contracts, revenue streams, ownership stakes. The platform runs an AI analysis and returns an estimated valuation range, a leverage score, the five value drivers behind that valuation, and the top portfolio risks. This auto-triggers a Strategic Roadmap.
3. **Strategic Roadmap** — A personalized plan with their position, recent deal signal, vision, misalignment flags (six structural patterns the platform detects), three concrete next steps (foundation / positioning / momentum), recommended reading from the library, and a 1:1 advisory CTA. The plan is built from their Creative Identity + Portfolio + last 90 days of deal evaluations.

Then it becomes ongoing:

4. **Deal Evaluator** — Whenever a real deal lands — a brand partnership, a publishing offer, a co-founder equity slice, a licensing request — they run it through the evaluator. The platform scores it across multiple dimensions, returns a verdict, and produces recommended actions. If the deal carries meaningful signal, the roadmap refreshes to reflect what they just learned. The point: never sign anything important without a second pair of eyes that knows your full picture.
5. **AI Advisor** — A conversational layer over everything. The advisor knows their profile, their portfolio, their roadmap, and their last 90 days of deals. They can ask "should I form an LLC for this?" or "what's the cleanest way to take advisor equity in this client's company?" and get answers grounded in their actual situation, not search-engine boilerplate.
6. **The Library** — When they want to go deep on a structure or learn from someone who's done it before, they read.

The system tightens with use. Every deal teaches the platform more about how this creator negotiates, what they're being offered, and where their leverage actually sits. The roadmap gets sharper. The advisor gets sharper. The recommendations get sharper. Over months and years, it becomes the thing they couldn't get from a manager, a lawyer, an accountant, or a friend: a single coherent system that knows them as well as they know themselves and is calibrated to one outcome — *that they own what they make.*

## What Makes It Different

- **Structural, not tactical.** Sequence will not tell a member to build their personal brand or post more often. Recommendations are about entities, equity, IP, licensing, advisory arrangements, deal terms — the things that compound.
- **Personalized end-to-end.** A shared member-context block flows into every AI surface. The assessment shapes the portfolio analysis, the portfolio shapes the roadmap, the roadmap shapes the advisor, the advisor shapes how deals get evaluated. There is no generic mode.
- **A closed loop.** Real deals feed back into the roadmap. The platform doesn't just teach in the abstract — it learns from the member's actual deal flow and updates what it tells them.
- **Library-grounded.** Recommendations point at real structures with real precedent. The case-study corpus is the proof: this is how creators actually built ownership in the wild.
- **Honest about defaults.** Sequence will tell a member that the deal in front of them is bad, that their current entity is wrong for what they're trying to build, or that they're at Stage 1 when they thought they were at Stage 3. Calibrated for accuracy over flattery.
