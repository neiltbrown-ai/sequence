<CbHeading>The Thesis: What Neither Could Build Alone</CbHeading>

<CbDropCap>In 2005, Scott Belsky was working at Goldman Sachs on leadership development — helping the firm think about how high-performing teams function. He was not a designer. He had no product background. But he was thinking constantly about a problem that had nothing to do with Goldman Sachs: the artists he knew were extraordinary at making things and nearly invisible professionally. There was no infrastructure for creative work to find its audience.</CbDropCap>

He started approaching friends, asking them to introduce him to the best designers they knew. Then he met Matias Corea — a print and brand designer from Barcelona, trained, precise, with a visual sensibility shaped by architecture, electronic music, and typography. Corea had never designed a website. He had no product experience. But he understood, from the inside, what it meant to be a creative professional trying to make your work visible in an opaque industry.

Belsky paid Corea out of his own pocket to explore the idea. They spent months in conversation before building anything. Then they launched Behance.

<CbPullQuote>Five years bootstrapped. One venture round. Acquired by Adobe for $150 million. What they built together was not what either could have built alone. That is the point.</CbPullQuote>

They applied three structures — co-founder equity preserved through bootstrapping, a co-creation partnership that served as the product's quality control, and an advisory model that compounded the exit into a portfolio. Together, these structures turned a portfolio platform into the infrastructure for the creative economy.

</CbSection>

<CbHeading>Founder Equity: Five Years of Patience</CbHeading>

VCs repeatedly declined Behance during the bootstrap years. Belsky describes it as years of being told the mission was too sprawling to fund. The bootstrap period was not failure — it was the mechanism that preserved founder equity and forced product discipline.

<CbAccordion>
  <CbAccordionCard num="01" label="Bootstrapping as Feature, Not Failure">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Five years without outside capital meant only things that worked and generated revenue survived. Action Method products. The 99U conference. Multiple experiments, some killed. The portfolio network itself came later, after the mission was clear. **Bootstrapping forced them to earn trust before they scaled** — which is why Behance had authentic creative credibility by the time it went wide.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="02" label="The Strategic Timing of the Series A">
    <p className="cb-card-text">The first outside round came only when the company was already profitable with 1M+ users. The $6.5M Series A from Bezos Expeditions lasted just seven months before the acquisition. **Every dollar of outside money before the product is real is dilution that reduces the equity event.** Not every company can bootstrap for five years. The lesson is to be honest about when capital is necessary versus when it is a shortcut that costs more than it appears.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="03" label="Why Adobe Needed Behance">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Adobe had just launched Creative Cloud — a massive pivot from selling boxed software to subscriptions. The pivot required knowing who its customers were, what they were making, and how to maintain an ongoing relationship. **Behance had built exactly that relationship with the creative community.** The strategic fit was so clear the company did not need to prove itself to dozens of acquirers.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
</CbAccordion>

<CbPullQuote>Five years of VCs saying no. No outside capital. The founders held their equity through all of it. When Adobe paid $150M, the people who had built it got paid for building it.</CbPullQuote>

<CbHeading>Co-Creation: Orthogonal Competencies</CbHeading>

The working relationship between Belsky and Corea was not divided along simple lines. Every product decision went through two processes — Belsky's business/strategy lens and Corea's design/user lens. That dual-filter process was the mechanism that made Behance credible to two audiences simultaneously: investors and creatives.

<CbAccordion>
  <CbAccordionCard num="01" label="Design Credibility as Trust Infrastructure">
    <p className="cb-card-text">A portfolio platform for professional creatives is only as valuable as the creatives who trust it. Corea led the design of every Behance product — the network, ProSite, the 99U conference identity, Action Method products. The result was a platform that looked like it had been made by designers for designers — because it had been. **Without Corea's insider credibility, Behance would have been built by someone who understood the business problem but not the community. Those products fail because the community can tell.**</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="02" label="Business Insight Without Design, and Vice Versa">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Belsky could see the market opportunity clearly. He could not have built a product that creatives trusted. Corea understood the creative community from the inside. He had no framework for the business model or capital structure. **Neither was more important than the other. Together they were sufficient.** This is the complementary partnership in its cleanest form — not two people who do similar things at different skill levels, but two people whose competencies are genuinely orthogonal and mutually necessary.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="03" label="Two Paths, Both Legitimate">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Scott used the equity event as a platform for deploying credibility, network, and capital into new domains. Matias used it as permission to return to the work he actually wanted to do — craft, personal projects, a 14-year dream deferred. **Neither choice is wrong. Both were only possible because the equity was preserved through five years of bootstrapping.** The structure created the freedom; each founder exercised it according to his own values.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
</CbAccordion>

<CbHeading>Advisory Model: The Exit as Beginning</CbHeading>

The $150M acquisition was not Belsky's last move. It was the capital and credibility that enabled every move since. His angel investment portfolio — made largely before and during the Behance era — includes some of the most successful venture bets of the past decade.

<CbAccordion>
  <CbAccordionCard num="01" label="The Career Mistake and the Self-Correction">
    <p className="cb-card-text">In 2016, Belsky left Adobe for Benchmark as a general partner. Three weeks in, he knew it was wrong. He stayed six months to exit gracefully, then returned to Adobe as its first-ever CPO. **The willingness to recognize that what everyone tells you to do is not always what you should do — and to correct course publicly — is rare.** The messy middle applies to careers, not just companies.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="02" label="The Equity Event as Platform">
    <p className="cb-card-text">The mistake many creatives make is treating an exit as a destination. For Belsky, the acquisition was the beginning of the most productive period of his professional life. **The $150M exit funded the angel portfolio. The angel portfolio funded the credibility. The credibility funded the board seats. The board seats funded the A24 partnership.** Each move enabled the next.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
</CbAccordion>

<CbPullQuote>The acquisition enabled two different but equally legitimate choices. Scott deployed capital into new domains. Matias returned to craft. Both were only possible because the equity was preserved.</CbPullQuote>

<CbHeading>The Compounding Effect: How Three Structures Multiply</CbHeading>

The three structures form a sequence: co-founder equity preserved through bootstrapping created maximum value at acquisition, the co-creation partnership ensured the product was credible to both investors and creatives, and the advisory model converted the exit into a compounding portfolio of investments and roles.

<CbPullQuote>Behance did not just succeed as a business — it changed the conditions under which creative professionals operate. The transparency Belsky diagnosed as absent in 2005 now exists at 55 million members. This is what it looks like when creative founders build for the community they understand.</CbPullQuote>

The largest structural point: Behance made it possible for everyone who came after. Designers, photographers, illustrators, and creative directors have portfolio infrastructure that did not exist before Behance built it. The downstream value — jobs landed, clients found, careers made visible — is uncountable.

<CbSection id="lessons">

<CbHeading>Transferable Lessons</CbHeading>

<CbAccordion>
  <CbAccordionCard num="01" label="Name Your Genuine Gap Before You Find Your Partner">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Belsky knew specifically what he lacked: someone who understood design and the creative process from the inside. He searched until he found it. **Most partnership failures come from partnering with people who are similar rather than complementary** — two designers, two strategists, two people good at the same thing. Complementarity requires knowing your own edges clearly enough to know what you are missing.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="02" label="Structure Before Scale">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Five years of bootstrapping produced equity that funded everything Belsky has done since 2012. **Every dollar of outside money before the product is real is dilution that reduces the equity event when it comes.** Not every company can bootstrap for five years. The lesson is to be honest about when capital is necessary versus when it is a shortcut that costs more than it appears.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="03" label="The Mission Is the Moat">
    <p className="cb-card-text">A platform that serves a specific community with a mission that community believes in is much harder to displace than one with superior features alone. **Organize the creative world** is a statement from 2006 that still accurately describes what Behance does at 55M members. That stability of mission kept the product coherent when individual features needed to be killed.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="04" label="Self-Correction Is a Skill">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Belsky made his career mistake in full view of the industry, acknowledged it directly, and corrected it. The mistake: leaving Adobe for Benchmark. The correction: returning as CPO and spending seven years through $150B of value creation. **The willingness to say this is not my work and act on it — rather than staying to avoid embarrassment — is a compounding advantage.**</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="05" label="The Equity Event Is the Beginning of Stage 4, Not the End">
    <p className="cb-card-text">The $150M acquisition was not Belsky's last move. It was the capital and credibility that enabled every move since: angel investments, board seats, Adobe CPO, A24 partnership. **The mistake many creatives make is treating an exit as a destination.** For Belsky, it was the beginning of the most productive period of his professional life.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
