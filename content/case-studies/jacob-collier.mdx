<CbSection id="thesis">

<CbHeading>The Thesis: Build the Label Before You Need the Label</CbHeading>

<CbDropCap>In 2014, Quincy Jones sent Jacob Collier an email. This was not a networking move. Jones — the producer of Thriller, the arranger of Count Basie and Frank Sinatra, the man with more Grammy nominations than anyone in history — had watched a YouTube video of a nineteen-year-old in his family's back room in North London playing twelve instruments simultaneously, harmonizing with himself across split-screen tracks, building chord structures that should not exist in the key he was playing in, and making all of it feel like an act of pure joy.</CbDropCap>

Jones forwarded the video to everyone he knew. He flew Collier to Montreux, introduced him around, and told his management company to sign him at any cost.

Collier said: **not yet.**

He wanted to finish making the album himself first. In his room. Alone. With no label, no budget, no co-producers, and no one to answer to. That decision — made at nineteen, in the presence of the most connected man in the music industry — is the structural hinge of the whole case.

<CbPullQuote>Collier did not refuse Quincy Jones. He refused to let the industry's infrastructure arrive before his own was built. By the time he needed distribution, he had Hajanga Records: his own label, his own masters, his own terms. Interscope and Decca arrived later, as partners, not owners.</CbPullQuote>

He applied three structures — founder equity through his own label, a product partnership structure for the Djesse distribution deal, and premium positioning built on Generative Taste (defining new standards rather than meeting existing ones). Together, these structures turned a bedroom YouTube practice into seven Grammys and owned masters across 50 songs.

</CbSection>

<CbHeading>Hajanga Records: Ownership Before Need</CbHeading>

The most structurally significant decision in Collier's career happened at nineteen, when he established Hajanga Records as the entity that would hold his master recordings — before he had a debut album, before he had a Grammy, before he had the industry leverage to demand favorable terms.

<CbSubheading>The Distinction That Defined Everything</CbSubheading>

/>

<CbAccordion>
  <CbAccordionCard num="01" label="Accept Mentorship, Defer Ownership">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Jones offered to sign Collier to his label. Collier accepted the mentorship and management relationship while deferring the label relationship. **Jones became the most powerful advocate Collier had — introducing him to Herbie Hancock, Hillary Clinton, Queen Rania of Jordan — all without owning a single bar of Collier's music.** The distinction sounds subtle. The consequences were enormous.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="02" label="Build the Vehicle Before the Music Has Value">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Hajanga Records existed as an entity before the music had commercial value. **When commercial value arrived, it arrived into a structure Collier controlled — not into a structure someone else owned.** This is the inversion of the conventional artist path. The ownership vehicle comes before the need for it, while the option still exists without commercial pressure forcing the terms.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="03" label="The Interscope Deal Arrived as Partnership, Not Purchase">
    <p className="cb-card-text">When Collier signed the Djesse distribution deal in 2018, he arrived with two Grammy wins, a self-released debut, and a four-volume concept already fully developed. **Interscope and Decca came in as distribution infrastructure for IP Collier already owned — not as the entity that owned him.** Nick Groff at Interscope described his position: it is rare to meet an artist who has a vividly clear understanding of what they want to do for the next four albums.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
</CbAccordion>

<CbPullQuote>The people who want to be associated with genuinely original work often want the association more than they need the ownership. Collier's music was so clearly original that Jones wanted connection to it more than he wanted a cut of it.</CbPullQuote>

<CbHeading>The Djesse Arc: 50 Songs, Four Volumes, One Owned IP Arc</CbHeading>

No major label in 2018 would have greenlit a four-volume, 50-song, multi-genre concept album series as a debut project for a 23-year-old. The Djesse project exists because Collier had, by 2018, removed himself from the category of artist who needs label approval to proceed.

<CbAccordion>
  <CbAccordionCard num="01" label="Clarity as Leverage">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Collier arrived at the Djesse negotiation with a complete creative vision for four albums and a clear sense of which musical territory each volume would occupy. **That clarity was not naivete. It was leverage.** A label cannot direct a creative vision it does not understand, and cannot redirect a vision the artist has already fully developed. The label's role was distribution and marketing, not creative oversight.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="02" label="Every Credit Says Hajanga First">
    <p className="cb-card-text">The deal structure: Hajanga Records / Decca / Geffen for Vol. 1, evolving to Hajanga / Decca / Interscope from Vol. 2 onward. **In every iteration, Hajanga Records appears first.** The masters are Collier's. The creative decisions are not subject to label approval. When streaming platforms pay for *Moon River* or *All Night Long*, the money flows to Hajanga.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="03" label="Zero Albums That Sound Like Each Other">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Vol. 1 is orchestral. Vol. 2 is acoustic and intimate. Vol. 3 is collaborative pop and R&B. Vol. 4 is synthesis. **Zero albums in the series sound like each other. Zero sound like anything that existed before them.** This range is only possible because the structural setup removed the need for label approval of creative direction. Generative Taste requires the freedom to pursue ideas that no existing category contains. That freedom is structural, not attitudinal.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
</CbAccordion>

<CbHeading>Generative Taste: Defining Standards, Not Meeting Them</CbHeading>

Collier is technically extraordinary — four-octave vocal range, twelve-instrument fluency, harmonic depth. But technique is not what makes his case structurally interesting. What makes it structurally interesting is that he consistently **defines new standards rather than meeting existing ones.**

<CbSubheading>The MIT Live Rig</CbSubheading>

Ben Bloomberg, a PhD student at MIT Media Lab, watched Collier's YouTube videos and contacted him about building a live performance system. The problem: the videos showed music of extraordinary complexity — 12-instrument, 40-part harmony — that existed only in split-screen. How do you perform that live, alone, without losing either the complexity or the spontaneity?

<CbAccordion>
  <CbAccordionCard num="01" label="A Single-Person Live Rig That Did Not Exist">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Bloomberg and Collier built something that had never existed before: **a single-person live rig capable of 6 simultaneous looping stations, custom vocal harmonizers, real-time 3D-captured video loops, all on a single laptop.** The rig does not run on a click track or a timer. It reads Collier's performance in real time and responds to it — so the show is never on autopilot.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="02" label="Fans Attend Consecutive Shows in Different Cities">
    <p className="cb-card-text">The experience is specific enough and unrepeatable enough that seeing it once is insufficient. Ben Bloomberg: *We are starting to see fans show up again and again to consecutive cities because something different happens every single night.* **Every other touring musician competes on repertoire and performance quality. Collier competes on a system that produces experiences no other live music context can match.**</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
</CbAccordion>

<CbSubheading>The Audience Choir</CbSubheading>

During the Djesse World Tour, Collier began an experiment: what if the audience was not a passive recipient but an active musical instrument? Collier divides the audience into three sections, assigns each a pitch using hand gestures, and conducts them through increasingly complex harmonic progressions — unrehearsed, with 2,000 strangers who have never sung together.

<CbMetrics>
  <CbMetric value="100K" label="A Cappella Choir Video (2022)" />
  <CbMetric value="2,000" label="Strangers Per Show" />
  <CbMetric value="250" label="Hideaway Retreat Participants" />
</CbMetrics>

In Rome, he took the audience choir chromatic. In Melbourne, an observer noted he was *playing two thousand strangers better than a lot of people can play the piano.* Native Instruments built the **Audience Choir plugin** — a free vocal instrument based on recordings made during the tour. Collier retains the underlying IP.

<CbPullQuote>Generative Taste means seeing a new way of doing something that no one was doing yet. The audience choir is not a gimmick. It is the insight that a concert audience is a musical instrument waiting to be played. That insight is not technically difficult to execute. It is conceptually original to conceive.</CbPullQuote>

</CbSection>

<CbSection id="compounding">

<CbHeading>The Compounding Effect: One Core Asset, Radiating Out</CbHeading>

Collier's system is centered on a single core asset — Generative Taste combined with owned masters — that radiates into every subsequent opportunity. The Hajanga entity holds the IP. The Djesse arc extends it. The MIT rig makes the live experience irreproducible. The audience choir converts listeners into participants. The Hideaway Retreat packages the pedagogy. Each element reinforces the others.

<CbPullQuote>He built the instrument. He built the label. He built the audience. In that order. Seven Grammys later, his masters are his, his live rig is unreplicable, and his audience is literally his instrument.</CbPullQuote>

The AOTY nomination in 2025 — competing against Beyonce, Taylor Swift, Sabrina Carpenter, Billie Eilish — vindicates the creative bet that made the Djesse series possible. No one approves a bet like that from the outside. You have to own the infrastructure that lets you take it yourself.

<CbSection id="lessons">

<CbHeading>Transferable Lessons</CbHeading>

<CbAccordion>
  <CbAccordionCard num="01" label="Build the Ownership Structure Before You Need It">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Collier established Hajanga Records before he had an album to put in it. **The label existed as an entity before the music had commercial value — which meant that when commercial value arrived, it arrived into a structure he controlled rather than one someone else owned.** Whatever your discipline, build the entity that will hold your IP before you are negotiating under commercial pressure.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="02" label="Accept Mentorship; Defer Ownership">
    <p className="cb-card-text">The Jones relationship is a template for how to benefit from the most powerful people in an industry without surrendering the asset that makes them want to know you. **Take the introductions, take the advocacy, take the creative dialogue. Structure those relationships as mentorship and representation, not as partnerships that extract a share of what you are building.** The people who want to be associated with genuinely original work often want the association more than they need the ownership.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="03" label="Make the Live Experience Irreproducible">
    <p className="cb-card-text">The touring economy has been disrupted by streaming, but live performance retains value precisely because it cannot be digitally replicated. **Collier invested in making his live experience structurally irreproducible — not just better than others, but categorically different in a way that requires his specific system to produce.** Every creative practitioner who tours, teaches, or performs should ask: what would make this experience impossible to replicate without me?</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="04" label="Convert Passive Audience into Active Community">
    <p className="cb-card-text">The difference between an audience and a community is participation. Collier's fans do not listen to his concerts; they sing in them. They do not observe his retreats; they learn in them. **Each of these formats converts passive consumption into active participation — and active participants have a fundamentally different relationship to the work than passive consumers.** Find the participation mechanic native to your discipline and build it in from the beginning.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="05" label="Let Creative Ambition Exceed What the Market Will Approve">
    <p className="cb-card-text">The Djesse series — four volumes, 50 songs, spanning orchestral jazz to neo-soul to folk to pop, released over six years — would never have received label approval as a pitch from an unsigned artist. **It happened because Collier had built the structural independence to execute it without approval.** Generative Taste requires the freedom to pursue ideas that no existing category contains. That freedom is structural, not just attitudinal.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
</CbAccordion>
