<CbSection id="thesis">

<CbHeading>The Thesis: Constraint Is a Moat, Not a Limitation</CbHeading>

<CbDropCap>Mark Duplass has a math equation. Companies normally pay X for things. They are thrilled that Duplass Brothers can deliver for 0.5x. He can make it for 0.25x. Everybody wins. That equation has sustained a 29-year career spanning micro-budget films, HBO series, Netflix deals, and Apple TV+. His models were not Hollywood producers. They were New Orleans jazz musicians: load your own gear, do your own soundcheck, do everything yourself.</CbDropCap>

When every streamer passed on Penelope — a young adult series he wrote during COVID — Duplass used his Morning Show paycheck to finance it himself, premiered at Sundance, then licensed it piecemeal: Netflix for US streaming, Fremantle for international, retaining VOD rights and the power to greenlight season two himself. He calls it the Cassavetes model: acting in hits to self-finance your own work.

<CbPullQuote>Duplass is the most directly transferable case in the inventory for the core audience. His net worth ($12M) is modest by entertainment standards. But his model is replicable: start with zero budget, build a reputation for quality at radically low cost, use high-paying gigs to fund ownership projects, license rights piecemeal, compound over decades.</CbPullQuote>

For the library, Duplass is the **constraint-based production case** — the creative who turned low budgets from a limitation into a structural moat. When you deliver quality at below-market cost, buyers always say yes because the risk is so low. He is also the **Cassavetes bridge case**: using high-paying execution work (The Morning Show acting) to fund ownership-oriented creative work (Penelope, Creep Tapes, The Long Long Night).

</CbSection>

Micro-budget films establish the reputation ($15K Puffy Chair, $15K Creep). Platform deals follow the proof (Netflix four-picture, HBO overall). Act in hits for the paycheck (Morning Show, $2–4M/yr). Use the paycheck to self-finance (Penelope, Creep Tapes, Long Long Night). License piecemeal to retain control (US to Netflix, international to Fremantle, VOD retained). Retain IP and greenlight power (no cancellation risk, season 2 is their decision).

The hub is "Deliver 0.5x, Make It 0.25x" because the entire flywheel depends on the cost advantage. When you can make things cheaply, buyers always say yes. When buyers always say yes, you always have options.

<CbSection id="lessons">

<CbHeading>Transferable Lessons</CbHeading>

<CbAccordion>
  <CbAccordionCard num="01" label="Make Constraint Your Competitive Advantage">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Whatever your budget is, make it a feature, not a bug. Deliver quality at radically below-market cost. Clients and partners love this — the risk is so low that saying yes is easy. Duplass has never struggled to find distribution because the investment required is so small that every buyer can justify the bet.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="02" label="Use High-Paying Execution Work to Fund Ownership Projects">
    <p className="cb-card-text">The Cassavetes model: take the well-paying gig, then invest the income in work you own. The Morning Show funds Penelope. This is the most practical advice in the framework for the audience earning $75K–$500K. You don't quit the day job to build your empire — you use the day job's income to fund it.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="03" label="License Rights Piecemeal, Not as a Package">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Every right you sell separately is a negotiating point. US streaming, international distribution, VOD, educational, merchandise, sequel rights — each can be sold to the highest bidder for that territory. By self-financing, Duplass can sell each right separately instead of selling everything to one buyer at a discount. Same principle Miranda uses with Hamilton.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="04" label="Build for 30 Years, Not 3">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Duplass's $12M net worth did not come from one hit — it came from 29 years of steady, constraint-driven output. The Puffy Chair → Cyrus → Room 104 → The Morning Show → Penelope. Each project built on the last. The cumulative body of work and relationships built over decades created the infrastructure. Consistency compounds.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="05" label="What Wouldn't Transfer">
    <p className="cb-card-text">**The Morning Show paycheck.** Self-financing TV requires capital. Duplass earns $2–4M/year from Apple TV+. Most indie creatives lack this level of income. The Cassavetes model requires being castable (or hireable) in high-budget productions. **29 years of relationships.** Decades-long connections with Ted Sarandos (Netflix), HBO executives, and the festival circuit open doors that do not open for newcomers.</p>
    <p className="cb-card-text">**But the constraint-based production model transfers completely.** Make things cheaply. Deliver below-market cost. Use execution income to fund ownership projects. License rights separately. Compound over decades through consistency. Even Duplass admits it is hard — but it works.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
</CbAccordion>
