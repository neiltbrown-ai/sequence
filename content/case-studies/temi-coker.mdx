<CbHeading>The Thesis: Style as Intellectual Property</CbHeading>

<CbDropCap>Temi Coker didn't build a design agency or hire a team of fifty. He built something more structurally interesting: a recognizable aesthetic so distinctive that brands pay to borrow it. His vibrant, Afrofuturistic visual language — layered photography, bold color, typographic play — became the product itself. Not a service for hire, but an IP asset that Apple, Adobe, and Google licensed on terms that compounded his creative and financial position with each successive deal.</CbDropCap>

Most designers compete on execution — delivering deliverables faster, cheaper, or at higher fidelity. Coker competed on recognition. And then he did what most individual creators never do: he **structured deals that scaled the value of that recognition across multiple partnerships simultaneously, without giving up ownership of the underlying work.**

<CbPullQuote>Temi Coker proved that an individual creator's aesthetic — when sufficiently distinctive and properly structured — can function as licensable IP, not just a service capability.</CbPullQuote>

He applied three distinct structures — product partnerships with major brands, non-exclusive licensing that multiplied revenue from single assets, and selective exclusive licenses that commanded premium fees. Together, these structures turned a personal style into an appreciating asset. This case study breaks down each structure, shows how Coker applied it, and extracts the principles any creative professional can use.

</CbSection>

<CbHeading>Product Partnership Model: Co-Creating With Brands That Have Distribution</CbHeading>

Coker's first structural shift was moving from flat-fee commissions to product partnerships. Instead of being hired to execute a brief, he co-developed products with brands that had existing distribution — meaning his creative output reached millions of users without him building infrastructure. He contributed creative IP; the brand contributed manufacturing, distribution, and marketing. Revenue was shared based on contribution.

The insight was that his aesthetic — not his hours — was the valuable input. A flat-fee model pays for time. A product partnership model pays for **the creative asset itself, which can generate ongoing revenue as the product sells or the campaign performs.**

<CbSubheading>How It Works</CbSubheading>

<CbAccordion>
  <CbAccordionCard num="01" label="Why Brands Accept Revenue Sharing">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Brands with massive distribution — Apple, Google, Adobe — don't need to buy out creative IP cheaply. They need distinctive creative that differentiates their campaigns. For them, an 8–12% revenue share is negligible relative to campaign value. For the creator, **it's the difference between $10K and $100K+.** The deal structure works because the value asymmetry favors both sides.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="02" label="The 'Creative Direction Retained' Clause">
    <p className="cb-card-text">The critical contractual element: Coker retains approval over how the work is used.   </CbAccordionCard>

<CbPullQuote>The product partnership didn't just change the economics of one project. It changed the framing of every project after it. Once Apple co-signs your aesthetic, you're not pitching — you're being courted.</CbPullQuote>

<CbHeading>Non-Exclusive Licensing: One Asset, Multiple Revenue Streams</CbHeading>

The second structural layer was non-exclusive licensing. Coker retained full ownership of his work and licensed usage rights to multiple brands simultaneously. The same artwork — or variations of the same visual system — could be licensed to Apple for marketing, to Adobe for product integration, and to a print publisher for direct sales. Each license was scoped by territory, duration, and medium.

This is the licensing model that musicians have used for decades — a single recording generates revenue from radio play, streaming, sync licensing, and physical sales simultaneously. Coker applied the same logic to visual art. **The key: he never sold the underlying IP. Every deal was a rental, not a sale.**

<CbSubheading>How It Works</CbSubheading>

<CbAccordion>
  <CbAccordionCard num="01" label="Territory and Medium Scoping">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Each license defines exactly where and how the work can be used. Apple's license might cover North American digital advertising for 18 months. This leaves European rights, print rights, and merchandise rights available for separate licensing to other parties. **Precise scoping is what makes non-exclusive licensing possible without conflicts.**</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="02" label="The Stacking Effect">
    <p className="cb-card-text">When the same artwork is licensed to three brands at $50K each, total revenue is $150K. If that artwork had been sold as a flat-fee buyout, the creator might have received $15K. **Non-exclusive licensing allows revenue multiplication from a single creative investment.** The math is simple, but most designers never structure deals this way.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="03" label="Duration Windows and Renewal Revenue">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Every license has an expiration date. When an Apple license expires after 18 months, Coker can renew it at a higher rate — given proven performance — or license to a competitor. **Expiration dates create recurring revenue moments and renegotiation leverage.** This is the same mechanic that makes music catalog licensing valuable.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="04" label="Usage Auditing and Compliance">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Non-exclusive licensing requires tracking who is using the work, where, and for how long. Coker maintains a licensing tracker documenting every active license, its scope, expiration, and payment schedule. **Without this, rights conflicts become inevitable.** The administrative overhead is minimal relative to the revenue increase.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
</CbAccordion>

<CbHeading>Exclusive Licensing: Premium Fees for Temporary Exclusivity</CbHeading>

The third structure is the complement to non-exclusive licensing: granting a single brand exclusive rights to specific work for a defined period. Exclusivity commands a premium — typically 3–5x the non-exclusive rate for the same work. The critical structural element: all rights revert to the creator when the exclusivity period expires.

Coker used exclusive licensing selectively — for flagship campaigns where a brand needed guaranteed differentiation. The Google Pixel campaign was an exclusive license: Google paid a premium for the assurance that Samsung, Apple, or any competitor couldn't use the same visual language during the campaign window. **After 18 months, all rights reverted to Coker.**

<CbSubheading>How It Works</CbSubheading>

<CbAccordion>
  <CbAccordionCard num="01" label="When to Grant Exclusivity (and When Not To)">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Exclusivity should only be granted when the premium compensates for lost non-exclusive revenue. If three non-exclusive licenses would generate $150K, exclusivity needs to pay at least $150K — and ideally more, since it also blocks future licensing during the term. **Coker's rule: exclusive only for flagship campaigns where the brand premium justifies the opportunity cost.**</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="02" label="The Rights Reversion Clause">
    <p className="cb-card-text">The most important contract term in exclusive licensing. After the exclusivity period, **all rights revert to the creator automatically.** No renegotiation required. This prevents the work from being shelved — a brand can't lock up the IP and then not use it. If they stop using it, Coker regains full control.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="03" label="Exclusivity as Brand Credential">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Being the exclusive creative partner for a Google Pixel campaign is itself a career asset. The exclusivity period generates revenue, but it also generates brand association that outlasts the contract. **After the 18-month Google window closed, Coker's association with the Pixel campaign continued to drive inbound interest** from other brands.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="04" label="Staggering Exclusive and Non-Exclusive Deals">
    <p className="cb-card-text">The advanced play: Coker runs exclusive licenses on specific pieces or campaigns while simultaneously maintaining non-exclusive licenses on other work in his portfolio. **The portfolio approach means exclusivity on one project doesn't freeze all revenue** — it coexists with a broader licensing business running in parallel.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
</CbAccordion>

The exclusive licensing structure completes the trio. Used selectively, it generates premium revenue that funds more creative production — while the non-exclusive model ensures ongoing income from the broader portfolio. **The combination of all three structures is what creates the compounding effect.**

<CbHeading>The Compounding Effect: How Three Structures Multiply</CbHeading>

The three structures are not independent. They form a flywheel: product partnerships create work that gets seen by millions, building brand recognition. Non-exclusive licensing multiplies the revenue from each creative asset across multiple clients. And selective exclusive licensing commands premium fees that fund more creative production. Each structure strengthens the others.

<CbPullQuote>Most creators apply one deal structure to every project. Coker applied different structures to different opportunities — and the result was not additive. It was compounding. Each deal type fed the others.</CbPullQuote>

You do not need to have Apple and Google calling. But you can apply the same logic: use product partnerships to get your work in front of audiences, retain ownership so you can license the same work multiple times, and save exclusivity for the deals where the premium justifies the constraint. The structures work at any scale.

<CbSection id="lessons">

<CbHeading>Transferable Lessons</CbHeading>

<CbAccordion>
  <CbAccordionCard num="01" label="Your Aesthetic Is IP — Structure It That Way">
    <p className="cb-card-text">If clients hire you for your distinctive visual style, that style is intellectual property. Treat it as licensable IP, not as a service you deliver per-hour. This means retaining ownership of underlying work, licensing usage rights rather than selling assets, and **building a portfolio that functions as a catalog with ongoing revenue potential.**</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="02" label="Move From Flat Fees to Revenue Participation Early">
    <p className="cb-card-text">The single highest-leverage structural change a designer can make: shift from flat-fee buyouts to deals that include revenue sharing or licensing fees. **Even a small backend percentage on a successful campaign pays multiples of a flat fee.** The sooner you make this shift, the sooner your revenue compounds.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="03" label="License Non-Exclusively by Default">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Non-exclusive licensing should be the default because it preserves optionality. Every exclusive deal closes doors. **Reserve exclusivity for flagship opportunities where the premium is substantial and the brand association is strategically valuable.** For everything else, license broadly and stack revenue.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="04" label="Every Deal Is a Credential for the Next Deal">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Structure deals with an eye toward what they enable, not just what they pay. An Apple partnership that pays $50K but leads to a Google pitch worth $180K was not a $50K deal — **it was a $230K sequence.** Think in terms of deal chains, not isolated transactions.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="05" label="Retain Ownership — Even When It Costs You Upfront">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Coker consistently accepted lower upfront fees in exchange for retaining IP ownership. Early in his career, this meant earning less per project. But **retained ownership is what made non-exclusive licensing, exclusive licensing, and direct-to-consumer revenue possible later.** Ownership is the foundation every other structure builds on.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
