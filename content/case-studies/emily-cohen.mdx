<CbSection id="thesis">

<CbHeading>The Thesis: Operational Excellence Is Necessary But Not Sufficient for Stage 3</CbHeading>

<CbDropCap>This case study is built differently from the others in the In Sequence library. Most cases document creators who have made the Stage 2 → Stage 3 transition that the framework is designed to teach. **This case documents someone who has built a sustainable, decades-long Stage 2 practice and has not made the Stage 3 move** — and who has, through her own writing and speaking, documented many of the operational problems that prevent creative firms from making the transition.</CbDropCap>

Cohen's body of work is the most thoroughly documented description of **how to run a creative service firm well** that exists in the field. Her book *Brutally Honest* and her ongoing *PRINT Magazine* "Business of Creativity" column with daughter and business partner Hunter Vargas catalog the operational issues that small-to-medium creative firms face: pricing, positioning, staffing, client management, business development, organizational structure, founder fatigue, conflict resolution.

<CbPullQuote>What her work mostly does not address is the structural value-capture move from running a service firm well (Stage 1 / Stage 2) to building outcome-based, equity-bearing, IP-generative income streams (Stage 3). Cohen's practice is itself a Stage 2 service business serving other Stage 2 service businesses.</CbPullQuote>

The structural lesson for the Creative Majority that emerges from Cohen's case is twofold. First, **operational excellence at Stage 2 is real and worth pursuing** — and Cohen is a credible source on it. Second, **operational excellence is not the same as structural transition**, and a creator who optimizes their service business indefinitely without ever attempting the Stage 3 move can build a respectable career that nonetheless caps at the income ceiling of service work.

The two bodies of work — Cohen's and In Sequence's — are complementary rather than competitive. **Operational discipline first, structural transition second.**

<CbHeading>Advisory/Consultant Model: The Foundation</CbHeading>

Cohen's entire career is the canonical example of structure #4 in the In Sequence library: **Advisory/Consultant Model**, where the practitioner has transitioned from hands-on creative execution to strategic guidance and is paid premium rates for expertise and recommendations rather than for production work.

<CbHeading>Retainer Model: The Recurring Revenue Engine</CbHeading>

The revenue model evolved over time. Early career was tactical work — proposal-writing, pricing negotiations, individual project advice. Mid-career and forward shifted toward **strategic business planning retreats**, where Cohen spends a full day with a firm's principal(s) helping them assess current state and plan forward.

The retreat model is structurally important because it productizes a deliverable (a day-long facilitated planning session) at a clean fee, then converts most clients to retainer relationships afterward. This is **consultant productization done correctly**: the retreat is the entry product, the retainer is the recurring product, and the relationship typically continues for years.

<CbSubheading>The Maximum Scale of a Solo Consulting Practice</CbSubheading>

By the 2010s, Cohen was running approximately 30 to 40 clients on retainer simultaneously, plus one-time retreats and engagements, plus university teaching, plus conference speaking. **The practice was visibly scaled within the limits of what a single-principal consultancy can scale to.**

<CbAccordion>
  <CbAccordionCard num="01" label="The Retreat → Retainer Conversion">
    <p className="cb-card-text">A facilitated day-long planning session at a clean fee. Most clients convert to retainer relationships afterward. **The retreat is the entry product, the retainer is the recurring product**, and the relationship typically continues for years.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="02" label="The Sweet Spot: 3–10 Person Firms">
    <p className="cb-card-text">The optimal range is three to ten employees. **Large enough to have actual operational complexity** (multiple designers, multiple clients, real revenue at risk) and small enough that the principal can absorb advice and implement it without bureaucratic obstruction.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="03" label="The Ceiling Beyond 30–40 Retainer Clients">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Adding more clients past this point requires either **(a) hiring associates who do the actual work** (which changes the value proposition, since clients are often paying specifically for the named principal's attention) or **(b) productizing further into courses, books, and licensed methodologies** (which reduces the per-engagement margin). Cohen has been navigating both forks simultaneously.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="04" label="The Casa Davka Succession Move">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Adding Hunter as business partner is structurally a **25-to-30-year extension of the practice's enterprise life.** A solo Cohen practice ends when Cohen retires. A mother-daughter Casa Davka practice continues, in some form, for as long as Hunter wants to run it.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
</CbAccordion>

<CbHeading>Royalty / Productized IP: Brutally Honest as the Productization Move</CbHeading>

In 2017, Cohen began work on what would become *Brutally Honest: No-Bullshit Strategies to Evolve Your Creative Business*. The book was conceived as a way to make her advisory thinking accessible to designers who could not afford or did not need full retainer engagement.

It is not Stage 3 transition content.</p>

**Reputational compounding within a defined community.** The American creative industry is a relatively small community at the senior-firm-principal level. Over 30 years of consulting, speaking, writing, and AIGA involvement, **Cohen has become known to most of the 2,000–5,000 senior creative firm principals** in the United States who are plausible clients. **The acquisition cost of new clients is structurally zero because the reputation is the acquisition mechanism.**

**Methodology refinement through volume.** Cohen has worked with 500+ creative firms over three decades. **The methodology improves with each new engagement because each engagement reveals edge cases the previous ones did not.**

**Industry-level visibility through advocacy.** The PRINT column has elevated Cohen from "respected consultant" to "industry-defining voice." **Industry-defining voices set the terms of conversation, attract clients who specifically want to work with them, and command premium rates** because the brand is itself a credential.

**Generational continuity through Casa Davka.** A solo Cohen practice ends when Cohen retires. A mother-daughter Casa Davka practice continues. **The kind of structural decision that does not pay off in the year it is made; it pays off in the decades after the senior partner is no longer working.**

<CbSection id="lessons">

<CbHeading>Transferable Lessons</CbHeading>

<CbAccordion>
  <CbAccordionCard num="01" label="The Side-Hustle-Before-Quitting Pattern Is One of the Highest-Confidence Career Moves">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Cohen built her consulting practice to roughly full-time scale on the side of her day job before she quit. The husband's intervention was the trigger, but **the readiness to quit was demonstrated by the fact that the consulting work was already producing 20–40 hours per week of revenue without active solicitation.**</p>
    <p className="cb-card-text">The signal-set: outside demand without active solicitation; repeated requests for the same kind of work; willingness to pay rates comparable to or higher than the day-job hourly rate; word-of-mouth referrals. **When all four signals are present, the side hustle has crossed the viability threshold.**</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="02" label="Productize the IP Through a Designed Object That Is Itself a Credential">
    <p className="cb-card-text">*Brutally Honest* sold 5,000+ copies because the content was good. **It sold for higher prices than equivalent advice-based books because the object itself was beautiful.** And it functioned as a credential for Cohen's consulting practice because the book demonstrated that she takes craft seriously enough to produce a book that designers would want to own.</p>
    <p className="cb-card-text">**The mismatch between the consultant's product and the consultant's clients' standards is a credibility signal that operates beneath the rational layer of evaluation.**</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="03" label="Build Succession Into the Practice While You Are Still in It">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Hunter Vargas joined in summer 2022. Cohen was still at full operational capacity. **A rushed succession (senior partner exits abruptly, junior partner inherits clients) usually fails because the client relationships were with the senior partner specifically.** A patient succession produces a practice that can operate without the senior partner because the trust has been deliberately rebuilt around the junior partner during the senior partner's continued involvement.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="04" label="Industry Advocacy Is the Bridge From Individual-Client Work to Platform-Level Positioning">
    <p className="cb-card-text">The transition from running 30–40 client retainers to **writing the *PRINT Magazine* column and speaking at industry conferences as a main-stage figure** is the structural move from "respected consultant" to "industry-defining voice."</p>
    <p className="cb-card-text">**Individual-client work produces income but does not produce platform.** Each engagement is a discrete revenue event. Industry-level advocacy produces platform — an audience that follows the consultant across multiple engagements, multiple years, and multiple topics — and the platform itself becomes a commercial asset.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="05" label="Operational Excellence Is Necessary But Not Sufficient for the Stage 3 Transition">
    <p className="cb-card-text">**This is the meta-frame lesson.** Cohen has documented operational excellence at Stage 2 with more rigor than most consultants in the field. *Brutally Honest* and the PRINT column are excellent guides to running a creative service business well. They are not, however, guides to making the structural transition from service-business income to outcome-based, equity-bearing, IP-generative income.</p>
    <p className="cb-card-text">**Operational excellence is a prerequisite for Stage 3, not a substitute for it.** A service business that is run badly cannot make the Stage 3 transition because the cash flow, the client relationships, and the practitioner's bandwidth are all consumed by operational firefighting. A service business that is run well can make the Stage 3 transition because the operational stability creates the bandwidth and the financial cushion required to take the structural risks that Stage 3 involves.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
</CbAccordion>
