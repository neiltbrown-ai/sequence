<CbHeading>The Thesis: Design the Deal, Not Just the Work</CbHeading>

<CbDropCap>In the late 1990s, Kristian Andersen was a freelance designer in Indianapolis building websites and configuring email servers on workstations shoved under his desk. Today he is a co-founder and partner at High Alpha, a venture studio that has launched 40+ companies, backed 100+ founders, and manages over $385 million across four funds. The bridge between those two points was not a career pivot. It was a series of compounding ownership moves — equity stakes taken in lieu of full design fees, a venture fund launched from inside an agency, companies co-founded with clients-turned-partners — that transformed a design practice into an institutional platform for building businesses.</CbDropCap>

Andersen did not stop being a designer. He applied design thinking to the thing most designers never touch: the deal itself.

<CbPullQuote>Each equity position was both a business relationship and a learning opportunity. He was studying deal mechanics, valuation, cap tables, and exit dynamics — not in an MBA classroom, but from inside the deals themselves. The equity education proved more valuable than any individual stake.</CbPullQuote>

For the library, Andersen is the **full-progression case** — the only creative professional in the inventory who has completed all four stages (Execution → Judgment → Ownership → Capital). He is also the definitive **equity-for-services case** at institutional scale: what starts as a designer trading reduced fees for small equity stakes ends, 27 years later, as a $385M venture platform. Every stage funded the next. Nothing was skipped.

</CbSection>

<CbSubheading>The Counterfactual</CbSubheading>

If Andersen had stayed on the traditional design agency path — growing Studio Science without equity positions, venture funds, or company co-founding — he would likely have built a successful regional consultancy generating $3–8M in annual revenue. Instead, by systematically converting creative judgment into ownership positions and then institutionalizing that model, he became a partner in a $385M venture platform. The difference is not just financial. It is structural: he moved from selling time to owning equity in an institution that creates and funds companies.

Design for clients (cash for Fortune 500, equity for startups). Equity positions teach deal mechanics (cap tables, valuations, exits — from inside the deals). Formalize the pattern as a venture fund (Gravity Ventures, 6 funds). Co-found companies from inside the agency (Studio Science Labs: 5 SaaS startups, 300+ jobs, $75M+ capital). Institutionalize at scale (High Alpha: $385M AUM, 90+ companies). Exit the agency to focus on the capital platform (Studio Science sold 2019). And the cycle continues at institutional scale.

The hub is "Each Stage Funds the Next" because the flywheel's engine is the compounding progression: design skills → client relationships → equity positions → venture fund → company co-founding → institutional platform. Nothing was skipped. Each transition was possible only because the previous stage had been fully developed.

<CbSection id="lessons">

<CbHeading>Transferable Lessons</CbHeading>

<CbAccordion>
  <CbAccordionCard num="01" label="Start Taking Equity Positions Now — Even Small Ones">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Even 0.5% to 2% stakes in early-stage clients in exchange for reduced fees build a portfolio and — more importantly — financial literacy. Every equity position is a curriculum in startup economics. Andersen described it as a back door education in early-stage startup finance. The education compounds even when individual stakes do not pay off.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="02" label="Let the Service Business Fund the Ownership Experiments">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Studio Science's cash-paying client base subsidized every equity experiment and venture activity. Andersen never needed the equity positions to pay rent. The dual-track model — service income funding ownership exploration — is the safest path for creatives entering the equity game. Same principle as Joey L (commercial funds documentary), Fried (Basecamp funds ONCE), or Chamberlain (content funds coffee company).</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="03" label="Formalize When the Pattern Proves Out">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Andersen did not start with a venture fund. He started with informal equity positions. Then a small ragtag fund. Then company co-founding. Then an institutional platform. Each step was justified by the evidence of the previous one. Do not skip to the institutional stage. Let the progression earn its own momentum.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="04" label="Design the Model, Not Just the Companies">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Andersen's distinctive contribution was not any single investment or product. It was applying design thinking to the venture studio model itself — the organizational structure, innovation frameworks, naming, identity, and go-to-market approach of High Alpha. Designers and creatives can shape not just the work but the business model, the organizational structure, and the ownership architecture.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="05" label="What Wouldn't Transfer">
    <p className="cb-card-text">**Co-founder access.** The partnership with Scott Dorsey (ExactTarget → Salesforce) provided institutional credibility and LP access that a solo designer would not have. **B2B SaaS focus.** The venture studio model works particularly well in enterprise software, where design quality is a genuine competitive advantage. **27-year timeline.** The full progression took nearly three decades. The compounding was real but slow. **Indianapolis ecosystem building.** The Speak Easy, Harrison Center, TechPoint involvement generated deal flow and goodwill that a purely transactional approach would not replicate.</p>
    <p className="cb-card-text">**But the compounding logic of equity-for-services transfers completely.** Start taking equity now. Let your service business fund the risk. Formalize when the pattern proves out. Apply your creative discipline to the deal itself.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
</CbAccordion>
