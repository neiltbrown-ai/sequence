<CbSection id="thesis">

<CbHeading>The Thesis: Be in the Cap Table, Not Just the Deliverable</CbHeading>

<CbDropCap>In 2004, Yves Béhar walked into a struggling startup that had been reduced to four employees and offered to keep designing for them — for free. Aliph, the company behind the original Jawbone Bluetooth headset, couldn't afford his fees. So instead of billing them, fuseproject took an equity stake. The moment realigned what an industrial design firm could be. Twenty years later, Béhar's agency has worked as equity owner, partner, or co-founder on more than 100 startups, sold itself to a Chinese conglomerate for a reported $46.7 million, and been bought back by its founder.</CbDropCap>

The structural argument is escalation through every stage the In Sequence framework names. Béhar moved from billable industrial designer (Stage 1), to equity-taking creative partner (Stage 2), to co-founder with an operating role (Stage 3), to capital allocator leading a Series A (Stage 4). **The same person occupies all four roles simultaneously today.**

<CbPullQuote>A creative who wants to capture the upside of work they shape has to find a way to be in the cap table, the royalty stack, or the founding team — not just the deliverable. A studio can design a category-defining object and watch the manufacturer take the entire downstream return.</CbPullQuote>

We read five structures from the In Sequence library against Béhar's career: project equity (the Aliph pivot), founder/co-founder equity (August Home, TELO, and the fuseproject sale itself), a holding-company reading of the firm's tiered architecture, royalty structures (Herman Miller), and diversified revenue across all three layers. Béhar didn't pick these from a deal-structure menu — Aliph couldn't pay, he wanted to keep working, and equity was what was available. The structural names came later. The fit between what fuseproject did and how the structures behave is what makes the case useful.

</CbSection>

<CbHeading>Project Equity: The Aliph Pivot</CbHeading>

Aliph, the developer of the Jawbone noise-canceling headset, hired fuseproject around 2004 to design the first commercial version of the device. The first version drew design-press acclaim but had connectivity and distribution problems; the company contracted from roughly 20 employees to four. Aliph couldn't afford to keep paying for outside design help. **Most agencies would have moved on.** Béhar wanted to keep iterating — he believed in the product, had revised the design five times, traveled to Asia to oversee production, and built packaging he felt the device deserved.

Béhar approached Aliph's CEO, Hosain Rahman, with a different proposition: instead of work-for-hire, fuseproject would take an equity stake in the company in exchange for ongoing design work. Rahman agreed. Béhar joined Aliph as VP and creative director, a role that ran into Jawbone's later years as Chief Creative Officer (a title Pitchbook records as held from 2003 to 2017). Fuseproject COO Mitch Pergola described the moment as a lightbulb — design work could be exchanged for ownership rather than hours.

<CbHeading>Founder Equity: Past Advisor-Equity Into Co-Founding</CbHeading>

As the venture-design model matured, the most ambitious projects required Béhar to commit deeper than equity-for-services. The line between designer-with-equity and operating co-founder thinned. Some products he'd designed (the Jambox, Up wristband, OLPC XO laptop) had become category-defining objects whose business outcomes were tied to ongoing design judgment, not a one-time delivery.

The clearest case is **August Home (2012)**, co-founded with technology entrepreneur Jason Johnson, with Béhar taking the title of Chief Creative Officer. August raised $2M in seed from a roster of Silicon Valley angels at founding, scaled through Series A and B rounds, was acquired by Swedish lock conglomerate Assa Abloy in 2017, and was sold again as part of a portfolio in 2023 to Fortune Brands for $800M (the combined Yale + August locks division, cash, debt-free basis). The same period included co-founder roles at Canopy, Forme Life, and Ouya (creative founder of the open game console, which raised $8.5M on Kickstarter in 2012).

    <p className="cb-card-text">Béhar moved from advisor-equity to operating co-founder where the project required it. **Some products demand the designer remain on the cap table because the design judgment is structurally inseparable from the product roadmap.** The CCO title is the visible part; the underlying shift is from one-time grant to ongoing operating equity. When the judgment isn't inseparable, advisor equity or a non-participation grant is cleaner.</p>

<CbHeading>Holding Company: The Firm as Asset, Then the Lead Check</CbHeading>

By 2014 fuseproject had 75 employees across San Francisco and New York and a deep client roster. Béhar told *Fast Company* the firm had been "approached by other companies — from big corporations to internet and IT firms to big advertising conglomerates." **The agency had become an asset that strategics wanted to own.** In July 2014 Béhar sold a 75% stake in fuseproject to Chinese marketing conglomerate BlueFocus Communication Group; the Financial Times reported the price at $46.7 million in cash, paid out over several years tied to performance milestones. BlueFocus took full ownership in 2017. Béhar remained CEO across both transactions.

In September 2023, Béhar bought fuseproject back from BlueFocus for an undisclosed sum and pivoted strategy back toward the equity-partner and co-founder model. In September 2025, he led TELO's oversubscribed $20M Series A as the named lead investor. **This is the structural inflection: Béhar is no longer the designer-with-equity in someone else's deal. He is the lead check.**

<CbAccordion>
  <CbAccordionCard num="01" label="The Tiered Architecture as a Functional Holding Company">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Fuseproject's tiered architecture — services-and-royalties + equity portfolio + co-founder operating roles — functions as a design-firm version of a holding company. **Béhar and Pergola did not legally structure the firm as a parent-and-subsidiaries model;** the tiered cash and ownership streams that flow through the agency are our analytical reading of how the firm actually generated and captured value. They described it as a model that "let us make fewer bets but stick with them."</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="02" label="The Agency Itself as Founder-Equity Exit">
    <p className="cb-card-text">The 2014 BlueFocus transaction is the founder-equity exit on fuseproject itself. Béhar started as 100% owner, sold 75% for a reported $46.7M in 2014, sold the remaining 25% in 2017, and reacquired ownership in 2023. **A reported $46.7M for 75% implies a firm valuation that is hard to defend on services revenue alone** — and the equity portfolio underneath it is what closed the gap.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="03" label="The Buyback Was a Correction">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Béhar characterized the post-2014 firm as having drifted toward "essentially a hired gun design firm" — losing the venture-design posture that made the model distinctive. The 2023 buyback was an explicit correction, returning fuseproject to the equity-partner and co-founder posture. The 2025 TELO Series A lead is the proof point: design judgment as the lead investment, not the contributed creative.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>

<CbSection id="compounding">

<CbHeading>The Compounding Effect: Four Roles, One Person, Simultaneously</CbHeading>

The four structures are sequenced but cumulative — Béhar didn't graduate out of each stage, he stacked them. Project equity (2004) funded the move to co-founding (2012). Co-founding produced the track record that made the firm an asset (2014). Owning the firm outright again (2023) is what let him deploy capital as the lead check (2025). **The same person is billable designer, equity partner, operating co-founder, and lead investor today.**

<CbSection id="lessons">

<CbHeading>Transferable Lessons</CbHeading>

<CbAccordion>
  <CbAccordionCard num="01" label="Trade Cash for Ownership Where the Project Warrants It">
    <p className="cb-card-text">The Aliph pivot was forced — Aliph couldn't pay, Béhar wanted to keep working, equity was what was available. **A creative who wants to capture the upside of work they shape has to find a way to be in the cap table, the royalty stack, or the founding team — not just the deliverable.** Identify one early-stage venture project where the founders are open to equity-for-services. Negotiate equity in lieu of fee reduction.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="02" label="Build a Tiered Model So Service Work Funds the Bets">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Strategic engagements with Samsung, J&J, Herman Miller, and Coca-Cola produced predictable cash flow that **underwrote speculative equity work with startups.** Identify one strategic client engagement that produces predictable retainer-scale cash flow. That's the floor. The equity bets sit on top of it, not in competition with it.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="03" label="Move to Co-Founding Selectively">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Béhar moved from advisor-equity to operating co-founder where the design judgment was structurally inseparable from the product roadmap (August, TELO, Canopy). **When it is, an ongoing CCO/co-founder role is appropriate; when it isn't, advisor equity or a non-participation grant is cleaner.** Don't take an operating seat on every deal — it doesn't scale and it dilutes the editing discipline.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="04" label="Editing Matters as Much as Scaling">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Pergola named editing — saying no — as the firm's first core value. **The equity model only works when the firm picks fewer bets and commits longer.** Hold the line on editing: refuse the second and third equity-only deals until the first has resolved or matured. Concentration matters more than diversification at small portfolio sizes; illiquid equity is only valuable in concentrated portfolios.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="05" label="What Wouldn't Transfer">
    <p className="cb-card-text">**Silicon Valley vintage matters.** Béhar built the venture-design firm in San Francisco in the late 1990s and early 2000s, when the regional design-engineering-startup network was small enough that one well-placed designer could be on first-name terms with most of the VCs and consumer-tech founders who'd populate the next two decades. **Identity is part of the model.** Béhar's Swiss-Turkish background, ArtCenter pedigree, and modernist-poetic aesthetic are constitutive — the brand isn't separable from the person, which is both a strength (an asset that compounds) and a constraint (the firm cannot be cleanly succeeded). **Tier-1 client gravity took years to build.** The strategic engagements with Samsung, Coca-Cola, Herman Miller, and J&J that funded the equity bets were earned over a decade of premium service work; a younger firm cannot replicate the floor without the same patience. **Financial data is estimated.** Specific equity stakes in portfolio companies, royalty percentages, and exit proceeds for everything except the BlueFocus transaction are not on the public record; figures here are based on industry comparables for designer-led co-founder roles at Series Seed through Series A scale.</p>
    <p className="cb-card-text">**But the structural pattern is universal.** A creative who wants to capture the upside of work they shape has to be in the cap table, the royalty stack, or the founding team — not just the deliverable. Trade some cash compensation for ownership where the project warrants it; build a tiered revenue model so service work funds speculative bets rather than competing with them; move from advisor equity to operating co-founder where the design judgment is structurally inseparable from the product; treat editing — saying no — as a core competency, because illiquid equity is only valuable in concentrated portfolios. These principles work whether the firm is 75 designers across two cities or one designer with a laptop, and whether the equity is in a smart lock company or a regional bakery.</p>
