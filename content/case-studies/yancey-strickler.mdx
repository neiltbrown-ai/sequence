<CbHeading>The Thesis: Move Up the Stack of Where Ownership Gets Decided</CbHeading>

<CbDropCap>In April 2026, Colorado's Senate passed SB 26-133 by a 31–3 vote — a bill that would create the country's first legal entity designed specifically for creative people. Artist Corporations would lock 51% artist control into statute, keep intellectual property from transferring to non-artist investors, and let economic rights be separated from governance rights. The architect of that legislation is the same person who, twenty years earlier, was earning $75 per concert preview for The Village Voice. The arc from freelance fees to drafting state law is not a financial story. It's a story about what happens when one person spends a quarter-century moving up the stack of where creative ownership actually gets decided.</CbDropCap>

Each move in Strickler's career built at a higher layer than the last. Layer one: write the criticism — the work itself. Layer two: build the funding platform (Kickstarter, 2009) — the capital layer. Layer three: build the release platform (Metalabel, 2022) — the distribution-and-treasury layer. Layer four: build the legal entity (Artist Corporations, 2025–26) — the structural-form layer where every later deal gets done. **Each layer makes the layer below it more useful.** And the move that financed the whole climb wasn't an exit — it was the 2015 decision to convert Kickstarter into a Public Benefit Corporation that pays dividends instead of ever selling.

<CbPullQuote>The cap on a freelance career is the per-piece rate. The cap on a platform is its pledge volume. The cap on a legal form is a generation of working creatives. Each layer up the stack, the same effort buys more leverage.</CbPullQuote>

Strickler is unusual in this library: he now designs ownership structures for a living. Even so, the numbered structures below are our reading, not his playbook — he didn't co-found Kickstarter in 2009 to "apply the Creator-as-Platform Model," and Metalabel wasn't assembled from a deal-structure menu. We're reading twenty-six years of decisions backward through a framework built later. The fit between what he built and how the structures behave is what makes the case useful.

</CbSection>

<CbHeading>Strickler's Evolution</CbHeading>

Five eras across twenty-six years. Three structural transitions — journalism to platform equity (2009), exit-track equity to dividend-paying PBC (2015), platform-building to legal-form-building (2025) — each one moving the work up a layer in the ownership stack.

<CbHeading>Creator-as-Platform, Applied Twice at Two Layers</CbHeading>

Kickstarter wasn't Strickler's individual practice — it was infrastructure other creators used to capture value, and his cofounder equity participated in that infrastructure's growth. The arrangement is structured as the Creator-as-Platform Model, though in 2009 there was no menu to pick from: Strickler, Chen, and Adler were inventing what crowdfunding would mean for creative work. In its first decade the platform processed more than $4.6 billion in pledges across 170,000+ successfully funded projects; by April 2025 the running totals had grown past $8.7 billion and 277,000 projects, per Kickstarter's own published numbers.

Metalabel is the same structure re-applied one layer up. Where Kickstarter funded individual creator *projects*, Metalabel formalizes the *group* as the unit — small teams ("squads") releasing work together, with treasury splits applied automatically as money comes in. The platform mechanics are publicly documented: a 10% commission on releases, and a $100,000 regenerating Advance Pool (launched August 2023) that fronts creators money on a 50/50 split until recouped. Metalabel's own revenue and user counts are not disclosed.

The best evidence for the model is a single release with published economics. Strickler chose to publish a full P&L for the *Dark Forest Anthology of the Internet* in a 2025 essay — an unusually complete worked example:

Several years in, the platform is not a billion-dollar venture outcome. **The thesis is that the platform's value accrues to the creators releasing through it and to the broader body of work — not to a startup-style multiple.** On the venture-economics axis, that thesis is unproven, and an honest reading of this case says so.</p>

<CbHeading>Founder Equity With a Dividend Instead of an Exit</CbHeading>

In September 2015, Kickstarter reincorporated as a Delaware Public Benefit Corporation — a legal form only available since 2013 — and Strickler and Chen committed publicly, in the New York Times, that the company would never sell or go public. Returns would flow to investors and employees as an annual dividend instead. The investors on the cap table — Union Square Ventures, Jack Dorsey, Chris Sacca among them — agreed in advance. **This is the load-bearing structural decision of the entire case.** Strickler has confirmed on the record (in a Long-Term Stock Exchange podcast with Eric Ries) that Kickstarter has been profitable since month 14 and began paying an annual dividend after the conversion. His specific equity percentage and the dividend's dollar size are private.

<CbPullQuote>"We don't ever want to sell or go public. That would push the company to make choices that we don't think are in the best interest of the company." — Strickler to the New York Times, 2015</CbPullQuote>

<CbHeading>The Constellation: Metalabel Studios and the Legal-Form Layer</CbHeading>

What Strickler now calls "Metalabel Studios" sits across Metalabel.com (the marketplace), the Artist Corporations push, DFOS (Dark Forest Operating System, 2025), and earlier projects like Bentoism. It **functions as** a holding-company model — the verb matters, because Strickler didn't set out to build a holding company. He built each project, and the holding framing emerged as a way to describe what the constellation does. Similarly, Metalabel's squad ethos — small egalitarian teams, shared treasuries, automatic splits — **functions as** a <CbStructureBadge num="07" href="/library/structures/platform-cooperative-model" inline /> Platform Cooperative, even though Metalabel, Inc. is a Delaware C-corp with conventional venture investors. The ethos is co-op; the cap table is C-corp.

<CbAccordion>
  <CbAccordionCard num="01" label="The Squad Is the Operating Unit, Not the Individual">
    <p className="cb-card-text">From the Dark Forest Collective (16 writers releasing books together) to Metalabel's squad framing to the A-Corp's separation of governance from economic rights, the consistent thesis is that **the creative person should be one node inside a small group, not a one-person LLC trying to do everything.** The lineage Strickler cites runs through Dischord Records, the Royal Society, and the Guerrilla Girls — and the A-Corp is the attempt to make that shape legible to insurance companies and the IRS.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="02" label="Why the Legal Layer Pays Differently">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Kickstarter improved funding for the projects that ran through it. Metalabel improves economics for the groups that release through it. **A state statute improves the default position of every artist who incorporates under it — including ones who never touch a Strickler platform.** By mid-2024, Strickler's own Metalabel releases were generating six-figure cumulative revenue without an LLC, which forced the legal-entity question in his own work first. The A-Corp is that question answered at the field level.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="03" label="From Platform Access to Institutional Access">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Drafting a bill, recruiting a 501c3 board with the CEO of Wondaland on it, securing bipartisan sponsors in a state legislature — this is institutional work. **Twelve years running Kickstarter built the access that made it possible.** The Senate vote was 31–3; the bill awaits a House vote, and Strickler has said he expects final enactment by July 2027. The outcome at this layer is genuinely still in motion.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>

<CbSection id="compounding">

<CbHeading>The Compounding Effect: Four Layers, Each a Precondition for the Next</CbHeading>

The structures here are sequenced, not parallel. Freelance criticism seeded the indie-label instinct. The platform equity from Kickstarter — restructured in 2015 for dividends instead of exit — financed the writing years. The writing years produced the audience and the thesis that became Metalabel. And Metalabel's operating experience (groups, splits, treasuries, the six-figure-revenue-without-an-LLC problem) produced the Artist Corporation. **Remove any layer and the ones above it don't exist.**

<CbPullQuote>The Stage-4 outcome here isn't a $4 billion exit. It's the infrastructure that lets eleven collaborators turn an essay into an MIT-collected paperback in 72 hours, with revenue automatically split and the treasury funded for the next release — now being formalized into state law.</CbPullQuote>

</CbSection>

<CbSection id="lessons">

<CbHeading>Transferable Lessons</CbHeading>

<CbAccordion>
  <CbAccordionCard num="01" label="Build the Small Institution Alongside the Work">
    <p className="cb-card-text">In his criticism years Strickler also ran zines and a small record label — operator positions held at roughly $0 revenue. **Anything that puts you in the operator seat, however small, is where the leverage starts compounding.** When the Kickstarter opportunity appeared in 2009, it fit a question his label-and-zine work had been answering implicitly for years: how do creative projects get funded when gatekeepers say no?</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="02" label="Take Cofounder Equity, Not Contractor Pay">
    <p className="cb-card-text">The Kickstarter cofounder equity is the compounding asset of this entire case; the CEO salary — documented at under 6x the median employee — was a smaller contributor. **When a platform-building opportunity appears, the seat on the cap table is worth more than the rate you could bill.** Everything after 2017 was financed by equity structured well, not by wages saved.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="03" label="Structure for Distribution, Not Exit">
    <p className="cb-card-text">The 2015 PBC conversion plus dividend bought Strickler a decade of writing and framework-building that exit-driven equity would not have. **The form matters less than the explicit refusal of the default sell-or-IPO assumption** — a PBC with dividends is one path; a cooperative is another; a foundation a third. The Etsy contrast is the control group: same year, opposite choice, public-market shareholder loop.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="04" label="Reinvest Latitude One Layer Up the Stack">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Strickler didn't reinvest his post-CEO latitude in more crowdfunding. Each move built at the layer above the last: work → funding platform → release platform → legal form. **Don't reinvest only in the current platform — reinvest in the distribution, legal-form, or advocacy layer that makes the current one more useful to the next generation.** The layer where ownership gets decided is where the leverage lives.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="05" label="Release as a Group, Write the Splits Down First">
    <p className="cb-card-text">The Dark Forest Anthology worked because the structural choices — splits, treasury, mission — were written into the release before money arrived: $30K flowed to eleven authors automatically, $4K to the collective treasury, no invoicing, no renegotiation. **Squad up when the work supports it, and treat each successful release as the seed of the next layer of infrastructure rather than a one-off win.**</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="06" label="What Wouldn't Transfer">
    <p className="cb-card-text">**The Kickstarter cofounder seat.** Co-founding the defining crowdfunding platform in 2009, with USV, Jack Dorsey, and Chris Sacca on the cap table, was a once-in-a-generation entry into the platform layer of the creator economy. **The 2015 PBC moment.** The Delaware Public Benefit Corporation form had existed for only two years; Kickstarter had an aligned investor base and a business profitable since month 14 that didn't need an IPO. That combination is hard to replicate. **The institutional access.** Drafting a state bill, recruiting a 501c3 board that includes Janelle Monáe's manager, and winning bipartisan sponsors is institutional work — twelve years running Kickstarter built that access. **Financial data is estimated.** Strickler's income, equity percentages, dividend amounts, and book advance are private; figures below the level of what he has personally disclosed are inferred from industry comparables.</p>
    <p className="cb-card-text">**But the structural pattern is universal.** Working creatives can move up the stack of where their ownership gets decided: build a small institution alongside the work, even at $0 revenue; take equity in any platform-like infrastructure you help build; insist on dividend or revenue-share rights when an exit isn't the plan; release as a small group with the splits written down before money is at stake; and treat each successful project as the seed of the next layer of infrastructure. These principles work whether the platform serves $8.7 billion in pledges or $66,000 in book sales.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
</CbAccordion>
