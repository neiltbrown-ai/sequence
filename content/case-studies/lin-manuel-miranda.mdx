<CbHeading>The Thesis: The Industry's Default Deal Structure Determines Whether the Creator Captures Generational Wealth</CbHeading>

<CbDropCap>Lin-Manuel Miranda wrote the first draft of In the Heights as a college sophomore. Seventeen years later, Hamilton had grossed over $1 billion on Broadway alone. Miranda receives 7% of the adjusted gross revenue from every Broadway performance — roughly $105,000 per week at peak. He gets up to 8.5% from every touring and international production. Disney paid $75 million for the filmed Broadway production. His total earnings from Hamilton exceed $200 million. And the show has not even entered its high school licensing phase — that launches in 2028 through Concord Theatricals, opening a revenue stream that could generate millions annually for decades.</CbDropCap>

Miranda owns Hamilton. Not metaphorically. Literally. Under the Dramatists Guild framework, the author retains copyright and the producer acquires a license to produce — not ownership of the work. This means Miranda owns Hamilton in a way that Rae does not own Insecure, Glover does not own Atlanta, and most creatives never own their defining work.

<CbPullQuote>Miranda left the show in 2016. Hamilton has generated hundreds of millions since — running eight shows a week whether Miranda is in the theater or on vacation. This is the ultimate test of ownership: does your IP generate revenue when you are not personally working?</CbPullQuote>

For the library, Miranda is the **IP royalty model** pushed to its purest expression. He is also the clearest demonstration of why the ownership structure you start with determines your financial outcome. The actionable insight is not "create a generational hit." It is: understand the ownership structures of your industry, and either work within favorable ones or negotiate for better terms before you need them.

</CbSection>

Write everything — sole authorship of book, music, and lyrics maximizes royalty share. Cultural phenomenon ($1B+ gross) generates extraordinary returns on the ownership position. Phase the rollout (Broadway exclusivity → touring → Disney+ film → amateur licensing). License the long tail (Concord Theatricals 2028 — decades of school/amateur revenue). IP works without the creator (Miranda left in 2016; show generates millions weekly). Free to build new IP (Warriors, Disney songs, 5000 Broadway Productions).

The hub is "Own the Copyright" because the entire flywheel depends on the Dramatists Guild framework giving the author copyright ownership. Without that structural foundation, every other element collapses.

<CbHeading>Transferable Lessons</CbHeading>

<CbAccordion>
  <CbAccordionCard num="01" label="Understand Your Industry's Ownership Structures Before You Need Them">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Broadway (Dramatists Guild) gives authors copyright ownership by default. Film gives studios ownership. TV gives networks ownership. Music labels typically own masters. The industry you work in — and its standard deal structures — determines how much of the value you create flows back to you. Learn the structures before you sign anything.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="02" label="Phase Your IP Rollout to Maximize Each Window">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Do not rush to license everything simultaneously. Hamilton's rollout: protect scarcity (Broadway exclusivity) → expand gradually (touring) → make the strategic film deal (Disney+ $75M) → open amateur licensing last (Concord 2028). Each phase maximized revenue for that window before opening the next. This applies to any creative work at any scale.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="03" label="Build IP That Works Without You">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Miranda left Hamilton in 2016. The show has generated hundreds of millions since — eight performances a week, whether Miranda is in the theater or on vacation. This is the ultimate test of ownership. Compare: McKinnon's YouTube income requires new videos. Most freelancers' income requires personal labor. Build something that generates revenue when you are not personally working.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="04" label="Stay Non-Exclusive to Preserve Leverage">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Miranda works with Disney extensively (Moana, Encanto, Mufasa) but never signed an exclusive deal. Disney must compete for his attention on each project. This preserves his ability to negotiate from strength indefinitely. If someone wants exclusivity, the price should be extraordinary.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="05" label="What Wouldn't Transfer">
    <p className="cb-card-text">**The Dramatists Guild framework.** This author-ownership model is specific to Broadway/theater. Film, TV, and most music default to studio/label ownership. **Hamilton-level cultural phenomenon.** $1B+ Broadway gross requires a generational hit. **Sole authorship.** Most creators collaborate; Miranda wrote all three components (book, music, lyrics) alone. **16-year development timeline.** College draft to generational hit required extraordinary patience.</p>
    <p className="cb-card-text">**But the IP ownership principles transfer completely.** Learn your industry's ownership structures. Phase your IP rollout. Build work that generates revenue without your presence. Stay non-exclusive. Think in decades — the most valuable phase of IP often comes last.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
