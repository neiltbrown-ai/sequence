<CbSection id="thesis">

<CbHeading>The Thesis: Final Cut Is the Asset. Refusal Is What Makes It Real.</CbHeading>

<CbDropCap>In late 2021, Coralie Fargeat had a meeting almost every up-and-coming director would have killed for. Marvel was considering her for *Black Widow*. Her debut feature *Revenge* (2017) had been a critical breakthrough; she was on the shortlist for major studio franchise work. She turned it down. The reason, in her own words years later: the prospect of a studio film did not appeal to her, as she would not receive final cut. She knew, before signing anything, that the structural arrangement of a studio franchise would deny her the one thing she would not negotiate away. So she didn't take the meeting any further.</CbDropCap>

She stayed in Los Angeles, sat down at a Silver Lake coffee shop, and started writing the spec script for what would become *The Substance*. She made herself a producer on the project, formed her own production company in Paris (Blacksmith) specifically to co-produce it, and made certain — contractually, in writing, before financing closed — that she would have final cut.

Three years later, when Universal demanded extensive recuts of the 140-minute version Fargeat delivered, she refused. The contract held. Universal could not force the changes. So they sold the worldwide distribution rights to Mubi for $12.5M weeks before Cannes — a $5.5M paper loss — rather than release the film as it stood. Mubi released the film as Fargeat had cut it. **It grossed $79.1M, earned 5 Academy Award nominations, won Cannes Best Screenplay, and made Fargeat the 9th woman ever nominated for Best Director at the Oscars.**

<CbPullQuote>Most working directors treat final cut as a negotiating point. Fargeat treated it as a precondition. The structural difference between those two postures is what produced everything that came next.</CbPullQuote>

This case study breaks down three structures: the Final Cut Provision (#35) negotiated upstream of the financier, the Three-Party JV (#5) that gave Fargeat producer-owner standing, and the Holding Company (#9) she formed at Stage 2 — before the inflection — to substitute for accumulated track record.

<CbHeading>Final Cut Provision: One Clause, Properly Defended</CbHeading>

Final cut is a contractual provision that grants the director (or producer/creative principal) the legal right to determine the final theatrical release version. With final cut, the studio cannot unilaterally re-edit the film, change the ending, or modify runtime against the director's wishes. **The director's cut is the studio's cut.**

Final cut is granted to a small number of directors based on track record (Coppola, Spielberg, Nolan, Tarantino) or as a deal precondition negotiated by directors with sufficient leverage to demand it. **Fargeat is unusual because she demanded and received final cut on her *second* feature, with leverage drawn from one prior film.**

<CbSubheading>Negotiated Upstream of the Financier</CbSubheading>

The structural sequence matters. Fargeat did not negotiate final cut after Universal had committed. She negotiated it as a precondition of her involvement at all, structured through Working Title before Universal's financing closed.

By the time Universal signed, the final-cut grant was already in place. **Their choice was: accept Fargeat's final-cut authority, or don't fund the project.** They accepted, financed at $17.5-18M, and became contractually bound to the provision.

<CbSubheading>How It Works</CbSubheading>

<CbAccordion>
  <CbAccordionCard num="01" label="Final Cut Survives Institutional Pressure Because It's Binary">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Most contractual provisions can be eroded through softer pressures — implicit threats about future projects, marketing dial-backs, public framing of the director as "difficult." **Final cut is harder to erode because it's binary.** Either the director's cut is the final cut, or it isn't. There's no middle ground where the studio gradually reasserts control. That binary character is what made it defensible against Universal's pressure.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="02" label="Enforceable When Refused">
    <p className="cb-card-text">When Universal demanded recuts Fargeat would not make, the contractual provision held. The studio's choices were: accept the cut, sell the rights, or breach the contract and face litigation. **Universal chose to sell the rights.** The provision worked exactly as designed. The director kept her version of the film. The system did what the system is supposed to do — both parties exercised the rights they had bargained for.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="03" label="Portable Across Projects">
    <p className="cb-card-text">A director who has demonstrated, in one project, that they will enforce final cut to the structural limit becomes, in subsequent negotiations, a director whose final-cut demand is taken seriously from the outset. **Fargeat's next deal will be structured assuming final cut is non-negotiable.** The reputation effect of having defended final cut once compounds across the entire subsequent career.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="04" label="What Final Cut Costs">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Most accounts of director-empowerment narratives omit the costs. Fargeat lost the *Black Widow* opportunity. She lost two-plus years of post-production stability when the Universal handoff threatened to kill the film entirely. She accepted "disappointment, bitterness, painful, difficult" feelings during the negotiation period. **Final cut is not a free option. It is a real cost paid in advance, against an uncertain return.** Directors who negotiate it without willingness to pay the cost are negotiating a paper provision.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
</CbAccordion>

<CbHeading>Co-Creation Joint Venture: The Three-Party Structure That Held Final Cut</CbHeading>

The legal structure underneath *The Substance* is a three-party joint venture: Blacksmith Productions (France), Working Title Films (UK), and Universal Pictures (US). Understanding this structure clarifies how Fargeat's contractual leverage was constructed at a stage when her individual standing was modest.

<CbSubheading>Why Three Parties Beat Two</CbSubheading>

A simpler structure — Fargeat directly contracting with Universal as a director-for-hire — would have been a much weaker position. Without a production company in between, the contractual leverage to negotiate final cut would have rested on Fargeat's individual standing, which after one prior feature was modest.

<CbAccordion>
  <CbAccordionCard num="01" label="Borrowed Institutional Credibility">
    <p className="cb-card-text">By interposing Working Title between herself and Universal, Fargeat essentially borrowed Working Title's 40 years of institutional credibility to negotiate the final-cut provision. **Working Title has the leverage to demand provisions that an individual director with one prior feature could not.** Working Title chose to grant final cut to Fargeat through their internal production agreement, and that grant became binding on Universal when Universal accepted the project.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="02" label="Co-Equal Production Partner, Not Talent">
    <p className="cb-card-text">By forming Blacksmith, Fargeat established herself as a co-equal production partner with Working Title rather than just talent attached to a Working Title project. **The two production companies (Blacksmith + Working Title) collectively held the production-side authority that the financing partner could not override.** A "first-position lien" on creative authority.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="03" label="Final Cut Travels With the Rights">
    <p className="cb-card-text">When Universal eventually sold to Mubi, what they sold was the *distribution rights* — not the underlying production agreement, which remained between Blacksmith and Working Title. **Mubi acquired the right to release the film. Mubi did not acquire the right to edit it.** Fargeat's final cut traveled with the rights as a structural feature of the underlying production deal.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="04" label="Replicable Beyond Film">
    <p className="cb-card-text">A writer might form an LLC and partner with a literary agency, with the publisher as financier; the contract flows through the agency-LLC structure with creative-control provisions negotiated upstream. A designer might form a studio LLC and partner with a brand-management firm with credibility with the corporate client. **Negotiating creative control with the financier directly is harder than negotiating it through institutional intermediaries.** Borrowing intermediary credibility through a JV is one of the most underappreciated structural moves in creative-economy contracting.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
</CbAccordion>

<CbHeading>Holding Company at Stage 2: Blacksmith Productions</CbHeading>

Fargeat's formation of Blacksmith Productions in 2024, specifically to produce *The Substance*, is a structural move worth analyzing on its own. Most case studies in this series document holding companies formed at Stage 3-4 (Coogler's Proximity, DuVernay's ARRAY, Tina's de facto portfolio). **Fargeat formed Blacksmith at Stage 2** — when she had one prior feature, no track record at scale, and a project that could very plausibly fail.

<CbSubheading>Why Form a Holding Company Before the Inflection?</CbSubheading>

<CbAccordion>
  <CbAccordionCard num="01" label="Producer-Owner Status Changes the Negotiation Posture">
    <p className="cb-card-text">A director attached as talent to someone else's production is a service provider. A director attached as a *producer* through her own production company is a structural partner. **The status difference is real and shows up in every subsequent meeting, contract draft, and credit decision.** Working Title and Universal had to treat Fargeat as Blacksmith's principal, not just as Fargeat the director, because Blacksmith was a contracting party. That structural reframing affected which clauses she could negotiate.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="02" label="Future Projects Compound Through It">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Blacksmith now exists. It has produced one feature. It has the legal infrastructure, contractual templates, producer-credit history, and institutional standing to produce future Fargeat projects. **The infrastructure investment was paid once, on *The Substance*. It will continue to amortize over every Fargeat project that runs through Blacksmith.** Cost was front-loaded; benefits compound.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="03" label="Why the Conventional Stage-3 Advice Is Miscalibrated">
    <p className="cb-card-text">The conventional advice — wait until Stage 3-4 to form a production company because the structure is operationally expensive at smaller scales — assumes the leverage benefits don't matter until you're already at Stage 3-4. **Fargeat's case demonstrates the opposite: leverage benefits matter especially at Stage 2**, when the creator does not yet have accumulated career leverage and needs structural compensation for that gap. The production company *substituted* for accumulated leverage in a way pure individual contracting could not.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
</CbAccordion>

<CbHeading>The Compounding Effect: Contractual Leverage Compressed Into a Single Provision</CbHeading>

The Fargeat compounding pattern is structurally distinct from every other case in the library. **Coogler compounded through accumulated track record. Stargate compounded through catalog accumulation. Tina compounded through portfolio diversification. DuVernay compounded through institutional infrastructure. Baker compounded through deferred recognition over 24 years. Fargeat's compounding ran through one contract clause, enforced when tested.**

The cycle: **Black Widow refusal** preserved optionality for the right project. **Blacksmith** formed to give Fargeat producer-owner standing. **Three-party JV** with Working Title borrowed institutional credibility. **Final cut provision** locked in upstream of Universal's financing. **Universal's refusal to release** triggered the test of the contract. **Mubi distribution** ($79.1M, 5 Oscar noms) validated the structural choice — and the entire flywheel becomes the leverage profile for every subsequent Fargeat project.

<CbPullQuote>One contract clause, enforced when tested, produced an outcome that other paths to comparable outcomes would have required ten or twenty years to achieve.</CbPullQuote>

<CbSection id="lessons">

<CbHeading>Transferable Lessons</CbHeading>

<CbAccordion>
  <CbAccordionCard num="01" label="Identify the One Contract Term You Will Not Negotiate Away">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Final cut is Fargeat's non-negotiable. Other terms (salary, marketing budget, theatrical-window length) are negotiable. **The non-negotiable is the one provision whose absence would invalidate the value of the project for you, regardless of how good the rest of the terms are.** For a writer, it might be ebook publication rights or right of refusal on adaptations. For a designer, the right to portfolio sample of finished work. For a developer, the right to maintain a copy of the source code. Identify yours. Treat it as a precondition, not a negotiating chip.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="02" label="Negotiate the Non-Negotiable Upstream of the Financier">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Fargeat negotiated final cut through Working Title *before* Universal's financing entered the deal. Universal accepted the provision as a precondition; they couldn't override what was structurally upstream. **The party who controls the money is, by definition, in a position to demand creative concessions.** The further upstream of the money your non-negotiable is locked in, the harder it is to override. Identify the institutional intermediary in your discipline (production company, agency, packager) who can negotiate your non-negotiable before the financier is on the deal.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="03" label="Be Willing to Pay the Cost of Enforcing the Contract">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Final cut is only meaningful if you're willing to pay the cost. Fargeat paid 18 months of post-production crisis, the loss of Universal's marketing infrastructure, the risk Mubi might not pick up the film. **A contractual provision the holder won't enforce is a paper provision.** Studios negotiate harder when they believe the provision will not be defended. Before signing, decide privately what you'd pay to enforce it if tested. If the answer is "not much," renegotiate other terms instead.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="04" label="Form the Production Entity Before You Need It">
    <p className="cb-card-text">Fargeat formed Blacksmith specifically to produce *The Substance*, before the project's outcome was known. **The right time to form your own corporate entity is before your next significant project, not after.** The entity changes the negotiating posture for the project itself. Waiting until after the project forfeits the negotiating advantage the entity would have provided. Setup costs are modest (a few thousand dollars in most jurisdictions); leverage effect is significant.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
  <CbAccordionCard num="05" label="Refuse Projects That Won't Honor Your Non-Negotiable">
    <p className="cb-card-text">The most underappreciated structural move in Fargeat's career is the **Black Widow refusal** — walking away from a major studio franchise opportunity *before negotiations had even started*, because she knew the structural arrangement would not honor her non-negotiable. Most working creatives accept projects that erode their non-negotiables, telling themselves they'll fight for the provision. **Most lose those fights, because the leverage to fight isn't there at Stage 2.** The Fargeat pattern is to refuse the project entirely. Costly short-term, preservative long-term.</p>
  </CbAccordionCard>
