<SbSection id="overview">

<SbHeading>Overview</SbHeading>

The Franchise/Licensing Model enables creatives to scale proven concepts by allowing others to replicate and operate them independently. Instead of opening more locations yourself (which requires massive capital and management), franchising leverages other people's capital and labor while you maintain quality standards and collect predictable recurring revenue — **upfront fees ($10K–100K+)** and **ongoing royalties (5–12% of gross revenue)**.

This differs from pure licensing (#27) in depth of relationship. Franchising typically involves brand usage rights, operational systems, training programs, quality standards, ongoing support, and territorial exclusivity. Pure licensing is simpler: use this IP, pay a fee. Franchising is a partnership — operate this business using our proven system, and we'll support you while ensuring quality.

The advantages are compelling: scalability without capital requirements, geographic reach, recurring revenue from dozens or hundreds of operators, brand amplification, and enterprise value creation (franchise systems command **6–10x EBITDA multiples** vs. 4–6x for single-location businesses). The disadvantages are real: brand risk from poor operators, training and quality control complexity, regulatory requirements (franchise disclosure laws vary by jurisdiction), upfront investment in system documentation ($50K–250K), and ongoing support obligations that demand a shift from practitioner to system operator.

<SbSection id="protect">

<SbHeading>Key Protections</SbHeading>

Five provisions that protect the franchise system, the brand, and both parties' interests.

<SbCards>
  <SbCard label="Franchise Disclosure Document (FDD)">
    <SbCardText>Required in the US — must be provided to prospective franchisees 14 days before signing. Details all fees, franchisee obligations, litigation history, and financial performance. Cost: $20–50K in legal fees. Must update annually. Item 19 (financial performance representations) is optional but builds credibility.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** FDD is legally required and your best sales tool. Disclosing real financial performance (average revenue, top quartile, bottom quartile) builds trust. Skipping it isn't just illegal — it signals you have something to hide.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Franchisee Financial Qualification">
    <SbCardText>"Minimum requirements: (a) net worth of $250K+, (b) liquid assets of $100K+, (c) credit score of 680+, (d) no bankruptcies in last 7 years." Franchisor reserves right to require higher qualifications or reject application for any reason. Background and reference checks mandatory.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Undercapitalized franchisees fail — and their failure damages your brand. The #1 lesson from every franchisor: reject marginal applicants. One failed location hurts more than 10 successful ones help.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Quality Standards + Cure Periods">
    <SbCardText>"Franchisee must maintain facility to brand standards (inspected annually), use only approved suppliers, complete training programs, maintain minimum customer satisfaction scores, submit to mystery shopping. Upon notice of violation: 30 days to cure operational issues, 10 days for payment defaults. Three uncured violations in 12 months = grounds for termination."</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** One bad franchisee damages all franchisees' reputations. Measurable standards and enforcement mechanisms protect the system. Without them, your weakest operator defines your brand.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Territory Protection">
    <SbCardText>"Franchisee granted exclusive territory defined by specific ZIP codes or radius. Franchisor may not operate or franchise within territory for duration of agreement. Franchisor retains rights to: (a) company-owned locations outside territory, (b) online sales nationally, (c) additional franchises outside territory."</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Vague territory definitions lead to encroachment disputes — the most common franchisee complaint. Define by ZIP codes, not "market area." Clear boundaries prevent cannibalization and protect franchisee investment.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Transfer + Exit Terms">
    <SbCardText>"Franchisee may sell/transfer franchise with franchisor approval. Franchisor has right of first refusal at offered price. Transfer fee of $10K due upon transfer. 2-year non-compete within territory upon sale or termination. Franchisee may terminate with 180 days notice if franchisor materially breaches."</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Clean transfer rights protect the system when franchisees want out. Right of first refusal lets you prevent bad buyers from entering. The non-compete prevents a departing franchisee from opening a competing operation next door.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
</SbCards>

</SbSection>

<SbSection id="manipulations">

<SbHeading>Common Manipulations</SbHeading>

Four tactics that extract value from franchise relationships. Two affect franchisees; two affect franchisors. Each is preventable with the right contract language.

<SbCards>
  <SbCard label="The Overpriced Supplier Scam" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Franchisor requires exclusive purchasing from "approved vendors" at 20–50% above market rates. Franchisor receives undisclosed kickbacks from suppliers. Franchisee's profit margins compress — they can't compete on pricing but can't switch vendors.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Supplier kickbacks are the most common form of franchisor overreach. If you're the franchisee, compare required supplier pricing to market rates before signing. If you're the franchisor, negotiate volume discounts and pass savings through — it builds goodwill and franchisee profitability.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>I'd like to add a supplier pricing clause: approved vendor pricing must be within 10% of independently quoted market rates, verified annually. If pricing exceeds this threshold, franchisee may source from alternative approved suppliers at market rate.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Territory Encroachment" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Franchisor grants "exclusive territory" but then opens a company-owned location nearby, sells products online at lower prices directly to your customers, or grants a franchise in an adjacent territory too close to yours — cannibalizing your customer base.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Vague territory language is designed to preserve franchisor flexibility at franchisee expense. "Market area" means whatever the franchisor decides. Only specific geographic definitions (ZIP codes, mile radius) provide real protection.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>I need territory defined by specific ZIP codes or a fixed mile radius — not "market area." The agreement should state: no company-owned or franchised locations within my defined territory for the full duration of the agreement plus any renewal terms.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Royalty Creep" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Franchise agreement allows franchisor to unilaterally increase royalties or add new fees (technology fees, marketing assessments, training fees, compliance fees) without franchisee consent. Economics deteriorate year over year as fees compound.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Uncapped fee provisions turn profitable franchises into break-even operations over time. If the agreement says "additional fees as determined by Franchisor," expect them to be determined upward.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>Royalty rate must be fixed at 8% for the initial term and all renewal terms. No additional fees may be imposed without written consent of 66% of active franchisees. Technology and marketing fund contributions capped at 3% combined.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="The Franchise-in-Name-Only Trap" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Franchisor sells "franchise" rights but provides minimal systems, training, or ongoing support. Essentially selling territorial rights to use a brand name without an actual proven methodology. Franchisee fails due to lack of support.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Premature franchising — before systems are truly documented and proven — destroys brand value and creates legal liability. If a franchisor can't show you 3+ successful locations with documented processes, they're selling a dream, not a system.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>Before I sign, I'd like to speak with at least 10 existing franchisees. I need to understand: Was training adequate? Is support responsive? Did you hit the financial projections in the FDD? If consistent complaints exist, I'll pass.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
</SbCards>

<SbSection id="negotiate">

<SbHeading>Negotiation</SbHeading>

Franchise negotiation differs depending on which side of the table you're on. Three approaches based on system maturity and franchisee profile.

<SbNeg tabsJson='["High Leverage", "Moderate", "Low Leverage"]'>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Charge premium fees for prime territories.** With 20+ successful franchisees and a waitlist, territories are the scarce asset. "This metro territory is priced at $75K franchise fee. Multi-unit commitments (3+ locations) receive 15% fee reduction."</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**Require multi-unit development agreements.** Committed franchisees sign for 3–5 locations over 5 years with development deposits. Performance requirements: minimum revenue targets, unit openings by milestone dates.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**Proven track record** (20+ locations, 3+ years). Strong Item 19 financial disclosures. Multiple applicants per territory. Low franchisee turnover (&lt;5% annual).</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Offer sliding-scale royalties for performance.** Standard 8% on first $500K revenue, 6% above $1M. Rewards successful franchisees and aligns incentives. "We want our best performers keeping more of what they earn."</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**Build Franchisee Advisory Council** (5–7 elected members) meeting quarterly with leadership. Non-binding advisory role on system improvements, marketing, vendor negotiations. Demonstrates franchisor commitment to partnership.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**10–20 locations, growing system.** Need good operators to fuel expansion. Brand recognition exists regionally but not nationally. Strong unit economics but still proving model at scale.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Offer reduced fees for early adopters** who accept higher risk. First 5 franchisees: 50% fee reduction in exchange for detailed feedback, testimonials, and willingness to be reference calls for future applicants.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**Provide exceptional support** to early franchisees. Their success IS your marketing. Over-invest in training, weekly check-ins, onsite visits. First 5 franchisees' results determine whether you can sell franchise #6–50.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**New to franchising** (fewer than 5 locations). Need success stories. Brand unknown outside home market. System documentation complete but unproven at scale.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
</SbNeg>

<SbSection id="value">

<SbHeading>Example Value Calculation: Maria's Education Studio</SbHeading>

Maria runs "Creative Kids Lab" — a STEAM education studio for ages 5–12 with 3 profitable locations in her metro area generating $1.2M combined revenue and $300K net profit. She's deciding: franchise or continue opening owned locations?

<SbChart title="5-Year Enterprise Value — Owned vs. Franchise">
  <SbChartRow label="Franchise model (28 franchisees)" value="$7.2M" pct="100" />
  <SbChartRow label="Owned locations (8 total)" value="$3.2M" pct="44" />
</SbChart>

**Without the structure (owned expansion):** Maria opens 5 more locations over 5 years. Capital required: $750K. Revenue Year 5: $3.2M from 8 locations. Net profit: $640K. Enterprise value: $2.5–3.8M. She works 60+ hours/week managing 8 locations.

**With the structure (franchise):** Maria sells 28 franchises over 5 years at $35K fee + 8% royalty. Capital required: $200K (system development). Year 5: royalty revenue $800K + franchise fees $175K + owned location profit $340K = **$1.315M annual income.** Enterprise value: **$7.2M** (9x EBITDA — strong growth trajectory, clean operations). She works 40 hours/week in the franchisor role.

**What changed:** Franchising created 2x the annual profit and 2.25x the enterprise value while requiring 73% less capital. Maria shifted from managing 8 locations to managing a system of 31 locations (3 owned + 28 franchised). Year 2 was hardest — first franchisee failure taught her to raise financial qualification standards. By Year 5, a PE firm offered $7.2M. She declined — targeting 50+ franchises before exit.

<SbSection id="cases">

<SbHeading>Example Use Cases</SbHeading>

How three creatives at different career stages franchised their proven concepts — and what happened.

<SbCase label="Case 01 · Education · Established" name="Maria Vasquez — STEAM Education Studios" tag="Education / Youth" outcome="$7.2M enterprise value in 5 years">
  <SbCaseText>Maria had 3 profitable "Creative Kids Lab" locations generating $1.2M combined. Rather than opening more owned locations at $150K each, she developed a franchise system: $35K fee, 8% royalty, 2-week training program, comprehensive operations manual.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The shift:** Year 1: 6 franchises sold ($210K fees + $96K royalties). Year 3: 18 active franchisees ($576K royalties + $210K fees). Year 5: 28 franchisees, $800K royalties, $175K fees, $340K owned locations = **$1.315M total.** Enterprise value: $7.2M at 9x EBITDA. Two failures along the way: one undercapitalized franchisee (closed Month 18), one non-compliant (terminated Year 3). Both taught her to raise standards.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** "Franchising forced me to systematize everything I'd been doing intuitively for 10 years. The operations manual alone made my owned locations more profitable. Franchisees pay me to scale what I built — and their success validates the system for future franchisees."</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>

<SbCase label="Case 02 · Design · Mid-Career" name="Tom Nakamura — Design Studio Framework" tag="Creative Services / Design" outcome="$371K Year 3 (+106% from starting)">
  <SbCaseText>Tom ran "UrbanSpace Design Agency" in San Francisco — $850K revenue, $180K profit, team of 6. Three potential partners in other cities wanted to open UrbanSpace locations. He couldn't afford $450K for 3 owned offices, so he developed a franchise model: $40K fee, 7% royalty.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The shift:** Year 1: Seattle franchise opened (validated model). Year 2: Austin added. Year 3: 2 franchises generating $650K each — royalties $91K + fees $80K + SF profit $200K = **$371K** (106% increase from $180K). 4 more cities waiting. "Franchising forced me to document processes I'd been doing intuitively for 15 years. That documentation alone made my own location more profitable."</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** Creative services franchises are rare — most assume design can't be systematized. Tom proved otherwise by separating the system (client onboarding, project management, quality standards) from the individual talent (designers are hired locally). The framework is replicable; the creative talent is sourced locally.</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>

<SbCase label="Case 03 · Wellness · Emerging (Cautionary)" name="Sarah Li — Yoga Studio Delay Decision" tag="Wellness / Fitness" outcome="Delayed franchise launch by 2 years">
  <SbCaseText>Sarah ran 3 profitable "Mindful Flow Yoga" studios in Portland ($1.2M revenue, $240K profit). She received 8 franchise inquiries from other cities. Tempted to capitalize on demand, she did an honest readiness assessment: proven model ✓, documented systems ✓, but no national brand recognition ✗ and zero franchise experience ✗.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The decision:** Delay franchising by 2 years. Year 1–2: Built national brand (launched podcast, online classes, social presence). Year 2: Hired franchise consultant ($50K). Year 3: Developed FDD, training program, operations manual. Year 4: Launched franchise program with national recognition, 12 applicants for first 5 territories.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** "Rushing to franchise would have meant selling a regional brand that nobody outside Portland recognized. The 2-year delay let me build demand before supply. By launch, applicants were coming to me — not the other way around. That leverage difference changed every negotiation."</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>
