<SbSection id="overview">

<SbHeading>Overview</SbHeading>

Platform Cooperative Models are a fundamental reimagining of how creative work is organized and compensated. Instead of selling your labor to platforms that extract **20–40% in fees** while you bear all risk, cooperatives distribute ownership, governance, and profits democratically among the workers who create the value. Each member holds one share regardless of capital contribution. Major decisions — pricing, profit distribution, strategic direction — are made through democratic voting. One member, one vote.

The economics are compelling. A photographer on a traditional stock platform earns **20% royalty** — the platform keeps 80%. The same photographer in a cooperative earns **50–75% royalty** plus annual profit distributions plus equity in a growing business. Triple compensation: wages + profits + ownership. Multiply that across hundreds of members, and you have a business that competes with extractive platforms while paying creators 2.5–3.75x more on the same sales.

The disadvantages are real: cooperatives require active participation (governance meetings, committee work), decision-making is slower than autocratic platforms, capital raising is harder (investors don't get control), and success requires genuine member commitment. Poorly run cooperatives fail when members free-ride, when governance devolves into endless debate, or when the collective lacks business skills to compete. **This structure rewards people who believe in collective ownership enough to invest time building it.**

<SbFlags>
  <SbFlagCol header="Use When">
    <SbFlag type="use">You're frustrated by platform extraction (paying 20–40% to Fiverr, Uber, Getty)</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="use">You believe in collective action and democratic governance</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="use">You have a community of 10+ peers with shared interests</SbFlag>

<SbSection id="protect">

<SbHeading>Key Protections</SbHeading>

Four operating agreement provisions that distinguish a sustainable cooperative from a well-intentioned mess.

<SbCards>
  <SbCard label="Clear Membership Standards">
    <SbCardText>"Members must: (a) contribute minimum 10 hours/month of work, (b) attend 75% of governance meetings, (c) pay annual dues of $100, (d) uphold cooperative principles. Failure results in probation (90 days to remedy) or expulsion (by 2/3 vote)." Standards must be explicit, measurable, and enforced.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Without clear standards, free-riders benefit from the cooperative while contributing nothing. Enforcement mechanisms turn aspirational commitments into real accountability.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Tiered Decision Authority">
    <SbCardText>"Operational decisions (under $5K, routine): delegated to committees or staff. Tactical decisions ($5–25K, moderate): board approval. Strategic decisions (over $25K, major changes): full member vote." Clear delegation prevents paralysis while maintaining democratic control.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Cooperatives that require full consensus on every decision die of paralysis. Tiering lets the co-op move fast on daily operations while preserving democratic governance for decisions that truly matter.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Mandatory Reserve Policy">
    <SbCardText>"Minimum 30% of annual profits retained until operational reserve reaches 6 months expenses and growth fund reaches $[X] target. After reserves met, members vote annually on distribution percentage (50–70% distributed, 30–50% retained)."</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Members voting to distribute 100% of profits is the #1 cause of cooperative stagnation. Mandatory reserves ensure the business can invest, weather downturns, and compete with better-capitalized competitors.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Exit and Dissolution Terms">
    <SbCardText>"Members may exit with 90 days notice. Buy-in refunded within 12 months. Pro-rata share of distributions for work completed. Non-compete: cannot solicit cooperative clients for 12 months. Dissolution requires 80% vote — assets distributed proportional to contributions after debts paid."</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Clear exit terms prevent messy departures that damage the cooperative. The 12-month client non-solicitation protects collective relationships without being punitive.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
</SbCards>

</SbSection>

<SbSection id="manipulations">

<SbHeading>Common Manipulations</SbHeading>

Four structural failures that destroy cooperatives from within. Unlike external manipulations, these come from misaligned internal dynamics.

<SbCards>
  <SbCard label="The Free-Rider Problem" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Members benefit from the cooperative — profit distributions, platform access, governance vote — while contributing minimal work. They attend just enough meetings to avoid expulsion and do minimum hours, but don't pull their weight.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Committed members become resentful, performance suffers, distributions shrink. The cooperative subsidizes non-contributors at the expense of those doing the real work.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>I'd like to propose tiered membership: Active members (15+ hours/month) receive full distributions and vote weight. Participating members (10–15 hours) receive 66% distributions. Below 10 hours for 2 consecutive months triggers probation with 90 days to remedy.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Tyranny of Structurelessness" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Cooperative claims "no hierarchy" or "consensus decision-making." But informal power structures emerge — charismatic or connected members dominate decisions while others are excluded. The "flat" structure becomes cover for invisible hierarchy.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Democratic ideals require explicit democratic structures. Without formal roles, term limits, and documented processes, the loudest voices rule. This is worse than transparent hierarchy because it can't be challenged.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>We need explicit governance: elected board (annual terms), committee chairs (2-year rotating terms), formal decision processes, transparent financials published quarterly, and documented policies accessible to all members. Structure enables democracy — it doesn't undermine it.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Governance Paralysis" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Every decision requires full membership vote and consensus. Operational decisions — marketing budget, client pricing, vendor selection — take months as members debate endlessly. Cooperative can't respond to market changes or competitive pressure.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Consensus is not the same as democracy. Democracy means majority rule with minority rights protected. Consensus means one person can block everything. Cooperatives that require consensus on operational matters lose to faster competitors.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>I'm proposing we adopt tiered decision authority. Operational decisions under $5K: delegated to committees, decided in 2 days. Tactical decisions up to $25K: board approval within 2 weeks. Strategic decisions: full member vote with 30-day notice. This preserves democracy where it matters while letting us compete.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Capital Starvation" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Members vote to distribute 100% of profits immediately rather than retaining earnings. Cooperative can't invest in technology, marketing, or expansion. Year after year, the co-op stagnates while better-capitalized competitors grow.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Short-term extraction by members mirrors the platform extraction cooperatives were built to oppose. Prioritizing immediate cash over investment is the cooperative version of "eating the seed corn."</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>I'm proposing a mandatory reserve policy: 30% of annual profits retained until we have 6 months operating expenses in reserve and $50K in a growth fund. After reserves are met, we vote annually on distribution percentage. This protects the business we all own.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
</SbCards>

<SbSection id="negotiate">

<SbHeading>Negotiation</SbHeading>

Cooperative negotiation is different — you're negotiating terms with peers, not clients. Three approaches based on whether you're joining an existing cooperative, co-founding one, or evaluating between options.

<SbNeg tabsJson='["High Leverage", "Moderate", "Low Leverage"]'>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Propose founding terms that reflect your contribution.** If you're bringing the client base, platform, or brand that makes the cooperative viable, negotiate governance weight or founding member privileges (first-year board seat, lower buy-in amortization).</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**Push for explicit governance structure** from day one — bylaws, decision tiers, reserve policy, exit terms. Resist "we'll figure it out later." Insist on attorney-reviewed operating agreement before anyone contributes work.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**You bring irreplaceable assets** — existing client base, technical platform, established brand, or operational expertise that 10+ other members need.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Evaluate existing cooperative on financial health and governance quality.** Request 3 years of financials, interview current members, attend a meeting as observer. Don't join without data.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**Negotiate probationary membership** — 3-month trial before full buy-in. This protects both sides: you evaluate cultural fit, they evaluate your contribution. Buy-in converts automatically if both parties agree.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**Multiple cooperatives or options exist.** You're choosing where to invest your time and capital. Cooperatives should recruit you as actively as you're evaluating them.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Join the best available cooperative** and prove value through contribution. Volunteer for committees, exceed hours commitments, bring in new clients. Build influence through demonstrated commitment.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**Negotiate earn-in options** — work off the buy-in through reduced fees or extra hours over 6 months. Most cooperatives accommodate members who can't afford upfront buy-in.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**You're new to cooperative models** or lack the financial runway for immediate buy-in. Your commitment and skills are your leverage, not your capital.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
</SbNeg>

<SbSection id="value">

<SbHeading>Example Value Calculation: Yuki's Photography Cooperative Switch</SbHeading>

Yuki is a professional photographer with 8 years of experience and 800 high-quality images. She currently earns $7K/year from a traditional stock platform that pays 20% royalty on $35K in annual sales. She's considering switching to a photographer-owned cooperative.

<SbChart title="3-Year Earnings — Platform vs. Cooperative">
  <SbChartRow label="Cooperative (50–75% royalty + distributions)" value="$60.5K" pct="100" />
  <SbChartRow label="Traditional platform (20% royalty)" value="$23.2K" pct="38" />
</SbChart>

**Without the structure:** Yuki stays on the traditional platform. 20% royalty on gradually growing sales = $23.2K over 3 years. No ownership, no governance voice, no profit share. The platform keeps $116K of the $139.2K her images generate.

**With the structure:** Yuki joins the cooperative for a $250 buy-in. Year 1: $16.2K (50% royalty + $800 distribution). Year 2: $19.8K. Year 3: $24.5K. **Total: $60.5K** — 2.6x the traditional platform. Plus ~$18K in estimated equity value as the cooperative grows, and democratic governance over the platform that affects her livelihood.

**What changed:** Yuki trades platform convenience for collective ownership. The cooperative pays 50–75% royalty vs. 20% — same images, same buyers, 2.5–3.75x the compensation. She invests 8 hours/month in governance (meetings, committee work) and recovers her $250 buy-in through distributions within 4 months.

<SbSection id="cases">

<SbHeading>Example Use Cases</SbHeading>

How three creatives at different career stages engaged with cooperative structures — and what happened.

<SbCase label="Case 01 · Design · Mid-Career" name="Jamie Reyes — Graphic Designer" tag="Design / Creative Services" outcome="$126K Year 2 (+46% vs. freelance)">
  <SbCaseText>Jamie was freelancing at $75K/year with 30% going to platform fees (Fiverr, Upwork) and another $13K to client acquisition, healthcare, and tools. Net: $72K working 45+ hours/week. A 25-member design cooperative in Brooklyn invited her to apply.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The shift:** She joined for a $3K buy-in. Co-op billed clients at $150/hour; she kept 75% ($112.50). Shared resources: group health plan (saved $8K/year), software licenses, office space. Year 1: $98K + $8.5K distribution = $106.5K. Year 2: $115K + $11K distribution = **$126K** — up from $72K net freelancing. Recovered buy-in through distributions in 4 months.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** The cooperative eliminated platform fees and client acquisition costs — the two biggest drains on freelance income. Group health insurance alone was worth $8K/year. The governance time (10 hours/month) was less than she'd spent on marketing and proposals as a solo freelancer.</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>

<SbCase label="Case 02 · Photography · Established" name="Yuki Tanaka — Stock Photographer" tag="Photography / Visual Arts" outcome="$60.5K over 3 years (2.6x platform)">
  <SbCaseText>Yuki earned $7K/year from a traditional stock platform — 20% of $35K in sales. She applied to a curated photographer cooperative and was accepted based on portfolio quality. Buy-in: $250.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The shift:** Cooperative paid **50% standard royalty, 75% extended license** — plus annual profit distributions based on sales contribution. Year 1: $16.2K. Year 2: $19.8K. Year 3: $24.5K. **Total: $60.5K** vs. $23.2K on the traditional platform. She was elected to the member advisory committee in Year 2, helping shape pricing and curation policies.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** Same images, same market, same buyers — 2.6x the income. The curated membership model (application-based) maintained quality that justified premium pricing. Yuki's governance participation wasn't just democratic idealism — her committee role gave her direct influence over the platform economics that determined her income.</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>

<SbCase label="Case 03 · Rideshare · Emerging" name="Andre Coleman — Rideshare Driver" tag="Gig Economy / Transport" outcome="$57.2K Year 3 (+39% vs. platform)">
  <SbCaseText>Andre drove full-time for a major rideshare platform: 50 hours/week, $75K gross fares, $18.75K platform fee (25%), $15K expenses = $41.25K net ($16.50/hour). No benefits, no voice in pricing, constant fear of algorithmic deactivation. A driver-owned cooperative launched in his city with 200 founding members.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The shift:** He negotiated an "earn-in" option — $1K buy-in worked off through reduced fees over 6 months. Year 1 was lean (co-op building rider base): $36K from the cooperative + $12K moonlighting on the old platform = $48K. Year 2: $44K as the cooperative grew. Year 3: **$56K + $1.2K distribution = $57.2K** — 39% more than his old platform net. Plus ownership equity and a vote on every policy affecting his work.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** The cooperative charged 10% vs. 25% — proving that 15% of every fare was pure platform extraction, not operational necessity. Year 1 required patience (and moonlighting), but by Year 3, Andre earned more, owned equity in the business, and helped set the policies governing his work. The earn-in option made participation accessible despite tight cash flow.</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>
