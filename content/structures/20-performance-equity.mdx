<SbSection id="overview">

<SbHeading>Overview</SbHeading>

Performance Equity supplements time-based vesting (#19) with milestone-triggered grants that reward measurable impact. Instead of waiting 4 years for equity to vest purely based on time served, you earn **additional equity tranches** when you hit specific targets: revenue milestones, product launches, fundraising success, user growth thresholds, or other quantifiable outcomes. Base equity vests on schedule; performance equity accelerates your ownership when you outperform.

The structure works in two forms. **Escalator model:** base equity grant (e.g., 1%) plus performance tranches (e.g., +0.5% at $1M revenue, +0.5% at $3M, +0.5% at $10M) for a potential total of 2.5%. **Bonus pool model:** company reserves 2–5% equity pool, distributed annually to contributors who hit targets. Both tie ownership to outcomes rather than tenure — rewarding impact, not just presence.

Performance equity solves the fundamental tension in equity-for-services deals: time-based vesting rewards showing up, not performing. A designer who creates the brand identity that drives a company's entire fundraising narrative deserves more than a designer who completes maintenance projects for the same duration. Performance equity creates that differentiation while giving companies confidence that equity goes to contributors who move the needle.

<SbSection id="when-to-use">

<SbHeading>When to Use This Structure</SbHeading>

<SbFlags>
  <SbFlagCol header="Use When">
    <SbFlag type="use">Your creative contribution has measurable business impact</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="use">You want more equity but company resists larger base grants</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="use">Milestones are specific, measurable, and within your influence</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="use">Company is pre-product-market-fit (lots of value to create)</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="use">You're confident in your ability to deliver on targets</SbFlag>
  </SbFlagCol>
  <SbFlagCol header="Avoid When">
    <SbFlag type="avoid">Milestones are vague ("improve brand perception") or uncontrollable</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="avoid">Company changes goalposts — moves targets after you've invested effort</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="avoid">Revenue milestones depend on sales team performance, not your work</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="avoid">Base equity is so small that performance upside isn't meaningful</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="avoid">You'd rather have guaranteed time-based vesting (#19) than outcome risk</SbFlag>
  </SbFlagCol>
</SbFlags>

<SbSection id="protect">

<SbHeading>Key Protections</SbHeading>

Five provisions that prevent performance equity from becoming a carrot that's always dangled but never delivered.

<SbCards>
  <SbCard label="Written Milestone Definitions Before Work Begins">
    <SbCardText>"Performance milestones must be documented in the equity agreement with: (a) specific measurable criteria, (b) measurement methodology, (c) measurement date, (d) who certifies achievement, (e) equity grant amount and vesting terms upon achievement." No verbal milestones. No "we'll define them later."</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Undefined milestones are moved milestones. If "hit revenue target" isn't specified as "$1M ARR measured by verified Stripe data as of December 31," the company can argue the target wasn't met regardless of performance. Specificity is your only protection.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Anti-Goalpost-Moving Clause">
    <SbCardText>"Once documented, performance milestones may not be modified, delayed, or redefined without Creative Partner's written consent. If Company changes business strategy in ways that make original milestones unmeasurable, parties negotiate replacement milestones of equivalent difficulty within 30 days."</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** The most common performance equity manipulation: company raises the bar after you've invested months of effort. "We said $1M but now we think $2M is the real target." Without anti-modification protection, milestones become aspirational rather than contractual.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Partial Credit Provisions">
    <SbCardText>"If Creative Partner achieves 75–99% of a performance milestone, 50% of the associated equity grant vests. If achievement reaches 50–74%, 25% vests." Example: target is $1M ARR. You reach $850K (85%). With partial credit: 50% of the milestone equity vests. Without: you get nothing for 85% achievement.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Binary milestones (all-or-nothing) create perverse incentives. A target of $1M that pays nothing at $999K discourages ambitious targets and rewards sandbagging. Partial credit recognizes near-misses while maintaining performance alignment.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Independent Verification">
    <SbCardText>"Milestone achievement is verified by: (a) audited financial statements for revenue milestones, (b) third-party analytics for user growth milestones, (c) executed legal documents for strategic milestones. Company may not self-certify milestones as unachieved."</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** If the company alone determines whether you've hit a milestone, they have an incentive to say you haven't — keeping the equity. Independent verification removes this conflict. For revenue milestones, use Stripe, QuickBooks, or audited statements — not the CEO's spreadsheet.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Acceleration on Change of Control">
    <SbCardText>"If Company is acquired before all performance milestones are measured, all remaining performance equity grants vest at 100% upon closing." Rationale: acquisition validates value creation — which is exactly what performance equity rewards. You shouldn't lose performance equity because the company was acquired before your measurement date.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Acquisition before your milestone date means your equity is at risk even though the acquisition itself proves value was created. This clause ensures performance equity converts to guaranteed equity at the moment it matters most.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
</SbCards>

<SbSection id="manipulations">

<SbHeading>Common Manipulations</SbHeading>

Four tactics that use performance equity mechanics to extract work without delivering ownership.

<SbCards>
  <SbCard label="The Moving Goalposts" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Original milestone: "$1M ARR by December." You're on track in October. Board meeting in November: "We've raised our internal targets — the milestone is now $1.5M." Or: "ARR should be measured as net revenue, not gross." The target changes after you've invested months of effort.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** This is the #1 performance equity manipulation. Companies rationalize it as "business evolves" but the pattern is consistent: targets change when you're close to hitting them. Anti-modification clauses are non-negotiable.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>Our agreement specifies $1M ARR measured by gross revenue in Stripe as of December 31. That milestone was documented and signed. Changing it now would violate our agreement — and would eliminate my confidence in any future milestones we set. Let's honor this agreement and set new targets for the next tranche.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="The Impossible Milestone" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Company sets milestones that sound reasonable but are statistically impossible given resources. "Hit $5M revenue in Year 1" when comparable companies average $800K. The performance equity exists on paper to recruit you, but was never intended to be achievable.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Research comparable company benchmarks before accepting milestones. If the target exceeds 90th percentile performance for similar companies at similar stages, it's designed to fail. Negotiate for achievable-but-ambitious targets at 60th–75th percentile.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>I've researched comparable companies at this stage — median Year 1 revenue is $800K, 75th percentile is $1.5M. A $5M target exceeds even the top performers. I'd propose a tiered approach: $1M triggers 50% of the performance equity, $2M triggers 100%, and $5M triggers a bonus tranche. This aligns us around ambitious-but-achievable targets.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="The Attribution Dispute" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Milestone hit: $2M revenue achieved. Company argues: "Revenue came from the sales team, not the brand work." They refuse to certify your milestone despite clear correlation between your brand launch and revenue acceleration. Attribution is contested to avoid paying equity.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Creative work rarely has direct, isolated attribution — it works through systems. Define milestones as company-level outcomes, not individually attributed results. "Company achieves $2M ARR" not "brand work generates $2M." If the company succeeds, your performance equity should vest.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>Our agreement specifies company-level milestones, not individually attributed outcomes — because we both know creative contribution works through systems, not in isolation. The company hit $2M ARR. That's the milestone. My equity vests per our agreement. If you want individually attributed milestones next time, we can discuss — but we can't retroactively change the measurement.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="The Diminishing Pool" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Company creates a 3% "performance pool" shared across 10 contributors. Sounds generous. Reality: after dilution and allocation, your share of the pool is 0.15% — less than a standard time-based grant with no performance requirement. The pool creates competition among team members while reducing individual grants.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Shared pools dilute individual incentives. If your 0.15% from the pool is less than you'd get from a standard 0.5% time-based grant, the "performance" framing is a discount, not a bonus. Negotiate individual performance grants separate from any shared pool.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>I'd prefer individual performance milestones rather than a shared pool. My contribution is measurable — let's tie my additional equity to outcomes I can directly influence. A 0.5% individual performance grant with clear milestones motivates me more than competing for a share of a 3% pool across 10 people.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
</SbCards>

<SbSection id="negotiate">

<SbHeading>Negotiation</SbHeading>

Performance equity negotiation has two dimensions: the milestones themselves (what you're measured on) and the equity amounts (what you earn). Three approaches based on leverage.

<SbNeg tabsJson='["High Leverage", "Moderate", "Low Leverage"]'>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Set aggressive milestones with aggressive equity.** "I'm proposing 2% base + 2% performance across 4 milestones. The milestones are ambitious — $1M, $3M, $5M, $10M ARR — and the equity reflects the difficulty. If I hit all four, 4% is justified by the value created."</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**Require partial credit, anti-modification, and acceleration on acquisition.** Push for independent verification of all financial milestones. Escalating equity per milestone: 0.25% for the first, 0.5% for the second, 0.75% for the third, 0.5% for the fourth.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**Proven track record of hitting targets.** Previous performance equity deals with documented outcomes. Company specifically needs your expertise for next growth phase. Multiple competing offers.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Propose 2–3 milestones at achievable-but-ambitious targets.** "1% base + 1% performance across 3 milestones. Targets at 65th percentile for comparable companies — ambitious but achievable with strong execution."</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**Include partial credit (75%+ = 50% vest) and written milestone definitions.** Push for company-level milestones (not individually attributed) and quarterly check-ins to track progress toward targets.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**Valuable contributor, growing track record.** Company wants your equity aligned with outcomes. Both parties believe milestones are achievable. Good-faith negotiation on terms.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Accept 1–2 simple, controllable milestones.** Focus on deliverable milestones you directly control: "Brand identity delivered by [date]" or "Website launched with 3%+ conversion." Avoid revenue targets you can't influence alone.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**Ensure milestones are written and signed before work begins.** Even at low leverage, documented milestones are non-negotiable. Push for at least one deliverable milestone (within your control) rather than only outcome milestones.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**First performance equity deal or limited negotiating position.** Better to hit easy milestones and build track record than negotiate aggressive targets you might miss.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
</SbNeg>

<SbSection id="value">

<SbHeading>Example Value Calculation: Priya's Escalating Performance Grants</SbHeading>

Priya is a product designer joining a seed-stage health tech company. Normal fee: $180K/year. She negotiates a hybrid deal: reduced salary + base equity + performance milestones.

<SbChart title="3-Year Equity Value — With vs. Without Performance Milestones">
  <SbChartRow label="Base + performance (3.25% at $80M)" value="$2.6M" pct="100" />
  <SbChartRow label="Base equity only (1.5% at $80M)" value="$1.2M" pct="46" />
  <SbChartRow label="Full salary (no equity)" value="$540K" pct="21" />
</SbChart>

**The deal:** $110K salary (61% of normal) + 1.5% base equity (4-year vest) + performance milestones:

- Milestone 1: Product launch within 6 months → +0.5%
- Milestone 2: $1M ARR within 18 months → +0.5%
- Milestone 3: Series A close within 24 months → +0.75%

**Month 6:** Product launches on time. +0.5% vests. Total equity: 2% (1.5% base vesting + 0.5% performance).

**Month 16:** Company hits $1.2M ARR (exceeded target). +0.5% vests. Total: 2.5%.

**Month 22:** Series A closes at $40M valuation. +0.75% vests. Total: **3.25%** (1.5% base + 1.75% performance).

**Month 36:** Company grows to $80M valuation. Priya's 3.25%: **$2.6M** — vs. $1.2M if she'd accepted base equity only, vs. $540K if she'd stayed full salary. The performance milestones added $1.4M in equity value because she chose targets she could directly influence.

**What changed:** Performance milestones let Priya earn 2.17x the base equity offer by proving impact through measurable outcomes. The company paid more equity — but only because Priya created measurably more value. Both sides won.

<SbSection id="cases">

<SbHeading>Example Use Cases</SbHeading>

How three creatives used performance equity — and what the milestone design meant for outcomes.

<SbCase label="Case 01 · Design · Mid-Career" name="Priya Mehta — Product Designer with Escalating Milestones" tag="Health Tech / SaaS" outcome="$2.6M equity on $210K foregone salary">
  <SbCaseText>Priya joined a seed-stage health tech company at $110K salary (vs. $180K market). Base equity: 1.5% with standard 4-year vest. She proposed 3 performance milestones: product launch (6 months), $1M ARR (18 months), Series A close (24 months). Each milestone: +0.5–0.75% additional equity.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The shift:** Hit all 3 milestones, earning 1.75% performance equity on top of 1.5% base = **3.25% total.** At $80M valuation (Month 36): **$2.6M** in equity value. Foregone salary over 3 years: $210K. ROI on foregone cash: 12.4x. The performance milestones were worth $1.4M more than base equity alone.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** "I chose milestones I could directly influence — product launch was in my hands, ARR was tied to product quality, and Series A depended on the product narrative I built. If they'd proposed 'increase social media engagement by 200%' — something I couldn't control — I would have declined. Controllability is everything."</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>

<SbCase label="Case 02 · Content · Established" name="Jordan Blake — Creative Director with Revenue Milestones" tag="DTC / Consumer" outcome="$1.8M from 4 milestones over 30 months">
  <SbCaseText>Jordan, a creative director with 12 years of experience, joined a DTC brand as fractional CCO. Base: $15K/month + 1% equity. Performance milestones: +0.25% at $2M revenue, +0.5% at $5M, +0.5% at $10M, +0.75% at $20M. Partial credit included: 75%+ achievement = 50% of milestone equity.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The shift:** Hit first 3 milestones cleanly ($2M at Month 8, $5M at Month 14, $10M at Month 22). Fourth milestone ($20M): reached $17.5M at Month 30 — 87.5% achievement. Partial credit triggered: 50% of 0.75% = 0.375% vested. **Total equity: 2.125%** (1% base + 1.125% performance). Company acquired for $85M. Jordan's 2.125% = **$1.8M** plus $450K cumulative salary.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** Partial credit on the fourth milestone was worth $318K. Without it, Jordan would have earned $1.49M — a $318K penalty for hitting 87.5% of target. "Partial credit is the provision I negotiate hardest for now. Binary milestones punish near-misses and reward sandbagging."</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>

<SbCase label="Case 03 · Branding · Emerging (Cautionary)" name="Kai Santos — Designer with Uncontrollable Milestones" tag="Marketplace / Tech" outcome="0 of 3 milestones hit despite excellent work">
  <SbCaseText>Kai, a talented brand designer, took an equity-for-services deal with a marketplace startup. Base: 0.75% equity + $45K cash (vs. $90K normal fee). Performance milestones: +0.25% at 10K users, +0.25% at $500K revenue, +0.5% at Series A close. All three depended on factors beyond Kai's control — user acquisition, sales, and fundraising.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The result:** Kai delivered exceptional brand work — praised by investors, users, and the founding team. But: user acquisition stalled due to engineering bugs (not design). Revenue missed because pricing was wrong (not brand). Series A delayed because the market cooled. **0 of 3 milestones hit.** Kai earned 0.75% base equity and $45K cash — effectively $45K for $90K worth of work, with no performance equity despite excellent creative contribution.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** "Every milestone depended on someone else's execution, not mine. I should have negotiated deliverable milestones: brand identity complete, website launched, pitch deck delivered. Things I controlled. Instead I got company-level outcomes that had nothing to do with my design quality. Never accept milestones you can't influence."</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>
