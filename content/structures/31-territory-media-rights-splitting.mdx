<SbSection id="overview">

<SbHeading>Overview</SbHeading>

Territory/Media Rights Splitting divides your IP into separate geographic territories and formats, licensing each independently to specialized buyers at market-specific rates. Instead of selling "worldwide rights in all formats" for a single payment, you license North American theatrical to Studio A, European streaming to Platform B, Asian broadcast to Network C — each negotiation optimized for that segment. The aggregate value of split rights exceeds a bundled deal by **2–5x.**

The principle is simple: different territories and media have different values, different buyers, and different market dynamics. North American film rights might command $500K from Studio A, but bundling European rights into the same deal means Studio A pays less than their actual market value — they discount what they don't specialize in. A European distributor would pay $300K for those same European rights. By splitting: $500K + $300K + $200K Asia = **$1M total** vs. $600K "worldwide."

This structure works across every creative discipline. Authors license North American English separately from UK, German, French, and 30+ other territories — each generating a separate advance. Film producers split theatrical, streaming, broadcast, and international. Musicians split masters by territory and medium. The pattern is universal: granular division enables market-specific optimization.

<SbHeading>When to Use This Structure</SbHeading>

<SbFlags>
  <SbFlagCol header="Use When">
    <SbFlag type="use">Work has multi-territory appeal (universal themes, proven genre)</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="use">You have specialized representation (foreign rights agent, sales agent)</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="use">Primary territory deal creates validation for foreign buyers</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="use">Can afford 12–24 month timeline for all territories to close</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="use">Different territories have distinct buyers and market dynamics</SbFlag>
  </SbFlagCol>
  <SbFlagCol header="Avoid When">
    <SbFlag type="avoid">Work is region-specific (local politics, regional humor, niche subject)</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="avoid">No specialized representation available or affordable</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="avoid">Need maximum cash immediately (can't wait 12–24 months)</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="avoid">Buyer offers world rights at genuine premium (within 80% of split value)</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="avoid">Administrative complexity exceeds capacity (10–50 licenses to manage)</SbFlag>
  </SbFlagCol>
</SbFlags>

<SbSection id="protect">

<SbHeading>Key Protections</SbHeading>

Five provisions that make territory/media splits enforceable and prevent boundary violations.

<SbCards>
  <SbCard label="Explicit Territory Boundaries (Country Lists, Not Regions)">
    <SbCardText>"NORTH AMERICA defined as: United States of America (including territories), Canada, and Mexico. Does NOT include Central America, South America, Caribbean, or other territories. EUROPE defined as: All EU member states, UK, Switzerland, Norway, Iceland. Does NOT include Turkey or Middle East." Never use vague regional names — list countries explicitly.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** "North American rights" is ambiguous — does it include Mexico? Caribbean? US military bases worldwide? Vague boundaries create disputes. Country lists leave no room for interpretation.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Digital Geo-Blocking Requirements">
    <SbCardText>"Digital distribution limited to customers with billing addresses in licensed territory ONLY. Licensee MUST implement geo-blocking preventing sales or streaming outside territory. Failure to geo-block constitutes material breach." Netflix, Spotify, Amazon, and Apple all geo-block by territory — it's standard and feasible.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Without geo-blocking, your North American digital licensee's ebook is available globally — destroying the value of your European and Asian digital licenses. Digital doesn't override geographic boundaries when geo-blocking is contractually required.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Format Definitions with Future Formats Retention">
    <SbCardText>"PRINT RIGHTS: Hardcover, paperback, mass market, book club, large print. Does NOT include ebooks, audiobooks, or digital subscription. FUTURE FORMATS: Any format not existing as of Execution Date retained by Author unless explicitly granted." Always enumerate what IS granted — everything unlisted is retained.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** E-books didn't exist when many book contracts were written — authors lost billions. Streaming wasn't in original film deals. Explicit format definitions plus future formats retention prevents history from repeating.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Territory-Specific Reversion">
    <SbCardText>"Reversion applies independently by territory. If German Publisher fails to keep Work in print or achieve 5,000 copies over 3 years, German rights revert without affecting North American, UK, or other licenses. Author may re-license reverted territory independently."</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Without territory-specific reversion, one underperforming territory can't be reclaimed without affecting all others. Each territory should revert independently based on its own performance, leaving successful territories unaffected.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Separate Accounting by Territory and Format">
    <SbCardText>"Each territorial and format license shall have independent accounting. Revenue from one territory shall not be cross-collateralized with, or applied to recoupment obligations of, any other territory or format. Each exploitation reports independently."</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Cross-collateralization across territories means your successful German edition recoups your underperforming French advance. Each territory's economics should stand alone — your German success is your German success.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
</SbCards>

<SbSection id="manipulations">

<SbHeading>Common Manipulations</SbHeading>

Four tactics that undervalue split rights and pressure creators into bundled world rights deals.

<SbCards>
  <SbCard label="The 'World Rights' Minimal Premium Grab" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Buyer offers $120K for "worldwide English + translations" vs. $100K for North America only. The $20K "premium" values $200K+ in foreign rights at $20K — a 90% discount. Buyer captures massive value for minimal additional payment.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Foreign rights for comparable works sell $200–300K+ when licensed separately. A $20K premium for $200K+ in value is not a premium — it's a 90% discount.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>Your $20K premium values $200K–300K in foreign rights at 93% discount. UK alone is worth $50–75K, translations $150K+. I'll license North America for $100K and handle foreign separately. If you want world rights, price must reflect actual foreign market value: $350K total minimum.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Digital Rights Override Geographic Boundaries" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Buyer claims "digital distribution is inherently global — we can't geo-block." Your North American digital licensee sells globally, destroying the value of European and Asian digital licenses. Buyer's argument: "Technology makes territorial splits impossible."</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Every major platform geo-blocks by territory — Netflix, Spotify, Amazon, Apple. Geo-blocking is standard, feasible, and expected. Buyers who claim otherwise are avoiding contractual obligations, not technical limitations.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>Netflix, Spotify, Amazon, and Apple all geo-block by territory. This is standard practice and entirely feasible. Your digital distribution must be limited to billing addresses in the licensed territory with geo-blocking implemented. Failure to geo-block is a material breach.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Holding Foreign Rights Hostage" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** "We're offering $150K for North American rights. But this is contingent on also acquiring UK and translation rights. If you split, our NA-only offer drops to $80K." You choose: $150K world (lose $200K+ foreign value) or $80K NA (artificial lowball).</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** The $80K lowball is artificial — designed to punish splitting. If the work is worth $150K bundled and $80K NA-only, the buyer is admitting foreign is worth $70K to them while it's worth $200K+ on the open market. Call the bluff.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>If your genuine NA valuation is $80K, make that offer without conditions. But $80K feels artificially lowered to pressure bundling. Comparable NA-only deals are $100–120K. I'm licensing NA only. If $80K is real, I'll evaluate. If it's a pressure tactic, I'll find a buyer who values the work at market rate.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Vague Territory Definitions Creating Overlap" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Contract says "North American rights" without country lists. Buyer claims this includes English sales to Latin America, US military bases worldwide, and digital sales to Canadian expats in Europe. Your UK licensee disputes overlap. Litigation ensues.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Vague territory definitions are disputes waiting to happen. Every territory must be defined by explicit country list with digital geo-blocking requirements. Ambiguity always benefits the party with more legal resources — usually the buyer.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>I need explicit country lists for each territory. "North America" must be defined as: United States (all states, DC, territories), Canada, Mexico. Digital limited to billing addresses in these countries. No implied coverage of bases abroad, expat populations, or adjacent territories.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
</SbCards>

<SbSection id="negotiate">

<SbHeading>Negotiation</SbHeading>

Rights splitting negotiation centers on which territories to separate, how to bundle smaller markets, and managing buyer resistance.

<SbNeg tabsJson='["High Leverage", "Moderate", "Low Leverage"]'>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Aggressive splitting — license primary only, retain everything.** "I'm licensing North American English hardcover/paperback for 7 years. I retain UK, all translations, audio, film, digital, and all other territories and formats. If you want additional territories, price them separately."</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**Auction foreign rights at book fairs/film markets** for competitive dynamics. License Tier 1 individually, bundle Tier 2/3. Pre-sales to de-risk primary deal. No ROFR on foreign territories.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**Proven multi-territory appeal.** Prior works sold in 15+ territories. Specialized representation with global sub-agents. Multiple primary territory buyers competing. Can afford 12–24 month licensing timeline.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Selective splitting — retain highest-value territories.** "I'll license North America + UK together for $160K. I retain translations, audio, film. Or: NA only for $100K and I handle UK separately." Trade convenience on lower-value territories for control of highest-value.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**Use primary deal validation to pitch foreign.** Close NA first, announce deal, then foreign agent uses momentum to place 5–10 major territories over 6–12 months. Bundle smaller territories for efficiency.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**Growing track record, moderate foreign demand.** First or second work with international potential. Representation available but limited. Primary territory success expected to drive foreign interest.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Accept world rights but carve out film/TV and sequels.** "I'll grant world publishing rights for increased advance. But I retain film/TV adaptation, sequel rights, and merchandise. These have independent value that your advance doesn't reflect."</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**If forced into world rights, add reversion by territory** (#29). Territories where licensee fails to publish within 18 months revert independently. This preserves future splitting potential.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**First deal, limited foreign demand, need maximum upfront.** World rights may be necessary to get the deal done. Retain film/TV and sequels (highest potential value), add territorial reversion as safety net.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
</SbNeg>

<SbSection id="value">

<SbHeading>Example Value Calculation: Mira's Debut Thriller — Split vs. Bundled</SbHeading>

Mira, 34, has a completed thriller generating publisher interest. She compares world rights vs. strategic territory splitting over 5 years.

<SbChart title="5-Year Outcome — Territory Split vs. World Rights">
  <SbChartRow label="Split (NA + 15 territories + film)" value="$494K" pct="100" />
  <SbChartRow label="World rights (bundled)" value="$225K" pct="46" />
</SbChart>

**World rights path:** $150K advance + $50–100K royalties over 5 years. Publisher handles foreign sublicensing, keeps 75% of foreign revenue. **Total: ~$225K.**

**Split path (after 20% agent commissions on foreign):**

- North America: $100K advance + $80K royalties = **$180K**
- UK/Commonwealth: $60K → net $48K
- Audio: $35K → net $28K
- Germany: $40K → net $32K
- France: $30K → net $24K
- Spain + Italy: $38K → net $30.4K
- Japan + Korea + Chinese: $60K → net $48K
- Nordic + Eastern European + Latin America + ROW: $55K → net $44K
- Film option (Year 3): **$50K** (retained, no commission)
- **5-year total: ~$494K** (after agent commissions)

**The gap:** $494K split vs. $225K bundled = **2.2x more.** Mira sacrificed $50K upfront ($150K → $100K) to capture $269K additional from foreign territories and film. Her $50K "investment" returned **5.4x.**

**Timeline trade-off:** World rights: all money in 12 months. Split: 18–24 months for all territories to close. The $269K premium required patience.

<SbHeading>Example Use Cases</SbHeading>

How three creatives used territory/media splitting to maximize IP value — and one who learned from not splitting.

<SbCase label="Case 01 · Publishing · Emerging" name="Mira Johansson — Debut Thriller Split Across 20 Territories" tag="Publishing / Thriller" outcome="$494K (2.2x world rights alternative)">
  <SbCaseText>Mira had 3 publisher offers. Two offered world rights ($140K and $150K). One offered $100K for North America only. Her agent recommended splitting. They licensed NA at $100K with improved royalty (12% vs. 10%), then spent 18 months placing foreign rights at Frankfurt and London Book Fairs.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The shift:** UK ($60K), Germany ($40K), France ($30K), Japan ($25K), and 11 more territories closed over 18 months. Audio licensed separately ($35K). Film option sold Year 3 ($50K). **5-year total: $494K** vs. $225K maximum under world rights. Foreign agent commissions: $70K (20%). Net gain from splitting: $199K after all costs.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** "The world rights offers were tempting — $150K upfront vs. $100K. But my agent modeled the foreign market: comparable thrillers sold $200K+ across territories. The $50K I 'lost' upfront generated $294K in foreign licensing. Every territory that closed validated the next one — German publisher saw UK success, Japanese publisher saw German success. Sequential licensing creates momentum."</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>

<SbCase label="Case 02 · Film · Established" name="Tomás Reyes — Indie Film Territory-by-Territory" tag="Film / Thriller" outcome="$6.85M (2.3x single worldwide deal)">
  <SbCaseText>Tomás directed a $5M thriller that premiered at a major festival to strong reviews. A worldwide distributor offered $3M all-rights. His sales agent recommended territory splitting with sequential windowing.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The shift:** North American theatrical ($1.5M, specialty distributor). International theatrical across 15+ territories ($1.1M net). North American streaming ($2M, 18 months post-theatrical). International streaming ($1.2M). Broadcast + home video ($1.05M combined). **Total: $6.85M** vs. $3M worldwide offer. Sales agent commission (20%): $1.37M. **Net: $5.48M** — still 1.83x the bundled deal.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** "The worldwide distributor valued my film at $3M. Territory-specific distributors valued it at $6.85M. Why? Specialized distributors know their markets — the North American arthouse distributor positioned it as a thriller. The French distributor positioned it as auteur cinema. The Japanese distributor used anime-style marketing. Each territory's marketing was optimized. Generic global marketing would have underperformed by 40–60%."</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>

<SbCase label="Case 03 · Music · Mid-Career (Cautionary)" name="Anika Osei — The World Rights Lesson" tag="Music / R&B" outcome="$180K received ($420K+ left on table)">
  <SbCaseText>Anika, an R&B artist with 2M monthly Spotify listeners, signed a worldwide distribution deal with an indie label: $180K advance, worldwide masters for 15 years, 20% net royalty. The label handled all territories through one global distributor. Comparable artist with territory-split deals: $600K+ aggregate over same period.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The result:** Label's global distributor underperformed in key territories — no dedicated marketing in Germany (strong R&B market), Japan (huge demand for Black American artists), or Brazil (growing streaming market). Anika earned $180K advance + $95K royalties over 5 years = **$275K.** Territory-split estimate based on comparable artists: $600K+ (dedicated marketing per territory). Gap: **$325K+ left on table** from generic global distribution.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** "My label had no presence in Germany, Japan, or Brazil — three of my biggest streaming markets. They used one global distributor who treated every territory the same. A German distributor would have gotten me on German playlists. A Japanese distributor would have booked Tokyo showcases. Worldwide deals with labels that lack territory-specific expertise leave massive money on the table."</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>
