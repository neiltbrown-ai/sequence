<SbSection id="overview">

<SbHeading>Overview</SbHeading>

Gross Participation gives you a percentage of a project's revenue **before most costs are deducted** — ensuring you profit even when the project "never makes money" on paper through creative accounting. Unlike net profit participation (#23) — which is notoriously manipulated to show zero profit even on billion-dollar hits — gross participation pays from the top line after only minimal, clearly defined deductions (typically distribution fees and marketing, both capped).

The structure's power lies in mathematical transparency. Box office revenue is public and verifiable. Distribution fees are standardized (30–42%). Your percentage is explicit. There's no room for a studio to claim a $1.5B film "lost money" — because your cut comes before they layer on overhead, interest, cross-collateralization, and affiliated company markups that evaporate net profits.

Three tiers exist: **First-Dollar Gross** (percentage of ALL revenue from dollar one — virtually extinct, reserved for 1–2 people in the world), **Adjusted Gross** (after distribution fees and capped P&A — what most A-list talent negotiates), and **Rolling Break/Escalating Gross** (percentage increases at revenue milestones — accessible to strong negotiators without A-list leverage). Gross participation is extraordinarily rare — reserved for talent whose involvement is essential to commercial success. But for those who can command it, no other compensation structure creates better risk-adjusted returns.

<SbSection id="protect">

<SbHeading>Key Protections</SbHeading>

Five provisions that ensure "gross participation" actually means gross — not net participation wearing a gross mask.

<SbCards>
  <SbCard label="Explicit Gross Receipts Definition">
    <SbCardText>"Gross receipts shall include all worldwide theatrical box office, home video, streaming licensing, television sales, and ancillary revenue received by Studio or any affiliated entity. Revenue received by Studio-affiliated companies shall be deemed Studio revenue for participation purposes."</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Without explicit inclusion of all revenue streams, studios can route revenue through affiliated companies or claim streaming licensing "isn't box office." If your deal says "box office gross" and the studio earns $200M from streaming — you see none of it. Define gross as ALL revenue, not just theatrical.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Distribution Fee Cap">
    <SbCardText>"Distribution fees shall not exceed 33% of domestic gross and 42% of international gross." These caps are market standard. Lower is better but hard to achieve. Without caps, studios charge 50–60% "distribution fees" — effectively converting gross participation into net.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Distribution fees are the largest deduction before your percentage. Uncapped fees let studios inflate this line item to absorb your backend. A 33/42% cap is the standard protection. Film generates $1B worldwide → your gross is calculated on ~$600M, not $400M.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="No Overhead, Interest, or Cross-Collateralization">
    <SbCardText>"No indirect costs, overhead, studio fees, interest charges, or production costs beyond P&A shall be deducted before Artist participation. Artist participation shall be calculated solely on this project's revenues. No cross-collateralization with other projects permitted."</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** This is where net participation gets destroyed. Overhead (15–25% of budget), interest (prime + 3% on all costs), and cross-collateralization (deducting losses from other films) are the accounting tools that make $1B films show zero profit. Excluding them is what makes gross participation gross.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="P&A Cost Cap with Documentation">
    <SbCardText>"Actual, verified prints and advertising costs capped at $[40–60M] for initial release. All P&A costs must be third-party invoiced expenses. No overhead or studio markup permitted on P&A."</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Studios routinely inflate P&A by routing costs through affiliated marketing agencies at marked-up rates. A $30M actual P&A campaign becomes $60M on paper. Your cap + documentation requirement prevents this inflation.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Audit Rights + Quarterly Reporting">
    <SbCardText>"Studio shall provide quarterly statements showing: box office by territory, home video revenue, streaming licensing fees, P&A expenses, and distribution fees. Artist has right to audit annually. If underpayment exceeds 5%, Studio pays audit costs plus 10% annual interest."</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** You can't enforce what you can't see. Quarterly reporting creates transparency. Audit rights with fee recovery create deterrent. The combination ensures studios report honestly — because the cost of getting caught exceeds the benefit of manipulation.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
</SbCards>

</SbSection>

<SbSection id="manipulations">

<SbHeading>Common Manipulations</SbHeading>

Four tactics that convert gross participation into something closer to net — each preventable with specific contract language.

<SbCards>
  <SbCard label="The 'Adjusted Gross = Net' Trick" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Studio offers "adjusted gross" but defines it so heavily that it's actually net: "Adjusted gross means gross receipts less distribution fees, P&A, production costs, overhead at 15%, interest at prime + 3%, contingent compensation to other talent, and..." By the time they're done "adjusting," adjusted gross equals $0 — just like net.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Legitimate adjusted gross deducts only: distribution fees (capped) and P&A (capped). If the definition includes overhead, interest, production costs, or contingent compensation, it's net participation disguised as gross.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>Your adjusted gross definition includes costs that should not be deducted — overhead, interest, and production costs are net participation deductions. Standard adjusted gross is receipts less distribution fees capped at 33/42% and P&A capped at $50M. Period. No overhead, interest, or other costs.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="The International Exclusion" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Studio offers gross participation on "domestic box office only." Modern films generate 60–70% of revenue internationally. Excluding international cuts your participation by two-thirds. A $1B worldwide film ($300M domestic, $700M international) pays you $15M on domestic-only vs. $50M on worldwide.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** There is no legitimate reason to exclude international revenue from gross participation. Studios try it because international is where the biggest money flows — and excluding it saves them tens of millions.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>My participation must include worldwide gross. Domestic-only is unacceptable — it excludes 60–70% of revenue. If I'm essential enough to justify backend, I'm essential enough to participate worldwide.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="The Streaming Revenue Shell Game" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** For streaming-first or hybrid releases, studio claims your percentage only applies to "theatrical box office" — when the film generated $200M in streaming licensing that isn't "box office." Your gross participation applies to $50M theatrical instead of $250M total revenue.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** As distribution shifts to streaming, "box office" definitions must evolve. Your contract should define gross as ALL exploitation revenue — theatrical, streaming, TVOD, SVOD, licensing, TV sales, and ancillary.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>Gross receipts must include all revenue from exploitation of this project — theatrical, streaming subscription allocations, TVOD/SVOD licensing, television sales, and ancillary revenue. Participation cannot be limited to traditional box office when the studio is monetizing across every platform.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="The Breakeven Trigger Disguise" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** "You get 10% of adjusted gross, but only after the film breaks even." Sounds reasonable. Reality: "breakeven" includes overhead, interest, and inflated costs — the film never "breaks even" by the studio's definition. Your "gross participation" pays $0 on a commercially successful film.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Breakeven triggers convert gross participation into net participation. Your backend should begin from first dollar (or after only capped distribution fees and P&A) — never after "breakeven" defined by the studio.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>Backend participation must begin from first dollar or after only capped deductions — distribution fees at 33/42% and P&A at $50M max. "Breakeven" language is unacceptable. If you need to recoup costs before my participation begins, this is net participation, not gross.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
</SbCards>

<SbSection id="negotiate">

<SbHeading>Negotiation</SbHeading>

Gross participation requires exceptional leverage. Three approaches based on commercial track record and project dynamics.

<SbNeg tabsJson='["High Leverage", "Moderate", "Low Leverage"]'>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Trade salary for maximum backend.** "I'll reduce my quote from $20M to $10M in exchange for 12% adjusted gross. I believe in this project — let's align our interests." Studios pay less upfront; you earn 3–5x more if the film succeeds.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**Require worldwide inclusion, all revenue streams, sequel escalation** (10% Film 1, 12% Film 2, 15% Film 3). Push for quarterly reporting, annual audit rights with fee recovery, and no cross-collateralization.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**Multiple studios competing for your involvement.** Recent $500M+ commercial success. Your name greenlights the project. Franchise role where your return is essential for sequels. You can walk away.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Propose escalating/rolling break structure.** "5% adjusted gross up to $500M, 10% from $500M to $1B, 15% above $1B. This reduces your risk on moderate performers while rewarding me if the film becomes a phenomenon."</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**Insist on clean adjusted gross definition** — distribution fees capped, P&A capped, no overhead or interest. If studio resists clean definition, demand higher upfront fee: "If you can't offer transparent backend, I need $20M+ upfront."</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**Strong commercial track record but not top-3 in the world.** Studio wants you specifically but other talent could fill the role. Franchise potential strengthens your position.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Push for milestone bonuses as entry point.** "$5M bonus if film hits $500M worldwide, $10M at $1B, $15M at $1.5B. These are verifiable, not subject to accounting, and align us around success."</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**Maximize upfront salary** since true gross participation isn't available at your current leverage. Use milestone bonuses to create backend exposure without the accounting complexity.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**Above-the-line talent building toward gross participation leverage.** Not yet essential to project success. Use this project to build the commercial track record that justifies gross participation on the next one.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
</SbNeg>

<SbSection id="value">

<SbHeading>Example Value Calculation: Rami's Franchise Reboot</SbHeading>

Rami is a 42-year-old action star whose last 5 films averaged $450M worldwide. A major studio offers him the lead in a franchise reboot — $200M budget, targeting $600M–$1B. Standard offer: $25M upfront, no backend.

<SbChart title="Three-Film Franchise — Gross Participation vs. Upfront Only">
  <SbChartRow label="Escalating gross (7% → 10% → 12%)" value="$169.7M" pct="100" />
  <SbChartRow label="Upfront salary ($25M × 3)" value="$75M" pct="44" />
</SbChart>

**Upfront path:** $25M per film × 3 films = $75M total. No upside, no alignment.

**Gross participation (what Rami negotiated):** $15M upfront + 7% adjusted gross, escalating: 10% on Film 2, 12% on Film 3.

- **Film 1** ($650M worldwide): Adjusted gross after capped fees = $353M. His 7% = $24.7M + $15M base = **$39.7M**
- **Film 2** ($800M, franchise growing): Adjusted gross = $472M. His 10% = $47.2M + $15M = **$62.2M**
- **Film 3** ($900M): Adjusted gross = $530M. His 12% = $63.6M + $15M = **$67.8M** (projected)
- **Three-film total: $169.7M** vs. $75M upfront-only = **2.26x more**

**What changed:** Rami positioned the deal as partnership ("I'll reduce my quote because I believe in this franchise"), proposed escalation that reduced studio risk on Film 1, and insisted on clean adjusted gross definition. The escalation structure was the key: Film 1 at 7% was less than his full $25M quote would have been, making it easy for the studio to accept. By Film 3, the franchise success justified 12%.

<SbSection id="cases">

<SbHeading>Example Use Cases</SbHeading>

How three creatives at different levels navigated gross participation — and what the deal structure meant for outcomes.

<SbCase label="Case 01 · Film · Established" name="Rami Aziz — Action Star Franchise Deal" tag="Film / Action" outcome="$169.7M over three-film franchise">
  <SbCaseText>Rami, 42, averaged $450M per film over his last 5 projects. A major studio offered $25M flat for a franchise reboot lead. He countered with a partnership structure: $15M + escalating adjusted gross (7% → 10% → 12% across 3 films). Studio accepted because the lower upfront reduced their risk.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The shift:** Film 1 earned $650M worldwide ($39.7M total to Rami). Film 2: $800M ($62.2M). Film 3 projected at $900M ($67.8M). **Three-film total: $169.7M** — 2.26x the $75M upfront alternative. The escalation was the key innovation: studio got lower risk on Film 1, Rami got maximum upside on the franchise's growth trajectory.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** "I positioned it as 'I believe in this franchise enough to reduce my upfront.' The studio saw alignment, not greed. And the escalation gave them confidence — they only pay 12% if the franchise is a proven success by Film 3. Clean adjusted gross definition was non-negotiable: distribution fees capped at 35%, P&A at $50M, no overhead or interest."</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>

<SbCase label="Case 02 · Film · Mid-Career" name="Lena Kovac — Director's First Gross Deal" tag="Film / Drama" outcome="$28.5M (4.75x upfront alternative)">
  <SbCaseText>Lena, a director with 3 critically acclaimed films ($150–300M each), was offered $6M to direct a prestige thriller with franchise potential. She proposed: $3M upfront + 8% adjusted gross. The studio agreed because her track record of awards + commercial success was rare — and her reduced fee lowered budget risk.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The shift:** Film earned $420M worldwide. Adjusted gross (after capped distribution and P&A): $225M. Her 8% = $18M + $3M base + $7.5M from streaming licensing (she'd insisted gross include ALL revenue streams). **Total: $28.5M** vs. $6M flat fee. The streaming inclusion was worth $7.5M alone — a single clause in the contract.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** "Every director wants gross participation and thinks they can't get it. But studios know that directors who win awards AND deliver commercially are rare. My leverage wasn't action-star box office numbers — it was the combination of critical prestige and audience appeal. That combination is scarcer than $500M box office."</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>

<SbCase label="Case 03 · Film · Emerging (Entry Point)" name="Tomás Reyes — First Milestone Bonus Deal" tag="Film / Thriller" outcome="$16M (3.2x his flat fee)">
  <SbCaseText>Tomás, 34, had one breakout hit ($280M worldwide) and a strong second film ($180M). Not yet A-list, but rising. A studio offered $5M for an action-thriller lead. He couldn't command true gross participation, so he proposed milestone bonuses instead.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The shift:** $5M upfront + milestone bonuses: $3M at $300M worldwide, $4M at $500M, $4M at $750M. Film earned $620M. He hit the first two milestones: $3M + $4M = **$7M in bonuses** + $5M salary = **$12M total**. Plus: the studio offered him the sequel with escalating gross (8% adjusted) based on his commercial proof. Sequel earned $780M — his 8% adjusted + salary = **$45M.** Combined two-film total: **$57M.**</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** "Milestone bonuses were my entry point to gross participation. I couldn't command percentage-based backend on Film 1 — my track record wasn't there. But the bonuses proved I could open $600M+ films. That proof earned me real gross participation on Film 2. The first deal built the leverage for the second."</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>
