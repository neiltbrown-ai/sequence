<SbSection id="overview">

<SbHeading>Overview</SbHeading>

Founder/Co-Founder Equity represents a fundamental career transition: from trading time for money to building equity value through operational leadership. Unlike project equity (#3) where you take 1–5% for specific deliverables while remaining external, founder equity means **10–50% ownership with ongoing operational responsibility** — you're not a vendor or advisor, you're building the company.

The mathematics are stark: a creative director earning $200K annually over 7 years generates $1.4M in fees. That same creative as co-founder with 25% equity in a company that exits for $50M receives **$12.5M** — nearly 9x the fee-based income. But the risks are proportional: **70–80% of startups fail completely**, returning $0 to founders. You sacrifice stable income, predictable work, and career diversification to focus entirely on one opportunity.

The model requires four prerequisites: (1) you've identified a massive market opportunity aligned with your creative expertise, (2) you have complementary co-founders handling skills you lack (operations, technology, sales), (3) you have **12–24 months financial runway** to survive startup salaries, and (4) your creative contribution is central to company value — brand-led businesses, design-driven products, or content platforms where taste is the moat.

<SbSection id="protect">

<SbHeading>Key Protections</SbHeading>

Five provisions that protect founder interests through the years of building — from co-founder disputes through investor negotiations to exit.

<SbCards>
  <SbCard label="Co-Founder Vesting Agreement">
    <SbCardText>"All Founder equity vests over 4 years with 1-year cliff. Upon voluntary departure, unvested equity is forfeited. 90-day continued vesting on involuntary termination without cause." Standard: 25% vests at 12-month cliff, remaining 75% monthly over 36 months.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Without vesting, a co-founder can leave after 6 months with their full equity stake — taking 33% of the company while contributing 10% of the work. Vesting protects everyone: the founder who leaves forfeits unvested shares; the founders who stay aren't diluted by a departed partner.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="83(b) Election Filing">
    <SbCardText>"Within 30 days of receiving founder stock, file IRS 83(b) election to be taxed on the fair market value at grant (typically near $0 for pre-revenue companies). This converts future appreciation from ordinary income (37%+ tax) to long-term capital gains (20%)."</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Without 83(b), you're taxed at ordinary income rates as stock vests — potentially owing hundreds of thousands in taxes on illiquid stock you can't sell. Filing 83(b) at founding (when stock is worth pennies) means paying minimal tax upfront and capital gains at exit. **Miss the 30-day window and there is no remedy.**</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Founder Voting Control">
    <SbCardText>Dual-class stock: Founder Class A shares with 10 votes per share, investor Class B with 1 vote per share. Maintains voting majority even after dilution. Board: founder-majority (3 of 5 seats minimum). Founder veto on: company sale, fundraising over $X, C-suite hiring/firing.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Without voting control, investors who own 51%+ can replace you as CEO, force an exit on their timeline, or pivot the company away from your vision. Once you lose control, you're an employee of your own company. Dual-class stock ensures the founder who built it retains strategic authority.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="IP Assignment with Personal Brand Carve-Out">
    <SbCardText>"All work product created by Founder during engagement belongs to Company. EXCEPT: (a) work created before Company formation, (b) work unrelated to Company business, (c) personal brand/likeness rights retained by Founder but licensed to Company on revocable terms."</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Investors require full IP assignment — but your personal name and brand are yours, not the company's. If the company is sold, the buyer should NOT acquire your identity. License your brand to the company; never assign it. This preserves your ability to build again after any exit.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Drag-Along with Minimum Price Floor">
    <SbCardText>"If holders of 75%+ of equity vote to accept acquisition, all shareholders must sell on same terms. HOWEVER: drag-along only applies if (a) price exceeds $[X minimum], (b) all shareholders receive same per-share price, and (c) founders receive same acceleration terms as investors."</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Drag-along without a floor lets investors force a fire sale — accepting $5M when you need $50M to make the equity meaningful. The minimum price ensures you can't be dragged into an exit that doesn't compensate years of below-market salary and commitment.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
</SbCards>

<SbSection id="manipulations">

<SbHeading>Common Manipulations</SbHeading>

Four dynamics that extract value from creative co-founders. Each is common, well-documented, and preventable with the right agreements.

<SbCards>
  <SbCard label="The 'Idea Person' Equity Grab" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Non-creative co-founder claims 50% equity because "it was my idea." You're doing 80% of the work — building brand, product, and creative infrastructure — while they attend meetings and "do strategy." Equal equity for unequal contribution.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Ideas are worth $0. Execution is worth everything. Equity should reflect ongoing contribution, not initial idea. If one founder works 60 hours/week and another works 20, equity should reflect that — especially after the cliff period.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>Equity should reflect ongoing contribution, not just the initial idea. I'm proposing 60/40 based on our actual work distribution — and we'll revisit at Year 1 cliff based on documented contributions. If our workloads equalize, we can adjust. But equal equity requires equal work.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="The Investor Dilution Squeeze" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Each funding round dilutes your ownership: 40% → 28% → 18% → 12%. By Series C, your "co-founder equity" is the same percentage as a senior employee's option grant. Investors control the board and effectively control the company.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Dilution is inevitable — but excessive dilution destroys founder motivation. Dual-class voting protects control even through dilution. Anti-dilution provisions in early agreements can limit how much each round reduces your stake.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>I need dual-class voting from incorporation — 10:1 votes per share on founder stock. I'll accept economic dilution through fundraising, but I retain voting majority. Board composition: 3 founder-appointed seats, 2 investor-appointed. If control isn't preserved, I'm better off as a consultant.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="The Co-Founder Who Checks Out" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Co-founder contributes intensely for Year 1, then gradually reduces hours — 50 hours/week → 30 → 15. But they retain full vested equity (25% after cliff) and continue vesting. You're doing 80% of the work while they own 33% of the company.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Vesting protects against early departure but not gradual disengagement. Operating agreements should include minimum commitment requirements (40+ hours/week) with cure periods and equity consequences for sustained underperformance.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>Our operating agreement needs minimum commitment clauses: 40+ hours/week for all founders. If any founder drops below 30 hours for 2+ consecutive months without board approval, vesting pauses. Below 20 hours for 3+ months triggers repurchase rights on unvested shares at 409A value.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="The Forced-Out Founder" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** You build the company to $20M revenue. Investor-controlled board decides you should be replaced by a "professional CEO." You're terminated, 50% of your equity is still unvested (forfeited), and the new CEO gets a 5% option grant from your lost shares.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Extremely common in venture-backed companies. Investors optimize for financial returns, not founder vision. Acceleration clauses (single-trigger: 100% vests on acquisition; double-trigger: 100% vests if terminated after acquisition) are essential protections.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>My terms include: single-trigger acceleration on acquisition (100% vests), double-trigger on termination without cause (100% vests if I'm fired and the company is subsequently acquired). Board cannot terminate CEO without 75% supermajority vote including at least one founder-appointed seat.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
</SbCards>

<SbSection id="negotiate">

<SbHeading>Negotiation</SbHeading>

Founder negotiation happens in two phases: equity split with co-founders (before launch) and terms with investors (during fundraising). Three approaches based on your position.

<SbNeg tabsJson='["High Leverage", "Moderate", "Low Leverage"]'>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**You ARE the company.** Your brand, audience, or creative vision is the primary asset. "I'm bringing the audience (500K+), the brand, and the creative direction. I need 50–60% equity with dual-class voting and creative control provisions."</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**Set board composition and governance before incorporation.** Founder-majority board, vesting for all founders (including you — builds investor confidence), 83(b) filing, personal brand carve-out. These are non-negotiable before signing.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**Your creative contribution is the competitive moat.** Existing audience, proven taste, industry relationships that attract capital. Multiple potential co-founders competing to work with you.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Equal contribution, equal equity — with accountability.** "I'm proposing 50/50 with 4-year vesting, 1-year cliff, and minimum commitment clauses. We both vest because we both earn it."</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**Define roles and responsibilities explicitly** before incorporation. Written document: who owns what decisions, what constitutes "full-time" commitment, how disagreements are resolved. Address "what if one of us wants to leave" before it happens.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**Both co-founders bring essential, complementary skills.** Neither could build this alone. Shared risk, shared commitment, shared upside. Neither has significantly more leverage.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Accept minority stake with strong protections.** "I'll take 25–30% if we include: acceleration on exit, board seat, veto on decisions that affect my equity (dilution beyond X%, company sale below $Y), and minimum salary escalation tied to revenue milestones."</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**Negotiate performance escalators.** "Additional 5% equity vest if company hits $5M revenue within 24 months." Bridges the gap between your minority starting position and the value you'll create.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**Joining an existing founding team** or contributing less capital/risk than other founders. Your creative contribution is important but others are contributing more capital, more operational risk, or more time.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
</SbNeg>

<SbSection id="value">

<SbHeading>Example Value Calculation: Three Designers Leave an Agency</SbHeading>

Three designers at a top agency each earn $200K annually ($600K combined). They're frustrated designing products for clients who dilute their vision. They decide to found a consumer product company together.

<SbChart title="5-Year Outcome — Founder Equity vs. Agency Salary">
  <SbChartRow label="Founder equity (33% each at $400M exit)" value="$20M each" pct="100" />
  <SbChartRow label="Agency salary (5 years)" value="$1M each" pct="5" />
</SbChart>

**Agency path:** $200K × 5 years = $1M each. $3M combined. Zero ownership, zero compounding, capped by hours.

**Founder path:** Equal three-way split (33.3% each). Year 1: $0 salary, living off savings, build prototype. Kickstarter raises $500K (validation). Year 2: Seed round $2M at $8M valuation. Salary: $100K each. Year 3: Ship product, $3M revenue. Series A: $10M at $40M (dilutes to ~25% each). Year 4: $12M revenue. Series B: $30M at $120M (dilutes to ~20% each). Year 5: Acquired for **$400M**. Each founder's 20% after liquidation preferences: **~$20M** — 20x the agency path.

**Cumulative founder salary over 5 years:** ~$600K each. Plus $20M equity. **Total: $20.6M each** vs. $1M.

**What changed:** They stopped designing products for clients and started owning the product. The hardest year was Year 1 ($0 salary, manufacturing delays, near-cash-out). Year 3 was the pivot: product-market fit validated, investors competed. One co-founder nearly left at Month 14 — the vesting cliff kept them committed through the trough.

<SbSection id="cases">

<SbHeading>Example Use Cases</SbHeading>

How three creatives made the founder transition — and what happened.

<SbCase label="Case 01 · Consumer Brand · Emerging" name="Zara Mitchell — Creator to Coffee Company Co-Founder" tag="Consumer / DTC" outcome="$35M+ paper value in 4 years">
  <SbCaseText>Zara, 22, had 1.2M YouTube subscribers and $180K annual income from sponsorships. She loved coffee, had opinions about it, and noticed no creator-led coffee brand was doing it well. She partnered with an experienced CPG operator — he'd built and sold two beverage brands previously.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The shift:** Split: Zara 40% (brand, content, creative direction) / co-founder 35% (operations, supply chain, retail) / option pool 25%. Year 1: DTC launch, $1.2M revenue. Year 2: Retail expansion (2,000 stores), $4.5M. Year 3: 6,000+ stores, $18M revenue, raised $12M at $80M valuation. Year 4: $28M projected. **Zara's 40% (diluted to ~32% post-raise): $25.6M paper value.** Working 50 hours/week as Chief Brand Officer.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** The complementary co-founder was essential. Zara's taste and audience drove the brand; her co-founder's CPG experience got them into retail. Neither could have built this alone. "I could have done a licensing deal for 5–8% royalty — maybe $1.5M/year. Instead I own 32% of a company on track for a $200M+ exit."</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>

<SbCase label="Case 02 · Media · Mid-Career" name="Derek Okafor — Content Creator to Production Studio Founder" tag="Media / Content" outcome="$35.5M on $400K exit">
  <SbCaseText>Derek had 500K YouTube subscribers earning $200K/year. He saw short-form video exploding and brands desperate for content factories. He founded a vertical video production studio — sole founder with 80% equity, 20% employee option pool. Bootstrapped entirely.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The shift:** Year 1: $400K revenue (4 brand clients), $80K salary. Year 2: $1.2M (12 clients), hired 3 employees (2% each from pool). Year 3: $3.5M (35 clients), raised $3M seed at $15M valuation (diluted to 64%). Year 4: $8M revenue. Acquired by talent agency for **$60M**. Derek's 64%: **$38.4M** minus preferences = ~$35.5M. Plus $470K cumulative salary. **Total: ~$35.5M** vs. $800K on the creator path.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** "My YouTube channel was the proof of concept — but the company scaled beyond my personal brand. By Year 3, 80% of revenue came from clients who'd never heard of my channel. The business had its own gravity. Keeping 64% through a seed round was crucial — I said no to three investors who wanted 30%+ for $3M."</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>

<SbCase label="Case 03 · Design · Emerging (Cautionary)" name="Priya Sharma — Solo Founder Without Co-Founder" tag="Design / SaaS" outcome="Shut down after 3 years, $0 equity value">
  <SbCaseText>Priya, a talented UX designer earning $160K, founded a design tool startup alone. No co-founder. She was designer, PM, marketer, fundraiser, and bookkeeper. Raised $500K from angels at $3M valuation — giving up 17% immediately.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The result:** Year 1: Built product (beautiful but slow — no technical co-founder). Year 2: 200 users, $30K revenue, burning $15K/month. Year 3: Couldn't raise Series A (investors wanted technical co-founder). Money ran out. Shut down. Total foregone income: $480K (3 years × $160K). Equity value: $0. Angel investors lost $500K.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** "I was a great designer trying to be a great engineer, marketer, and CEO simultaneously. If I'd found a technical co-founder and split equity 50/50, we'd have built 3x faster and been fundable. Solo founding a tech company as a non-technical founder is the hardest path — I chose it out of ego, not strategy."</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>
