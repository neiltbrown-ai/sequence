<SbSection id="overview">

<SbHeading>Overview</SbHeading>

Option Agreements grant a buyer the exclusive right — but not the obligation — to purchase or license your creative work at a predetermined price within a specified timeframe. You receive an upfront, non-refundable option fee (typically **10–20% of the full exercise price**) immediately, while retaining complete ownership until the buyer exercises the option. If the option expires without exercise, you keep the fee and all rights revert — free to re-license to a new buyer, often at higher rates.

Options manage uncertainty and timing mismatches. A film studio wants adaptation rights to your novel but needs 18 months to develop a screenplay, attach talent, and secure financing before committing $500K+. Rather than waiting (you earn nothing) or selling outright (you lose ownership prematurely), the option creates a middle path: $50K now for 18 months of exclusivity, $500K more if they proceed. If they don't, you keep $50K and your book.

This structure works across every creative domain. Publishers option an author's next book ($15K–$100K) with a larger advance ($75K–$1M) upon manuscript delivery. Labels option next albums. Brands option creator partnerships. The pattern is consistent: modest payment for a defined waiting period, larger payment if the buyer proceeds. For creators with multiple optionable properties, the aggregate option income creates a reliable revenue stream while the occasional exercise generates windfalls.

<SbSection id="when-to-use">

<SbHeading>When to Use This Structure</SbHeading>

<SbFlags>
  <SbFlagCol header="Use When">
    <SbFlag type="use">Genuine uncertainty exists about timing or feasibility (buyer needs development time)</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="use">Work has speculative value but unproven commercial potential</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="use">Option fee is meaningful (≥10% of exercise price)</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="use">Rights can be separated (film optioned while publishing retained)</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="use">You can afford to have rights tied up during the option period</SbFlag>
  </SbFlagCol>
  <SbFlagCol header="Avoid When">
    <SbFlag type="avoid">Buyer refuses non-refundable option fee (wants "deposit" or refundable advance)</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="avoid">Exercise price is below market at signing (locked into bad deal if exercised)</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="avoid">Buyer wants indefinite extensions at minimal cost (warehousing your rights)</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="avoid">You're financially dependent on exercise happening (option fee must be sufficient alone)</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="avoid">Buyer has history of optioning rights and never exercising (defensive strategy)</SbFlag>
  </SbFlagCol>
</SbFlags>

<SbSection id="protect">

<SbHeading>Key Protections</SbHeading>

Five provisions that ensure option agreements protect your IP while generating real income.

<SbCards>
  <SbCard label="Non-Refundable Option Fee (Separate from Exercise Price)">
    <SbCardText>"Option Fee of $[X] is non-refundable under all circumstances. Fee is earned by Creator upon execution and payable immediately. If Buyer does not exercise, Creator retains Fee and all rights revert. Fee is separate from — and not an advance against — Exercise Price." Total value if exercised = option fee + exercise price, NOT just exercise price.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Refundable fees give buyers a free option — rights tied up with zero cost if they walk. Non-refundable ensures you're compensated for exclusivity regardless of exercise. Separate from exercise means the fee is additional compensation, not just early payment on the same deal.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Specific, Objective Exercise Conditions">
    <SbCardText>"To validly exercise, Buyer must within 30 days of exercise notice: (a) pay Exercise Price in full, (b) demonstrate committed financing for ≥50% of production budget, (c) attach key talent (director + lead), (d) provide production timeline with principal photography within 18 months. If any condition unsatisfied within 30 days, exercise is void."</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Without production-readiness requirements, buyers exercise speculatively — sending a notice to secure rights without actual intent or ability to proceed. Your work sits in development indefinitely. Objective conditions ensure exercise means genuine commitment, not a blocking tactic.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Limited Extensions with Escalating Fees + Exercise Price">
    <SbCardText>"Maximum TWO extensions. First: 12 months at $[X] (75% of initial fee). Second: 12 months at $[Y] (100% of initial fee). Exercise Price increases with each extension: +20% for first, +50% for second. No further extensions. After expiration, all rights automatically revert."</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Unlimited renewals at token fees let buyers warehouse your rights indefinitely. A $2,500 option renewed at $1,000/year ties up your IP for a decade at $10K total. Limited extensions with escalating costs force genuine commitment — if the buyer needs more time, they pay more, and the exercise price reflects your work's growing value.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Automatic Reversion + Development Materials Transfer">
    <SbCardText>"If Option expires unexercised, all rights automatically revert without action required. Buyer must cease development, deliver all materials (scripts, treatments, art) to Creator within 30 days, and Creator receives perpetual royalty-free license to use development materials for own exploitation."</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** If a producer develops a screenplay during the option but doesn't exercise, that screenplay has value. You should benefit from their investment if they walk away. Development materials transfer means the next buyer gets a screenplay already written — dramatically increasing your leverage.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Success-Based Exercise Price Escalators">
    <SbCardText>"Exercise Price adjusts: Base $150K. If Work appears on NYT Bestseller List: $250K. If Work sells >250K copies: $400K. If third-party offers exceed $500K: Buyer must match highest offer or Option expires." Escalators ensure the exercise price reflects market value at exercise, not speculative value at signing.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Options signed when your work is unknown lock in today's prices. If your novel becomes a bestseller during the 18-month option, you're trapped at $150K when comparable adaptations sell for $500K+. Escalators are the mechanism that prevents options from becoming below-market forced sales.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
</SbCards>

<SbSection id="manipulations">

<SbHeading>Common Manipulations</SbHeading>

Four tactics that convert temporary options into permanent control at minimal cost.

<SbCards>
  <SbCard label="Tiny Fee + Indefinite Extensions" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** "$2,500 option for 18 months. Renewable at $1,000 per year, unlimited renewals. Exercise price $50K." Over 10 years: $12,500 total for a decade of exclusivity on work worth $200K+. Buyer prevents competitors from accessing your rights at trivial cost. Your IP sits idle.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Options with unlimited cheap renewals are indefinite warehousing. They exist to block competitors, not to develop your work. Limit to 2 extensions, escalate fees, and escalate the exercise price.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>I need meaningful option compensation and limited extensions. Option fee: $12,500 (10% of exercise). Maximum two extensions: $7,500 then $10,000. Exercise price escalates: $125K base, $150K first extension, $187K second. Total maximum 42 months. After that, rights revert automatically with no further extensions.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Option Fee as 'Advance' Against Exercise" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** "$25K option fee, recoupable against $100K exercise price. Upon exercise, Creator receives $75K (balance)." Total compensation if exercised: $100K, not $125K. The option fee isn't separate payment for exclusivity — it's just early payment on the same deal.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Non-recoupable option fees are industry standard. The fee compensates you for granting exclusivity and delaying sale to others. Exercise price is separate payment for actual rights purchase. Total should equal fee PLUS exercise price.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>Option fee must be non-recoupable — separate from the exercise price. The fee compensates me for 18 months of exclusivity during which I can't sell to other buyers. Exercise price is separate payment for the actual rights. Total if exercised: $25K + $100K = $125K. If you want the fee credited against exercise, then the exercise price needs to be $125K.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Below-Market Exercise Price Locked Forever" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Option signed when your book is unknown. Exercise price: $150K. During the 18-month option, your book becomes a bestseller. Comparable adaptations now selling $500K–$1M. Buyer exercises at $150K — you've been forced into a deal $350K–$850K below market by terms set before your success.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Options without escalators reward the buyer for your success. Success-based exercise price adjustments are the standard protection. If your work appreciates, the exercise price should reflect actual market value, not speculative value at signing.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>I need success-based exercise price escalators. Base: $150K. If bestseller during option: $250K. If sales exceed 250K copies: $400K. If a third-party offers more than $500K, you match or the option expires. This ensures the exercise price reflects actual value at the time you exercise — not speculative value from 18 months ago.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Speculative Exercise Without Production Intent" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Month 16 of 18-month option: buyer hears a competitor is interested. Buyer sends exercise notice to block the competitor — no financing, no script, no director. Pays exercise price but sits on rights indefinitely without producing. Your work is "sold" but never made.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Exercise without production-readiness requirements is a defensive tactic disguised as a deal. Require financing commitments, talent attachment, and a production timeline as conditions for valid exercise.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>Valid exercise requires production readiness: committed financing for ≥50% of budget, attached director and lead, and a timeline with principal photography within 18 months of exercise. Exercise notice alone — without these conditions met within 30 days — is void. If you exercise and don't commence production within 18 months, all rights revert and I retain the exercise price.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
</SbCards>

<SbSection id="negotiate">

<SbHeading>Negotiation</SbHeading>

Option negotiation centers on fee adequacy, exercise price fairness, extension limits, and reversion protections.

<SbNeg tabsJson='["High Leverage", "Moderate", "Low Leverage"]'>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Premium option fee + success-based exercise.** "Option fee: $75K for 18 months (15% of base exercise). Exercise: $500K base with escalators to $750K if bestseller. Backend: 2.5% adjusted gross. No extensions without my approval — each at $50K with exercise price +25%."</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**Multiple buyers competing — auction dynamics.** Sequel/derivative rights excluded (separate negotiation). Creative consultation during development. Development materials revert to you if unexercised. First right to additional commissions (sequels, merchandise).</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**Hot IP, competitive interest, proven commercial potential.** Multiple studios or buyers pursuing same rights. Can walk away. Previous work successfully optioned and exercised. Your leverage is highest when the buyer fears losing to a competitor.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Standard 10–15% option with balanced protections.** "Option fee: $15K (12% of exercise). Exercise: $125K with modest escalators (+$25K if bestseller). 1 extension at $10K for 12 months. Automatic reversion. Non-refundable. Sequel rights excluded."</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**If buyer resists escalators, trade for other protections:** "I'll accept flat $125K exercise if you include 2.5% adjusted gross backend. If the film succeeds, backend compensates for the locked-in exercise price." Use backend as insurance against underpriced exercise.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**Good IP with development potential, moderate interest.** First or second option deal. Buyer is credible with production track record. Balanced negotiation where both parties see genuine potential.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Accept modest terms with fundamental protections intact.** "Option fee: $5K (10% of exercise). Exercise: $50K. 12-month option, 1 extension at $3K. Non-refundable. Automatic reversion. Sequel rights retained. Short term and low cost — but protections are non-negotiable."</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**Prioritize short option period** (12 months) over higher fee. If buyer doesn't proceed quickly, you can re-option to someone more serious — often at higher rates (first option validates interest).</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**First option deal, building track record.** Relationship value matters. Better to learn option mechanics with small fee and strong protections than to hold out for premium terms you can't yet command.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
</SbNeg>

<SbHeading>Example Value Calculation: Jennifer's Debut Thriller — Option vs. Outright Sale</SbHeading>

Jennifer, 34, has a debut thriller generating film interest before publication. She compares optioning to selling outright across three possible outcomes.

<SbChart title="3-Year Value Across Three Scenarios">
  <SbChartRow label="Option path (exercised with backend)" value="$357K" pct="100" />
  <SbChartRow label="Option path (expired + re-optioned to new buyer)" value="$215K" pct="60" />
  <SbChartRow label="Outright sale" value="$100K" pct="28" />
</SbChart>

**Outright sale:** $100K immediate. No backend. No future participation. **Total: $100K.**

**Option Scenario A (exercised, film produced):** $12K option + $85K exercise + $60K backend (2.5% adjusted gross on $8M box office) + $200K Book 2 sequel option (ROFR exercised). **Total: $357K (3.6x outright).**

**Option Scenario B (expired, re-optioned):** First option: $12K (expired, kept fee). Second option: $25K (higher — first option validated interest). Second exercise: $175K (higher market rate). **Total: $215K (2.15x outright).** Even the "failure" scenario outperformed outright sale.

**Option Scenario C (expired, never re-optioned):** $12K option fee. Rights revert. No further interest. **Total: $12K** — significantly less than outright sale. This is the risk: if your work has no future takers, the option fee alone is all you get.

**The calculus:** Jennifer estimated 60% probability of exercise (Scenario A/B blended) and 40% probability of expiration without re-option. Expected value: 60% × $286K + 40% × $12K = **$176K** — still 76% more than the $100K outright sale.

<SbSection id="cases">

<SbHeading>Example Use Cases</SbHeading>

How three creatives navigated option agreements — and what fee structure, extensions, and reversion meant for outcomes.

<SbCase label="Case 01 · Film · Emerging" name="Jennifer Park — Debut Thriller Option" tag="Publishing / Film" outcome="$357K (3.6x outright sale alternative)">
  <SbCaseText>Jennifer had a completed thriller generating film interest before publication. Producer offered: $5K option, $50K exercise, sequel rights included, 2% net profits. Her entertainment attorney benchmarked comparable deals and countered: $12K option, $85K exercise with bestseller escalators, sequel rights excluded (ROFR only), 2.5% adjusted gross (not net).</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The shift:** Option signed at $12K. Book published, moderate success. Producer secured financing, exercised at $85K (no bestseller trigger). Film released to modest success ($8M box office). Backend: $60K. Book 2 published — original producer exercised ROFR, paid $200K for sequel film rights. **Total: $357K.** If she'd accepted the original offer ($5K + $50K + net profits = ~$55K) or sold outright ($100K), she'd have captured 15–28% of what the counter-negotiation generated.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** "I almost took the first offer — $5K felt like free money. My attorney's fee was $5K, but the negotiation generated $300K+ in additional value. The biggest win was excluding sequel rights: the producer's ROFR on Book 2 still gave them priority, but at $200K market rate instead of the $50K locked into the original offer. One change to one clause = $150K difference."</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>

<SbCase label="Case 02 · Music · Mid-Career" name="Marcus Thompson — Escaping Multi-Album Options" tag="Music / R&B" outcome="$850K (vs. $350K under original terms)">
  <SbCaseText>Marcus signed a 3-album option deal at 22: $75K Album 1, $100K Album 2, $150K Album 3, label owns masters. Album 2 was a breakout hit — Grammy nomination, #3 on charts. His market value: $750K+ advances. But Album 3 was locked at $150K. Label exercised. Marcus was trapped at below-market terms on his own recordings.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The shift:** Marcus's attorney proposed: deliver Album 3 at $150K per contract, BUT retain masters (label gets 5-year distribution rights only, then masters revert). Label accepted — better than a reluctant artist delivering a bad album. Album 3 performed well. After 5-year term: Marcus licensed his own masters for $500K additional. **Lifetime Album 3 value: $850K** (with master retention) vs. **$350K** under original terms. Master retention added $500K.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** "At 22, I didn't understand what 'option' meant. I thought it was standard. By 26, I realized the label could force me to deliver at $150K when I was worth $750K. The renegotiation to retain masters was the only leverage I had — the label knew I'd deliver a terrible album if forced. Now I tell every young artist: never sign multi-album options without escalating advances, and fight for master ownership or reversion."</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>

<SbCase label="Case 03 · Publishing · Established (Cautionary)" name="David Okafor — The Perpetual Option Warehouse" tag="Publishing / Literary" outcome="$7,500 received, $200K+ in opportunity cost">
  <SbCaseText>David, a literary fiction writer, optioned his novel to a mid-tier production company: $2,500 for 18 months, renewable at $1,000/year unlimited, $50K exercise. He was thrilled. Three years later: company renewed twice ($2,000 total), developed nothing. David's novel was getting critical attention — a major studio expressed interest but couldn't pursue because rights were optioned.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The result:** After 4.5 years, rights finally reverted when company didn't renew. Major studio's interest had cooled. David eventually re-optioned to a different producer at $15K — but the window of peak interest had closed. **Total received: $7,500 across both options.** Estimated value if original deal had 12-month limit with no extensions: $150K–$250K (sold during peak interest to major studio). **Opportunity cost: $200K+.**</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** "Unlimited renewals at $1,000/year let them warehouse my rights for almost nothing. They had no development plan — just wanted to prevent competitors from acquiring it. If I'd limited to one 12-month extension at $5K, rights would have reverted in 2.5 years, and I could have sold during peak interest. One missing clause — 'maximum two extensions' — cost me $200K."</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>
