<SbSection id="overview">

<SbHeading>Overview</SbHeading>

Non-Exclusive Licensing is the simplest path from trading time to owning assets: you create work once, retain full ownership, and grant limited usage rights to multiple buyers simultaneously. A photographer licenses the same image to 500 different companies. A musician licenses one track to 12 different films and commercials. A designer licenses a template system to 200 brands. Each license generates revenue. No license transfers ownership. The work remains yours forever.

The economic power lies in **infinite scalability with zero marginal cost.** Your first license costs the full creative investment. Every subsequent license costs essentially nothing — the work already exists. License #1 might earn $5,000. License #200 earns the same $5,000 with no additional production cost. This creates compounding returns that are impossible in fee-for-service work, where every dollar requires proportional time.

Non-exclusive licensing differs from exclusive licensing in one critical way: you can license the **same work to competing buyers in overlapping markets.** Exclusive licenses command higher per-license fees (2–10x) but limit you to one buyer per market. Non-exclusive licenses trade lower per-license fees for unlimited volume. The math usually favors non-exclusive: $500 × 200 licenses ($100K) beats $5,000 × 1 exclusive ($5K) by 20x. Your strategy depends on whether your work's value comes from scarcity (exclusive) or ubiquity (non-exclusive).

<SbSection id="protect">

<SbHeading>Key Protections</SbHeading>

Five provisions that protect ownership, prevent scope creep, and ensure non-exclusive actually means non-exclusive.

<SbCards>
  <SbCard label="Explicit Scope Limitation (Usage, Territory, Duration)">
    <SbCardText>"License grants non-exclusive right to use [Work] for [specific use: web marketing] in [territory: North America] for [duration: 12 months]. All uses outside this scope require separate license at then-current rates." Every license must define three boundaries: what they can do, where they can do it, and how long they can do it.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Without explicit scope, buyers assume unlimited usage. A $2K web license becomes a $50K broadcast campaign without additional payment. Scope limitation creates upsell opportunities: buyer needs broadcast? That's a separate $15K license.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="No Sublicensing Without Written Consent">
    <SbCardText>"Licensee may not sublicense, transfer, or assign this license without Creator's prior written consent. Any sublicense requires Creator approval and additional compensation of no less than 50% of sublicense fee."</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Without this, a buyer licenses your image for $500, then sublicenses to 10 clients at $2K each — earning $19.5K on your work while you get $500. Sublicensing prohibition ensures you capture the full value of your distribution network.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Copyright Notice + Attribution Requirements">
    <SbCardText>"All licensed uses must include: © [Year] [Creator Name]. For digital uses: hyperlinked credit to [Creator website]. Attribution may only be waived with separate written agreement and 25% surcharge on license fee."</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Attribution builds your brand with every license. Each use of your work with credit is free marketing that drives future licensing inquiries. Waiving attribution should cost the buyer more — they're removing your marketing benefit.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Competing Use Restriction (Optional)">
    <SbCardText>"Creator reserves right to restrict licenses to direct competitors within same product category during license term. Competitor restriction applies only when Licensee pays 2x standard non-exclusive rate (Semi-Exclusive Premium)."</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Some buyers will pay premium for non-exclusive licenses that exclude their direct competitors. This creates a middle ground between full exclusive (expensive, limits you) and pure non-exclusive (cheap, unlimited). Semi-exclusive at 2x rate captures 80% of exclusive's value without fully locking up your asset.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Automatic License Expiration + Renewal Terms">
    <SbCardText>"License expires automatically at end of term. No implied renewal. Continued use after expiration constitutes infringement at 3x standard license rate. Renewal available at then-current rates with 30-day advance notice."</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Without automatic expiration, buyers continue using your work indefinitely without paying renewal fees. The 3x infringement rate creates strong incentive to renew on time. "Then-current rates" ensures your pricing evolves as your catalog value increases.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
</SbCards>

<SbSection id="manipulations">

<SbHeading>Common Manipulations</SbHeading>

Four tactics that convert non-exclusive licenses into permanent work-for-hire by another name.

<SbCards>
  <SbCard label="The 'Perpetual License' Ownership Grab" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Contract says "non-exclusive, perpetual, irrevocable, worldwide license." Sounds like a license, but "perpetual" + "irrevocable" = they own it functionally forever. You retain copyright in name only — they never need to renew, renegotiate, or pay again. Your $2K license is functionally a $2K buyout.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Perpetual licenses eliminate your renewal income and pricing power. Always limit duration (1–3 years) with renewal at then-current rates. If they insist on perpetual, charge 5–10x the term-limited rate.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>A perpetual irrevocable license is functionally a buyout. I license on 12-month terms with renewal at then-current rates. If you want perpetual rights, the fee is 8x the annual license — $16K instead of $2K. That's the true value of never having to renew.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="The 'Broad Usage' Scope Expansion" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** License says "for marketing purposes." Buyer uses your image on website (intended), social media (arguably marketing), product packaging (stretch), and a Times Square billboard (definitely not what you priced). All "marketing" by their definition. Your $3K web license just funded a $50K+ campaign.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** "Marketing" is infinitely expandable. Specify exact channels: "digital display advertising on Licensee's owned websites and social media accounts. No print, broadcast, OOH, or third-party placement." Each channel is a separate line item.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>I price by usage channel. This license covers your website and social media at $3K. Print adds $4K. Broadcast is $12K. OOH/billboard is $8K. I can bundle all four channels at $22K — 20% discount from individual pricing. Which channels do you need?</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="The Platform Rights Grab" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Platform's terms of service include: "By uploading content, Creator grants Platform a non-exclusive, perpetual, worldwide license to use, reproduce, distribute, display, and create derivative works." You uploaded your image to earn licensing income. The platform itself now has rights to license your work — competing with you.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Read platform TOS carefully. Many stock platforms claim broad rights beyond what's necessary for their marketplace function. Best platforms limit their license to "displaying and distributing on behalf of Creator for licensing purposes only."</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>I've reviewed your platform terms. Section 8 grants you derivative works rights beyond what's needed for marketplace display. I need that narrowed to "display and distribution on the Platform for the purpose of licensing to end users on Creator's behalf." I won't upload to platforms that claim broader rights than necessary.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="The AI Training Data Appropriation" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Licensee (or platform) uses your non-exclusively licensed work to train AI models. The AI then generates unlimited derivative works that compete with your original. Your $500 license funded a competitor that produces infinite imitations of your style. No additional compensation.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** AI training rights are the newest licensing battleground. Explicitly exclude AI/ML training from every license: "License does not include right to use Work as training data for machine learning, artificial intelligence, or generative models."</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>My license explicitly excludes AI/ML training use. If you want to include my work in training datasets, that's a separate license category at $[25K] minimum — reflecting the unlimited derivative value AI generates from each training input. Standard usage license and AI training license are completely separate.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
</SbCards>

<SbSection id="negotiate">

<SbHeading>Negotiation</SbHeading>

Non-exclusive licensing negotiation centers on scope, volume, and whether to add semi-exclusive premiums.

<SbNeg tabsJson='["High Leverage", "Moderate", "Low Leverage"]'>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Premium direct licensing with tiered scope pricing.** "Web usage: $5K/year. Social media: +$3K. Print: +$8K. Broadcast: +$15K. All-channel bundle: $25K/year (20% discount). Each channel is separately priced because each has different value."</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**Semi-exclusive premium** (2x rate to exclude direct competitors). Volume discounts for 5+ licenses. Escalating rates as your catalog/reputation grows. First-mover pricing for early licensees.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**Unique, in-demand work with proven licensing history.** Buyers approach you (inbound). Track record of high-performing licensed assets. Premium positioning justified by commercial results.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Balanced direct + platform licensing with scope controls.** Maintain platform presence for volume while building direct licensing relationships at 3–5x platform rates. Standard scope limitations on all licenses.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**Renewal incentives** (10% discount for multi-year commitments). Category-based pricing (editorial vs. commercial vs. advertising). Attribution requirements that drive discovery.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**Growing portfolio with some direct demand.** Platform income provides baseline. Direct licensing building. Each direct client represents 3–10x the revenue of platform licenses.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Platform-first strategy with catalog growth focus.** Accept platform rates (15–50% royalty) to build volume and discoverability. Target 500+ assets within 24 months. Use platform analytics to identify highest-performing categories.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**Retain all rights outside the platform.** Don't accept platforms that claim exclusive or perpetual rights. Use platform income to fund creation of premium assets for future direct licensing.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**Building catalog, establishing market presence.** Volume and discoverability matter more than per-license fee. Every asset is an investment in future licensing income.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
</SbNeg>

<SbHeading>Example Value Calculation: Tomás's Music Licensing Catalog</SbHeading>

Tomás is an ambient/electronic producer who builds a non-exclusive music licensing catalog over 3 years, licensing through platforms and direct relationships simultaneously.

<SbChart title="3-Year Licensing Income — Catalog vs. Commission-Only">
  <SbChartRow label="Non-exclusive licensing catalog" value="$287K" pct="100" />
  <SbChartRow label="Commission work only (no catalog)" value="$180K" pct="63" />
</SbChart>

**Year 1 (50 tracks):** Platform income (Artlist, Epidemic Sound): $18K. Direct licenses (small sync placements): $12K. **Total: $30K.** Production cost: ~200 hours.

**Year 2 (120 tracks, cumulative):** Platform income: $42K (earlier tracks still earning). Direct licenses: $35K (reputation growing, inbound requests). **Total: $77K.** Production: ~150 hours (faster workflow).

**Year 3 (200 tracks, cumulative):** Platform income: $68K (catalog compounding). Direct licenses: $62K (including $25K Apple ad placement). Semi-exclusive premium licenses: $50K (3 licenses at 2x rate). **Total: $180K.** Production: ~120 hours.

**3-year total: $287K** from 200 tracks (470 hours total). Effective rate: **$611/hour.** Commission work over same period: $180K (estimated 1,800 hours). Effective rate: $100/hour. Licensing catalog earned **$107K more at 6x the hourly rate** — and the catalog continues earning indefinitely. Year 4 projected: $220K+ with zero new tracks added.

<SbHeading>Example Use Cases</SbHeading>

How three creatives built non-exclusive licensing businesses — at different scales and in different industries.

<SbCase label="Case 01 · Photography · Mid-Career" name="Nia Osei — Travel Photographer's Tiered Licensing" tag="Photography / Travel" outcome="$195K/year (3x previous income) from 4,200 images">
  <SbCaseText>Nia, 33, transitioned from assignment photography ($65K/year) to licensing-first. Over 3 years, she built a 4,200-image catalog across three tiers: premium stock (Stocksy, 50% royalty), mid-tier (Adobe Stock, 33%), and volume (Shutterstock, 15–40%). She also maintained a direct licensing portal on her website.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The shift:** Year 3 breakdown: Stocksy (800 premium images): $52K. Adobe Stock (2,000 images): $38K. Shutterstock (4,200 images): $25K. Direct licensing (48 custom licenses, $500–$8K each): $80K. **Total: $195K/year** — 3x her previous assignment income. Time spent on catalog maintenance: ~10 hours/week. She still shoots assignments selectively at premium rates ($5K/day) — adding 300+ licensable images per year.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** "The tiered platform strategy was essential. Premium platforms pay more per download but generate fewer downloads. Volume platforms pay less but move high volume. Direct licensing is the most profitable — but only works because the platforms established my discoverability first. Each tier serves a different function: volume for baseline income, premium for per-image value, direct for maximum revenue."</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>

<SbCase label="Case 02 · Design · Established" name="Marco Vasquez — UI Component Library" tag="Design / Software" outcome="$480K/year from one component system">
  <SbCaseText>Marco, a senior product designer, built a comprehensive UI component library (1,200+ components, 40+ page templates) and licensed it non-exclusively through his own website and marketplace platforms. Per-license pricing: Personal ($49), Team ($199), Enterprise ($999). Annual subscription model.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The shift:** Year 1: 2,100 personal + 340 team + 45 enterprise = **$216K.** Year 2: 3,800 personal + 680 team + 120 enterprise = **$441K.** Year 3: 4,200 personal + 900 team + 180 enterprise = **$565K** (trending). Average across 3 years: **~$480K/year.** Time investment: 6 months initial build (2,000 hours), then ~20 hours/week maintenance and updates. Effective rate after Year 1: **$280/hour** (and rising as subscriber base grows).</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** "The component library is one product licensed thousands of times. My competitors sell exclusive client work — one design for one buyer. I sell the same design system to 5,000+ buyers simultaneously. Non-exclusive licensing only works if your work has broad utility — a custom brand identity should be exclusive, but a UI system should serve everyone."</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>

<SbCase label="Case 03 · Music · Emerging (Cautionary)" name="Yuki Tanaka — Platform Dependency Risk" tag="Music / Sync" outcome="$78K → $31K (–60% in one year from platform changes)">
  <SbCaseText>Yuki, a composer with 300 tracks, built her licensing income entirely on one platform: $78K in Year 2. Then the platform restructured: reduced per-license fees 40%, changed from per-track to subscription model (unlimited downloads for flat monthly fee), and added 50,000 AI-generated tracks that competed directly with her catalog.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The result:** Year 3 income: $31K (–60%). Same 300 tracks, same quality, dramatically less revenue. Single-platform dependency meant one business decision wiped out $47K annually. Recovery took 18 months of diversifying across 4 platforms + building direct licensing relationships.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** "I treated platform licensing like a job — one employer, one income source. When they changed terms, I had no leverage and no alternatives. Now I'm on 4 platforms, have a direct licensing site, and no single platform is more than 30% of my income. The platform giveth and the platform taketh away — diversification is the only protection."</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>
