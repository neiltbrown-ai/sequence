<SbSection id="overview">

<SbHeading>Overview</SbHeading>

Net Profit Participation grants you a percentage of a project's profits **after all expenses** have been deducted — production costs, distribution fees, marketing, overhead, interest, and often other talent's gross participation. Unlike gross participation (#22), which pays from revenue before most costs, net participation only begins after the project "breaks even" on paper.

The structure earned its infamous nickname **"monkey points"** because even massively successful projects frequently show zero net profit through creative accounting. A $678M hit can show a $62M "loss." A $938M film can report zero net profit. These aren't anomalies — they're the predictable outcome of uncapped overhead (25%+ of budget), compound interest on production costs, affiliated company markups, and cross-collateralization. In major studio deals, **95%+ of net profit participants receive $0 regardless of commercial success.**

However, net participation isn't inherently worthless. In **transparent partnerships** with indie producers, small labels, or creator-led projects — where accounting is straightforward, costs are capped, and both parties genuinely want shared success — net participation can significantly outperform flat fees. The key distinction: net participation fails with entities that have infrastructure and incentive to manipulate accounting. It works with transparent partners who share detailed financials and cap costs contractually. **Know the difference before you sign.**

<SbSection id="protect">

<SbHeading>Key Protections</SbHeading>

Five provisions that separate net participation that pays from net participation that evaporates.

<SbCards>
  <SbCard label="Explicit Net Profit Definition (No External References)">
    <SbCardText>"Net Profits shall be defined as Gross Receipts less the following costs, and ONLY the following costs: (a) direct production costs with receipts, (b) P&A capped at $[X], (c) distribution fees capped at 33% domestic / 42% international. NO overhead, interest, or indirect costs shall be deducted." The contract must define every deduction — not reference external accounting documents.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Contracts that define net by reference to "Studio's customary accounting practices" or "Exhibit A" (40 pages of dense accounting definitions) are designed to be unreadable and unenforceable. If you can't understand the definition, you can't protect yourself. Every deduction must be named and capped in plain language.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Overhead Cap at 10–15% Maximum">
    <SbCardText>"Overhead shall not exceed fifteen percent (15%) of Direct Production Costs only. Overhead shall not be calculated on marketing, distribution, or other expenses. Producer shall provide itemized breakdown annually." Studios routinely allocate 25–30%+ to overhead — executive salaries, building rent, corporate jets — none related to your project.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Uncapped overhead is the primary tool for eliminating net profits. A $100M film with 25% overhead = $25M added before your calculation. At 15% capped on direct production costs only, overhead drops to $15M. That $10M difference can be the entire net profit pool.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="No Interest Charges (or Strict Caps)">
    <SbCardText>"No interest, financing charges, or carrying costs shall accrue on production budget or any other costs." Studios charge compound interest at prime + 3–5%, turning a $50M budget into $63M over 3 years. If interest must apply: prime rate only, non-compounding, calculated only from release date.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Interest is invisible profit extraction. It adds 15–25% to the cost base over a typical 3-year production-to-accounting cycle. Your project was greenlit because the studio expected profit — why should you pay their cost of capital?</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Profit Floor Definition">
    <SbCardText>"Notwithstanding any other provision, Net Profits shall not be less than twenty percent (20%) of Adjusted Gross Receipts [defined as gross revenue less distribution fees and P&A, capped as above]." This guarantees some payout if the project achieves commercial success, regardless of cost manipulation.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** A floor makes net participation function like gross participation with a lower percentage. Even if the studio claims zero net profit, the floor ensures your minimum payout scales with revenue. This single provision transforms "monkey points" into real compensation.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Audit Rights with Fee Recovery">
    <SbCardText>"Participant has right to audit books annually upon 30 days notice. If discrepancies exceeding 5% are found, Producer pays audit costs plus 10% annual interest on underpayment." Without fee recovery, a $50K audit cost prevents challenging $100K in questionable expenses.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Audit rights without fee recovery are economically useless — the cost of auditing exceeds what you'd recover. Fee recovery creates deterrent: the producer knows manipulated numbers will be caught and will cost them more than honest reporting.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
</SbCards>

<SbSection id="manipulations">

<SbHeading>Common Manipulations</SbHeading>

Four accounting tactics that ensure net profit participants receive $0 — even on commercially successful projects.

<SbCards>
  <SbCard label="The Overhead Allocation Scam" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Studio allocates 25–30% of production budget to "overhead" — executive salaries, building rent, corporate services — before calculating net profits. $50M budget becomes $65M. None of these costs relate to your project specifically.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Overhead allocation is the primary tool for eliminating net profits. At major studios, overhead alone can exceed the entire net profit margin. Cap overhead at 15% of direct production costs in your contract — and ensure it's calculated only on production, not marketing or distribution.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>Your overhead allocation of 25% is unreasonable. I need overhead capped at 15% of direct production costs only — not marketing, distribution, or other expenses. If you can't agree, my upfront fee needs to increase $50K to compensate for backend that will never pay out.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="The Interest Compounding Trap" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Studio charges compound interest at prime + 4% on all production costs. $50M budget compounds monthly for 3 years (production through release to accounting): $50M becomes $63M. Your net participation must recoup an additional $13M that has nothing to do with production.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Interest charges alone can add 15–25% to the cost base. If production costs $50M and marketing costs $50M, interest adds $19M+ over 3 years. That $19M could be the entire net profit margin — extracted before your participation begins.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>Interest charges on production costs are unacceptable — I'm helping create value for this project, not borrowing money from you. No interest should apply. If you insist, cap it at prime rate only, non-compounding, calculated only from release date — not from first expenditure.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="The Affiliated Company Self-Deal" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Studio routes services through affiliated companies at inflated rates. Distribution through studio subsidiary: 45% fee (market: 33%). Marketing through affiliated agency: 15% markup. Post-production at studio facilities: 20% above market. All padding, none legitimate. Excess charges extracted before net profit calculation.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Related-party transactions are the second-most-common vehicle (after overhead) for profit extraction. If the studio controls both sides of a transaction, they set the price — and the price always inflates costs at your expense.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>All services from affiliated entities must be at market rates, verified by independent third-party comparables. If affiliated fees exceed market by more than 10%, I have approval rights over those vendors. Without this protection, I assume this participation will never pay out.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="The Cross-Collateralization Trap" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Your successful project's profits pay for the company's failed projects. Album 1 earns $500K profit, Album 2 loses $300K. Label cross-collateralizes: you receive 50% of $200K ($100K) instead of 50% of $500K ($250K). Your hit subsidizes their flop.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Cross-collateralization is standard in music, publishing, and multi-project entertainment deals. It means your success can be offset by failures you had nothing to do with. Each project must be treated as a separate financial entity.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>I need each project treated as a separate financial entity — no cross-collateralization. My successful work shouldn't subsidize your other investments. If you can't agree, I need a higher advance to compensate for the risk of my profits being absorbed by unrelated losses.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
</SbCards>

<SbSection id="negotiate">

<SbHeading>Negotiation</SbHeading>

Net participation negotiation is fundamentally a leverage question. Three approaches based on your position.

<SbNeg tabsJson='["High Leverage", "Moderate", "Low Leverage"]'>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Demand gross participation or reject net entirely.** "Net participation from a major studio historically pays $0. I need adjusted gross (#22) or my upfront fee increases 30–50% to compensate for worthless backend."</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**If forced into net, require full protections:** explicit definition, 10% overhead cap, no interest, affiliated company restrictions, floor at 20% of adjusted gross, quarterly reporting, and audit rights with fee recovery. All eight provisions are non-negotiable.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**Proven commercial value, competing offers.** Your involvement drives revenue. You can walk away. Multiple projects want your participation. You don't need this deal.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Accept net only with transparent indie partners.** "I'll take 25% net with explicit cost definitions, 15% overhead cap, no interest, and quarterly reporting. If you're transparent, this works for both of us. If not, I need 80% of my fee upfront."</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**Hybrid structure:** 70–80% of normal fee upfront + net participation as bonus. Never sacrifice full fee for net points alone. Add milestone bonuses as backup (verifiable, can't be manipulated).</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**Good track record, growing leverage.** Partner wants your involvement but alternatives exist. You believe in the project and trust the partner's transparency — but verify contractually.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Maximize upfront fee, treat net as lottery ticket.** "I appreciate the net participation offer, but I view it as bonus compensation. My upfront fee needs to reflect fair market value for my contribution — net points are additional, not replacement."</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**If upfront is firm, add milestone bonuses instead of depending on net.** "$5K bonus at 10K copies, $15K at 25K copies" — verifiable, can't be manipulated. Net participation expectations: $0. Plan accordingly.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**Early career, building track record.** Need the credit/experience. Can't negotiate from strength. Better to accept fair upfront terms and learn the mechanics than fight for protections you can't enforce.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
</SbNeg>

<SbSection id="value">

<SbHeading>Example Value Calculation: Two Identical Films — Two Different Outcomes</SbHeading>

Same screenplay, same $12M budget, same $120M worldwide box office. Two producers make two different net participation offers. The contract terms determine whether the writer earns $0 or $288K.

<SbChart title="Same $120M Hit — Two Net Participation Outcomes">
  <SbChartRow label="Indie producer (capped costs)" value="$288K" pct="100" />
  <SbChartRow label="Major studio (uncapped)" value="$0" pct="0" />
</SbChart>

**Major studio version:** Studio receives ~$60M (after theatrical cut). Deductions: production $12M, P&A $18M, distribution 35% ($21M), overhead 25% ($3M), interest 8% for 3 years ($7.2M), gross participations to stars ($15M). Total deductions: $76.2M. Net profit: **-$16.2M ("loss").** Writer's 5%: **$0.**

**Indie producer version (capped costs):** Producer receives ~$60M. Deductions: production $12M (capped), P&A $6M (capped at 50% of budget), distribution 30% ($18M, capped). No overhead. No interest. No gross participations. Total deductions: $36M. Net profit: **$24M.** Writer's 12%: **$288K.**

**What changed:** Not the film. Not the box office. The contract. Capped costs prevented $40.2M in deductions that would have evaporated the entire profit. The writer's net participation went from $0 to $288K — entirely because of five contract provisions.

<SbHeading>Example Use Cases</SbHeading>

How three creatives experienced net profit participation — one success, one near-miss, and one cautionary tale.

<SbCase label="Case 01 · Documentary · Emerging" name="Maya Chen — Doc Filmmaker with Transparent Partner" tag="Documentary / Impact" outcome="$172K total ($112K from net participation)">
  <SbCaseText>Maya, 28, with 100K YouTube subscribers, partnered with an impact investment fund (Footprint Films LLC) to make a $175K environmental documentary. Director fee: $35K. Net profit split: 40% to Maya, 60% to Footprint. Cost definition: actual production costs only — no overhead, no interest, no affiliated companies.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The shift:** Film premiered at Hot Docs, acquired by a major streamer for $400K. Educational distribution: $45K. YouTube ad revenue: $12K. Merch: $18K. **Total revenue: $475K.** Costs (all documented with receipts): $195K. Net profit: $280K. Maya's 40%: **$112K** + $35K director fee + $25K speaking fees = **$172K total** from a $175K documentary.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** "Net participation worked because Footprint was transparent — they showed me the books, capped costs in the contract, and actually wanted me to succeed financially. I'd never take net profit from a major studio, but with the right indie partner, it can be transformative. My $35K fee became $172K because the structure was honest."</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>

<SbCase label="Case 02 · Music · Mid-Career" name="Sofia Martinez — Indie Label 50/50 Net Split" tag="Music / Indie" outcome="$241K over 5 years (+69% vs. traditional royalty)">
  <SbCaseText>Sofia, 26, had 250K social followers and 5M streams on 2 indie EPs. An indie label (12-person operation) offered 50/50 net profit split after ALL costs — both sides' costs recouped from the same pool. Advance: $25K. She retained 50% master ownership. No cross-collateralization.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The shift:** Album generated $388K in 18 months (45M streams, vinyl sales, 2 sync licenses). Costs: $143K. Net profit: $245K. Sofia's 50%: **$122.5K.** Year 2 catalog revenue: $48.5K. Years 3–5: ~$70K. **5-year total: ~$241K** vs. $143K on a traditional 18% royalty deal. Plus she owns 50% of her masters — worth $150–250K if sold at 5x annual streaming.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** "The traditional music industry wants you to think you need them more than they need you. If you have even a modest following, you have leverage. Indies offering 50/50 net profit with master ownership are far better than major label 15% royalties where you own nothing. I make more money, own my music, and the label wants the album to succeed because we both win."</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>

<SbCase label="Case 03 · Film · Established (Cautionary)" name="Jordan Rivers — Screenwriter's Studio Dilemma" tag="Film / Thriller" outcome="$400K (chose upfront certainty — left $1M+ on table)">
  <SbCaseText>Jordan, 42, with 3 produced features and an Emmy nomination, had a spec script generating bidding interest. Studio offered $400K purchase + 5% net (standard — expected value: $0). An indie producer offered $225K upfront + 12% net with full protections (capped costs, no overhead, no interest, audit rights).</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The result:** Jordan chose the studio's $400K for certainty. Film earned $48M worldwide. Studio's accounting showed $3M loss. Jordan's 5% net: **$0.** If he'd taken the indie offer and the same film performed identically: $24M to producer, $14M capped costs, $10M net profit. His 12%: $1.2M + $225K upfront = **$1.425M.** He left $1M+ on the table by choosing certainty over protected net participation.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** "At 42 with a family, I chose certainty. That was rational for my risk profile — but it cost me $1M. The lesson isn't that I made the wrong choice. It's that the indie offer with real protections was a fundamentally different instrument than the studio's net participation. One was worth $1.2M. The other was worth $0. Same words — 'net profit participation' — completely different economics."</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>
