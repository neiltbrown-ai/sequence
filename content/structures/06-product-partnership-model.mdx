<SbSection id="overview">

<SbHeading>Overview</SbHeading>

Product Partnership Models represent the evolution from pure licensing (flat fee for your name) to genuine co-creation where you participate in the product's success. You contribute brand equity, creative direction, audience access, and taste-making authority. The brand partner contributes capital, manufacturing, supply chain, retail distribution, and operational infrastructure. Rather than a one-time licensing fee, you negotiate **10–20% of gross revenue** or **15–35% equity** in the product line, plus creative approval rights.

Unlike a Joint Venture (#5) where you form a new legal entity, product partnerships typically operate within the brand's existing structure — simpler to set up, less operational burden on you, but also less ownership and governance control. The tradeoff is accessibility: you can launch a product partnership in 6–12 months versus 12–24 months for a JV, with lower legal costs and without the operational commitment of co-running a company.

The economics scale dramatically with audience size and engagement. A creator with **100K+ engaged followers** who can convert 2–5% of their audience into product buyers creates enormous value for brand partners. At 15% gross revenue on a product line doing $3M annually, that's **$450K/year** — recurring, growing, and building toward enterprise value if the product line is eventually sold (at 3–7x revenue multiples). Compare that to a flat licensing fee of $50–100K. **Participation in success creates 5–10x more wealth than renting your name.**

<SbFlags>
  <SbFlagCol header="Use When">
    <SbFlag type="use">You have 100K+ engaged followers who trust your recommendations</SbFlag>

    <SbFlag type="use">Partner has proven operational track record in the category</SbFlag>

<SbSection id="protect">

<SbHeading>Key Protections</SbHeading>

Five provisions that separate profitable product partnerships from exploitation disguised as opportunity.

<SbCards>
  <SbCard label="Gross Revenue Definition">
    <SbCardText>"Revenue means all amounts received from product sales, including DTC, wholesale, and all other channels. Excludes only: (a) actual product returns, (b) sales taxes collected, (c) payment processing fees not exceeding 3%." This is the most important clause in the entire agreement — everything else flows from how "revenue" is defined.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** "Net revenue" definitions let partners deduct COGS (40–60%), marketing (20–30%), overhead (25%), and distribution fees (15%) — turning $1M gross into $50K "net." Your 10% of net = $5K vs. 10% of gross = $100K.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Creative Approval Rights">
    <SbCardText>"Creator shall have final written approval over: (a) product formulation/ingredients, (b) product design and packaging, (c) pricing strategies, (d) retail partnerships, (e) all marketing materials featuring Creator's name/likeness." Don't accept "consultation" — you need approval with veto power.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Without approval rights, the partner can cheapen the product, change the formula, or run marketing that damages your brand. Your name is on it — you need to control what it represents.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Quarterly Reporting + Audit Rights">
    <SbCardText>"Company shall provide quarterly reports detailing: unit sales by SKU, gross revenue and deductions, revenue share calculation. Creator has right to audit books annually at Company's expense if discrepancy exceeds 5%."</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Without reporting, you're trusting the partner to calculate your share honestly. Audit rights ensure accountability. The 5% threshold means audits are at their cost when they underreport.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Minimum Revenue Guarantee">
    <SbCardText>"Company guarantees minimum annual payment of $[X] regardless of sales. Guarantee is recoupable against future revenue share for 24 months only." This protects your downside if the product underperforms — you gave up other opportunities for this partnership.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Without a guarantee, you could invest 200 hours in product development and promotion, the product flops, and you earn $0. The guarantee ensures minimum compensation for your contribution.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Acceleration on Sale">
    <SbCardText>"Upon sale of Company or Product Line, Creator's revenue share converts to lump sum equal to 3x trailing 12-month revenue share, payable at closing." This captures exit value that otherwise goes entirely to the brand partner.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** If the product line sells for $30M, without this clause you get nothing beyond your ongoing revenue share (which terminates). With it, you capture a meaningful exit payout.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
</SbCards>

<SbSection id="manipulations">

<SbHeading>Common Manipulations</SbHeading>

Four tactics that erode product partnership value. Each one is systematic, not accidental — and each is preventable.

<SbCards>
  <SbCard label="The 'Net Revenue' Shell Game" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Contract defines "Net Revenue" as gross revenue less: COGS (40–60%), shipping (10–15%), marketing (20–30%), overhead allocation (25%), distribution fees (15%). On $1M gross, "net" becomes $50K. Your 10% of net = $5K instead of 10% of gross = $100K.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Every line item in the "net" definition is money the brand would spend anyway. They're deducting their own business expenses from your share.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>I need gross revenue participation with limited, capped deductions. I'll accept a lower percentage on gross — say 12% instead of 15% — but it needs to be calculated on gross sales with only returns, sales tax, and processing fees deducted.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Inflated Related-Party Costs" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Manufacturing through a sister company at 2x market rate. "Distribution fees" to a related entity. "Marketing services" from partner's agency at inflated rates. All deducted before calculating your share.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Related-party transactions are the primary mechanism for extracting value before revenue share calculations. If costs seem high, they probably are.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>COGS shall not exceed $[X] per unit as verified by independent third-party quotes. Any manufacturing cost above this benchmark shall not be deductible. I reserve the right to request competitive bids annually for all related-party services.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="The Exclusivity Lockout" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** "Creator agrees not to endorse, promote, or participate in any products that compete during Term and for 24 months after termination." This blocks your entire category for years — even if the partnership fails or the partner terminates you.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Broad non-competes are designed to lock you in. If the product underperforms, you can't leave AND you can't do anything else in the category.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>Exclusivity limited to this specific product subcategory only. Non-compete terminates immediately upon contract termination. If you want post-termination exclusivity, it requires continued minimum guarantee payments during that period.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Revenue Recognition Delays" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Partner doesn't count sales until payment clears (60–90 days). Holds 20–30% "reserve for returns" for 12+ months. Reports on cash basis instead of accrual. Your money sits in their account as free working capital.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Delayed recognition is a cash flow manipulation. The partner has the money — they're choosing when to report it to you.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>Revenue recognized on accrual basis when products ship. Returns reserve capped at 10%, reconciled quarterly. Reports and payments due within 30 days of quarter-end. Late payments accrue interest at 1.5% per month.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
</SbCards>

<SbSection id="negotiate">

<SbHeading>Negotiation</SbHeading>

Your leverage determines your strategy. Three approaches based on audience size, conversion ability, and how many brands want to partner with you.

<SbNeg tabsJson='["High Leverage", "Moderate", "Low Leverage"]'>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Open at 20–25% gross revenue** or 35–40% equity in the product line entity. Request $200–500K minimum guarantee for Years 1–2. "My audience has proven 5%+ conversion — my name is the primary acquisition channel."</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**Demand full creative approval,** acceleration clause on sale (3x trailing revenue share), and limited exclusivity (specific subcategory only).</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**500K+ engaged followers,** proven conversion, multiple brands competing for your partnership, your name is the primary selling point.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Target 12–18% gross revenue** with hybrid compensation (share + equity or share + guarantee). "I'll take 14% gross with a $75K Year 1 guarantee — that balances my risk with your distribution upside."</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**Push for audit rights,** quarterly reporting, and creative approval on product quality and marketing. Negotiate acceleration clause and reasonable non-compete.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**100–500K followers,** engaged audience but unproven product conversion. Both your name and the brand's reputation matter equally.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Accept 8–12% gross** with performance escalator. "8% on first $500K, 12% above $500K, 15% above $2M." Your share grows as you prove value.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**Propose phased partnership** — limited product run first, escalate terms if it performs. Include termination clause with audience portability if sales drop below threshold.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**First product partnership.** Under 100K followers or unproven conversion. Brand's distribution is the primary value driver — your audience is additive, not primary.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
</SbNeg>

<SbSection id="value">

<SbHeading>Example Value Calculation: Priya's Spice Brand Partnership</SbHeading>

Priya is a food content creator with 180K Instagram followers and a published cookbook. A strategic CPG brand offers her three options for an Indian-inspired spice blend line (6 SKUs). She evaluates each against 3-year projected revenue of $5.9M.

<SbChart title="3-Year Total Compensation — Three Partnership Structures">
  <SbChartRow label="Revenue share + equity (hybrid)" value="$2.94M" pct="100" />
  <SbChartRow label="Revenue share only (18%)" value="$1.06M" pct="36" />
</SbChart>

**Without the structure:** Priya accepts 18% gross revenue share with no equity. Over 3 years: $1.06M in cash. Strong income, zero capital risk — but when the partner sells the sub-brand in Year 3, Priya receives nothing from the exit.

**With the structure:** Priya trades 4 percentage points (18% → 14%) for **22% equity** in the product line entity. Revenue share: $826K over 3 years. Brand acquired in Year 3 — her 22% = $2.11M. **Total: $2.94M** — nearly 3x the revenue-share-only deal. The partner's Target/Walmart distribution was something she could never replicate alone.

**What changed:** Priya recognized that equity captures enterprise value that revenue share misses. The 4% she gave up in annual share ($236K over 3 years) bought her $2.11M in exit value. That's a 9x return on the revenue she "lost."

<SbSection id="cases">

<SbHeading>Example Use Cases</SbHeading>

How three creators at different career stages structured product partnerships — and what happened.

<SbCase label="Case 01 · Beauty · Mid-Career" name="Lena Torres — Skincare Creator" tag="Beauty / Skincare" outcome="$2.34M over 3 years">
  <SbCaseText>Lena, a skincare educator with 320K followers and 8% engagement, was offered $100K flat licensing for a serum line. She calculated: if the line does $5M, a 15% gross share would pay $750K — 7.5x the flat fee.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The shift:** She negotiated **15% gross revenue** with $100K Year 1 guarantee and an acceleration clause (3x trailing share if brand sold). Year 1: $225K. Year 2: $525K. Year 3: beauty conglomerate acquired the line — acceleration triggered: $1.59M. **Total: $2.34M** vs. $100K flat fee.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** The acceleration clause was worth 68% of her total compensation. Most creators never negotiate it — yet it's the single highest-value protection in product partnerships.</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>

<SbCase label="Case 02 · Supplements · Emerging" name="Marcus Webb — Fitness Creator" tag="Supplements" outcome="$760K over 3 years">
  <SbCaseText>Marcus, 180K YouTube subscribers in science-based fitness, was earning $25K/year on affiliate commissions. The supplement company approached him about co-developing a pre-workout line because his conversion rates were 3x their other affiliates.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The shift:** He negotiated **12% gross with escalator** (15% above $2M, 18% above $5M), $50K Year 1 guarantee, and termination clause with audience portability if sales dropped below $300K for two quarters. Year 1: $96K. Year 2: $240K. Year 3: $420K (escalator triggered). **Total: $760K** vs. ~$75K in affiliate commissions.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** The performance escalator was more valuable than equity here. As sales grew from his audience's conversion, his share grew proportionally. The termination + portability clause was the real leverage — the company couldn't risk losing his 3x conversion to a competitor.</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>

<SbCase label="Case 03 · Fashion · Mid-Career" name="Yuki Tanaka — Streetwear Designer" tag="Fashion / Apparel" outcome="$525K over 3 years">
  <SbCaseText>Yuki, 75K followers but deep credibility in sneaker culture, had three offers: a major brand ($150K flat + 3% wholesale), an emerging brand (15% gross + creative control), and an incubator (35% equity + $25K). He needed income stability and couldn't afford to wait 3+ years for an equity payout.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The shift:** He chose **15% gross with the emerging brand** — $75K Year 1 guarantee (recoupable Year 1 only), full creative approval, and acceleration clause. Year 1: $90K. Year 2: $165K. Year 3: $270K. **Total: $525K** — 59% more than the major brand licensing deal, with creative control he wouldn't have had elsewhere.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** Revenue share with guarantee gave him both income stability and creative integrity. The equity deal could have paid more long-term — but he couldn't afford the wait. Matching the structure to your financial reality is as important as maximizing theoretical upside.</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>
