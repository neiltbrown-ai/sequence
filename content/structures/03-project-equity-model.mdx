<SbSection id="overview">

<SbHeading>Overview</SbHeading>

The Project Equity Model reimagines the creative professional relationship by shifting from pure fee-for-service to strategic investment partnership. Instead of accepting standard market-rate cash compensation, you provide services at **40–70% of your normal fee** in exchange for equity ownership — typically **0.5–5%** — in the client's company. This transforms you from vendor to stakeholder, aligning long-term incentives and creating potential for exponential returns that no project fee could generate.

This isn't merely "accepting stock instead of cash." It's a disciplined portfolio-building strategy where each equity position represents a calculated bet on future value creation. The mathematics are compelling: trading $50K in foregone fees for 2% equity in a company valued at $5M that exits at $100M means your stake becomes **$1.2–1.5M after dilution.** That's a 24–30x return on the cash you didn't take. Do this across 10–15 companies with varied success rates, and the portfolio mathematics create wealth that pure fee-for-service work never could.

The model works best when you have sufficient cash flow to absorb reduced fees, strong judgment about which founders will succeed, meaningful scope where your contribution impacts company value, and patience to wait 3–7+ years for liquidity events. It requires shifting mindset from "maximizing this year's income" to **"building long-term wealth through ownership."**

<SbSection id="when-to-use">

<SbHeading>When to Use This Structure</SbHeading>

<SbFlags>
  <SbFlagCol header="Use When">
    <SbFlag type="use">Your cash flow is healthy — you can absorb a 30–60% fee reduction</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="use">Company has a clear path to exit (acquisition or IPO within 5–7 years)</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="use">Your work is central to company value (brand, product, go-to-market)</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="use">Founding team has relevant experience and complementary skills</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="use">Company is pre-Series B — earlier stage means more equity for same cash</SbFlag>
  </SbFlagCol>
  <SbFlagCol header="Avoid When">
    <SbFlag type="avoid">You need the full cash for living expenses or financial obligations</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="avoid">Company has no clear exit path — lifestyle businesses don't generate liquidity</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="avoid">Founder is defensive about equity terms or won't share financials</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="avoid">Project scope is too small to justify equity complexity (under $25K fee)</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="avoid">You can't assess the business model or market opportunity</SbFlag>
  </SbFlagCol>
</SbFlags>

<SbSection id="protect">

<SbHeading>Key Protections</SbHeading>

Equity deals require more legal precision than cash deals. Five provisions that separate smart equity-for-services from giving away your work for free.

<SbCards>
  <SbCard label="Information Rights">
    <SbCardText>"Company shall provide quarterly unaudited financials within 30 days of quarter close, notice of all fundraising activities or acquisition discussions, and access to cap table upon request."</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Without visibility into company performance, you're flying blind on an illiquid asset. You need to track your equity value and make informed decisions about pro-rata participation.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Single-Trigger Acceleration">
    <SbCardText>"Upon Change of Control (acquisition, merger, or sale of substantially all assets), 100% of unvested equity shall immediately vest." Push for single-trigger; accept double-trigger (50% on acquisition, 50% if terminated within 12 months) as compromise.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Without acceleration, an acquirer can cancel your unvested equity. If you've delivered the work that made the company valuable, you deserve the full stake.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Pro-Rata Rights">
    <SbCardText>"Contractor shall have right to purchase pro-rata portion of future equity rounds to maintain ownership percentage." Example: You own 2%, company raises Series B with 25% dilution. Without pro-rata, you drop to 1.5%. With it, you invest to stay at 2%.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Small cash investments ($5–15K) to maintain your percentage can protect hundreds of thousands in equity value. This is the most underrated protection in equity deals.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Valuation Clarity (409A)">
    <SbCardText>"Equity percentage calculated based on Company's most recent 409A valuation, on fully-diluted basis including all outstanding shares, options, warrants, and convertible instruments." Always ask for the 409A — not the preferred stock valuation from the last funding round.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Companies routinely quote inflated Series A valuations to make equity percentages seem more generous. A $20M preferred valuation might have a $8M 409A — your 1% is worth $80K, not $200K.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Vesting Aligned to Scope">
    <SbCardText>Standard startup vesting is 4 years with 1-year cliff. But if your project is 3–6 months, negotiate 2-year vesting with 6-month cliff. Your vesting timeline should reflect your contribution timeline, not the company's employee retention schedule.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** 4-year vesting on a 3-month project means you lose 75% of your equity if the relationship ends after year one. Match the lock-up to the engagement.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
</SbCards>

<SbSection id="manipulations">

<SbHeading>Common Manipulations</SbHeading>

Four tactics that inflate apparent equity value while reducing what you actually receive. Each one is preventable with the right contract language.

<SbCards>
  <SbCard label="The Inflated Valuation" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Company says "We're raising Series A at $30M valuation," offers 0.5% equity claiming it's worth $150K. You accept thinking 3:1 equity-to-cash ratio. Reality: 409A common stock valuation is $12M. Your 0.5% is worth $60K — a 1.2:1 ratio, not 3:1.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Preferred stock valuations from funding rounds are always higher than common stock 409A valuations. Founders know this — and use the larger number to make offers look generous.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>I appreciate the offer. I'd like to base our equity calculation on the most recent 409A valuation rather than the preferred stock valuation from your Series A. Can you share the 409A? That ensures we're both working from the same fair market value.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="The Future-Dilution Surprise" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Company grants you 2% equity. Doesn't mention they're raising a $15M Series B next month with 25% dilution. Your 2% becomes 1.5% immediately after funding. The company knew — you didn't.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Founders withhold fundraising timelines to lock in equity grants before dilution events. Without asking, you won't know until it's too late.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>Before we finalize terms, I need to understand the fundraising timeline. Is there any round planned in the next 12 months? I'd like to either negotiate my percentage post-funding or include anti-dilution provisions in the agreement.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="The Unrealistic Vesting Cliff" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Your project is a 3-month engagement. Company proposes 4-year vesting with 2-year cliff. If the relationship ends after 18 months — after you've delivered everything — you get zero equity.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Long vesting cliffs on short engagements are designed to minimize the equity the company actually pays out. The cliff is calibrated so you're likely to leave before it triggers.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>My scope of work is 3 months, so 4-year vesting doesn't align with my contribution timeline. I'd like 2-year vesting with a 6-month cliff instead — this protects both of us while matching the engagement.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="The 'We Can't Share Financials' Excuse" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** You request information rights. Company says "We can't share financials with contractors for confidentiality reasons." You accept equity without visibility. Company performs poorly, equity becomes worthless, you never knew.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Information rights are standard for equity holders. If a company won't provide quarterly financials to someone they're granting equity, that's not a confidentiality issue — it's a red flag about what those financials would show.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>I'm taking meaningful risk by accepting equity instead of cash. Information rights are standard for equity holders — I need visibility into company performance to justify this tradeoff. If we can't agree on basic transparency, I'll need to take the full cash fee instead.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
</SbCards>

<SbSection id="negotiate">

<SbHeading>Negotiation</SbHeading>

Your leverage determines your strategy. Three approaches based on how critical your creative contribution is to the company's trajectory and how much the founder values partnership over vendor relationships.

<SbNeg tabsJson='["High Leverage", "Moderate", "Low Leverage"]'>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Demand 3–5x equity-to-cash ratio** at current 409A valuation. "I'm foregoing $60K in cash — I need equity currently worth $180K–$300K to justify the risk and illiquidity."</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**Push for single-trigger acceleration,** pro-rata rights, and milestone equity grants. Add an advisor conversion option for ongoing $5K/month retainer after project completion.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**Your work is mission-critical** — brand identity, core product, go-to-market. Founder approached you specifically. Few alternatives exist with your expertise.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Accept 2–3x equity-to-cash ratio** with strong protective provisions. Retain 55–65% cash to maintain income stability.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**Lock in vesting aligned to scope** (2-year max for 3–6 month projects). Negotiate information rights and double-trigger acceleration as minimums.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**Multiple creatives could do the work,** but you bring specific value. Founder is open to equity partnerships but hasn't committed to you specifically.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Take higher cash (70–80%)** with smaller equity (0.5–1%). Treat equity as optionality, not compensation. Don't sacrifice income for speculative upside.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**Propose a project-first approach** — deliver at full rate, then convert to equity deal for phase two if the relationship proves strong.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**You're pursuing the opportunity** more than they're pursuing you. Company is early-stage with high failure probability. Prove value first, negotiate equity later.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
</SbNeg>

<SbSection id="value">

<SbHeading>Example Value Calculation: Priya's Equity Portfolio</SbHeading>

Priya is a brand strategist with 8 years of experience and $40K in savings. Her normal fee is $80K for a 4-month brand identity engagement. A pre-seed startup with a repeat founder offers her the choice: full cash or equity deal.

<SbChart title="Same 4-Month Project — Two Outcomes After 42 Months">
  <SbChartRow label="Equity structure ($40K cash + 3%)" value="$2.23M" pct="100" />
  <SbChartRow label="Full cash fee" value="$80K" pct="4" />
</SbChart>

**Without the structure:** Priya takes the $80K, delivers excellent work. The startup she built the brand for gets acquired for $85M three years later. She made $80K. Her brand work was central to the acquisition.

**With the structure:** Priya accepts $40K cash + 3% equity with single-trigger acceleration and pro-rata rights. She invests $7.2K at Series A to maintain her percentage. When the company sells for $85M, her 3% (after liquidation preferences) pays **$2.19M.** Total compensation: $2.23M — a **28x return** on the $40K she didn't take in cash.

**What changed:** Priya had the cash reserves to absorb the fee reduction, the judgment to pick a repeat founder in a growing market, and the negotiation skill to secure protections that preserved her full stake through exit.

<SbSection id="cases">

<SbHeading>Example Use Cases</SbHeading>

How three creatives at different career stages structured equity deals — and what happened.

<SbCase label="Case 01 · Consumer Goods · Emerging" name="Ines Moreau — Brand Designer" tag="Consumer" outcome="$2.23M over 3.5 years">
  <SbCaseText>Ines had 6 years of brand design experience and $40K in savings when a pre-seed sustainable home goods startup approached her for a 4-month brand identity engagement. The founder had a previous $18M exit. Normal fee: $80K. Offer: $30K cash + 4% equity at $1.5M valuation.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The shift:** She countered to **$40K cash + 3%** with single-trigger acceleration and pro-rata rights. Invested $7.2K at Series A to maintain percentage. Company was acquired 42 months later for $85M — her 3% after preferences paid $2.19M. **Total: $2.23M** vs. $80K full cash fee.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** Pro-rata rights were the decisive protection. Her $7.2K investment at Series A preserved $150K+ in equity value. And she evaluated the founder's track record before taking the deal — previous exit was the strongest signal.</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>

<SbCase label="Case 02 · Fintech · Established" name="Rafael Kwon — Content Strategist" tag="Fintech / SaaS" outcome="$1.69M over 3 years">
  <SbCaseText>Rafael, 12 years in content strategy, was offered $120K cash + 0.3% options for a 6-month engagement at a Series A fintech ($25M ARR, $75M valuation). He recognized the options structure would create a significant tax event and the 4-year vesting didn't match his 6-month scope.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The shift:** He negotiated **$125K cash + 0.25% restricted stock** (not options) with 3-year vesting and a 1-year cliff, plus an 83(b) election. He continued as a $5K/month advisor after the project. When the company was acquired for $850M at month 36, his 0.19% (after dilution) paid $1.51M. **The restricted stock vs. options negotiation saved ~$280K in taxes.**</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** At later stages, equity type matters as much as percentage. Stock vs. options, 83(b) elections, and tax structure can represent hundreds of thousands in real value. And continuing the relationship as an advisor added $54K in cash while deepening his position.</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>

<SbCase label="Case 03 · SaaS · Mid-Career" name="Juno Park — Mobile Developer" tag="SaaS / Tech" outcome="$4.67M over 4 years">
  <SbCaseText>Juno, a senior mobile developer with 10 years experience, was offered $60K cash + 2% options to build iOS and Android apps for a pre-seed B2B startup ($3M valuation). He calculated the 1:1 equity-to-cash ratio was too low for pre-seed risk — he wanted 3–5x to justify the failure probability.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The shift:** He negotiated **$70K cash + 3% common stock** with a milestone bonus: an additional 1% if the company raised Series A within 24 months. Apps drove 40% signup growth, Series A closed at month 14 (triggering the bonus), and he converted to $5K/month advisor. Acquisition at $180M — his 3.2% (after dilution) paid $4.48M. **Total: $4.67M** vs. $120K full fee.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** Milestone equity grants can double your position without renegotiating the original deal. Juno's 1% bonus grant — contingent on Series A — cost the company nothing if they failed and rewarded him for directly enabling the raise. The advisor conversion kept him engaged and earning cash while equity appreciated.</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>
