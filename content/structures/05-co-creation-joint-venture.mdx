<SbSection id="overview">

<SbHeading>Overview</SbHeading>

Co-Creation Joint Ventures transform the creative professional from vendor to co-owner. You and a partner — a brand, manufacturer, production company, or hospitality group — form a **new legal entity** (typically an LLC) to build and commercialize a product, brand, or venture from the ground up. Unlike project equity (#3) where you take a small stake in someone else's company, a JV means shared ownership (**30–50%** for the creative partner), shared governance through board representation, and shared profit from day one.

The core exchange: you contribute creative assets — brand, design, audience, taste, IP — while your partner contributes capital, manufacturing, distribution, or operational infrastructure. These contributions are valued, and ownership splits accordingly. A designer contributing $250K in brand value and creative direction paired with a manufacturer investing $375K in tooling and working capital might split **40/60**. The designer couldn't manufacture at scale. The manufacturer couldn't differentiate without the designer's vision. Together, they build something neither could alone.

The advantages are powerful: **30–50% ownership** (vs. 1–5% in project equity deals or 5–10% royalty in licensing), profit distributions from operations, creative control through board votes, and enterprise value if the venture is acquired. The risks are equally significant: **40–60% of JVs fail within 5 years**, profits may take 2–5 years to materialize, partner dynamics can strain, and the operational commitment is real — 10–30 hours/week, not a one-time creative delivery.

<SbHeading>When to Use This Structure</SbHeading>

<SbFlags>
  <SbFlagCol header="Use When">
    <SbFlag type="use">Your creative contribution is central to venture value (brand-led, taste-driven)</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="use">Partner brings capabilities you completely lack (manufacturing, distribution, capital)</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="use">Venture has clear revenue model and path to $10M+ within 5 years</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="use">You can commit 10–30 hours/week for 2–5+ years</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="use">You have financial runway to survive 2–3 years without profit distributions</SbFlag>
  </SbFlagCol>
  <SbFlagCol header="Avoid When">
    <SbFlag type="avoid">Partner wants you to contribute cash — you should contribute creativity, not capital</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="avoid">No clear revenue model ("we'll figure out monetization later")</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="avoid">You need income immediately — JVs take 2–5 years to pay out</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="avoid">No operating agreement exists or partner resists formalizing governance</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="avoid">Ownership split is below 20% for a central creative role</SbFlag>
  </SbFlagCol>
</SbFlags>

<SbSection id="protect">

<SbHeading>Key Protections</SbHeading>

Joint ventures require the most legal precision of any structure. Five provisions that protect your ownership, your brand, and your exit.

<SbCards>
  <SbCard label="Pre-Formation Operating Agreement">
    <SbCardText>"Operating Agreement must be signed by all parties before Creative Partner begins any work or contributes any IP." Include: ownership percentages, contribution valuations, vesting (if applicable), IP ownership, decision-making authority, and exit provisions — all in writing before you create anything.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Handshake deals are the #1 cause of JV disputes. Once you've contributed creative work without a signed agreement, your leverage to negotiate fair terms drops to zero.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Personal Brand Protection">
    <SbCardText>"Creative Partner retains exclusive rights to Personal Brand (name, likeness, social media accounts) in all contexts outside of JV. Upon departure, JV's license to Personal Brand terminates within 12 months with inventory wind-down." Your name is your career — the JV gets a license, not ownership.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** If the JV owns your name permanently, leaving means losing your identity. Every future career move depends on this clause.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Capital Call Protection">
    <SbCardText>"Creative Partner's ownership shall not be diluted by more than 10% per capital call. Alternative: Creative Partner may contribute additional services in lieu of cash at fair market value." Without this, an operational partner can repeatedly call capital and dilute your stake to nothing.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** You contributed creativity, not cash. If the venture needs more capital, your partner should fund it — not use your inability to write checks as a dilution weapon.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Buyout Provisions with Shotgun Clause">
    <SbCardText>Trigger after Year 3. Valuation: average of two independent appraisals OR 4x trailing revenue, whichever is higher. Include shotgun clause: one partner names a price, the other must buy or sell at that price. This self-regulates fairness — no one lowballs knowing the other can flip the offer.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Partnerships sour. Without a clean exit mechanism, you're trapped in a venture with a hostile co-owner and no way out except expensive litigation.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Tag-Along / Drag-Along Rights">
    <SbCardText>Tag-along: if your partner sells their stake, you can sell yours on the same terms (prevents being left with an unknown new partner). Drag-along: if 75%+ of equity holders approve an acquisition, all must sell (prevents minority blocking an attractive exit).</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Without tag-along, your partner can cash out while you're stuck holding illiquid equity with a stranger. Without drag-along, a minority partner can block a life-changing exit.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
</SbCards>

</SbSection>

<SbSection id="manipulations">

<SbHeading>Common Manipulations</SbHeading>

Four tactics that extract disproportionate value from the creative partner. Each one is common in JV negotiations and preventable with the right contract language.

<SbCards>
  <SbCard label="The 'Equal Partners' Unequal Split" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Partner proposes 50/50 ownership. You contribute: brand ($200K value) + design ($100K) + ongoing creative direction (10 hrs/wk for years). They contribute: $50K initial capital + operations billed to JV at cost. Your $300K+ contribution vs. their $50K, but ownership is "equal."</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** When contributions are unequal, "equal ownership" means you're subsidizing your partner. The split should reflect actual contribution value.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>I value partnership, but let's align ownership with contribution value. My creative contribution — brand, design, ongoing direction — values at $300K using market rates. Your capital contribution is $50K. A fair split based on these valuations would be closer to 70/30. I'm open to 60/40 if we include a salary for my ongoing creative direction.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="The Hidden Salary Scheme" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Operational partner is also Managing Member with $150K salary paid from JV before profit distributions. Revenue: $500K. Expenses including their salary: $350K. "Profit" of $150K split 50/50 — you get $75K, they get $75K + $150K salary = $225K. Three times your take on "equal" ownership.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Management fees and partner salaries paid before distributions shift the real economics dramatically. What looks like 50/50 is actually 25/75.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>I'd like to review all compensation flowing to partners before profit distributions. If one partner receives a management salary, that needs to be reflected in the operating agreement and both partners should either receive comparable compensation or neither should until profitability is established.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="The Valuation Lowball Buyout" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Partnership sours after 3 years. Operational partner triggers buyout. Their appraiser values JV at $2M (your 40% = $800K). You believe the JV is worth $5M (your 40% = $2M). They argue "appraisal is binding" using a friendly appraiser.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Single-appraiser buyouts are designed to be manipulated. The triggering party controls the valuation narrative.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>Our operating agreement requires the average of two independent appraisals — one selected by each partner. If the two differ by more than 25%, a third appraiser is selected jointly. Alternatively, I'm happy to use the shotgun clause: name your price, and I'll decide whether to buy or sell at that number.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="The IP Theft on Exit" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** You contribute creative work to JV. Partnership ends. Operational partner claims JV owns all IP — including your creative methodology and process. They start a competing venture using "your" frameworks with a different designer's name on it.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Without explicit IP carve-outs, JV ownership of "work product" can be interpreted broadly enough to capture your general expertise.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>The operating agreement distinguishes between JV-specific deliverables (owned by JV) and my general creative methodology, processes, and approaches (retained by me). Upon dissolution, I may use similar creative approaches for other ventures. Only deliverables created specifically for this JV remain with the entity.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
</SbCards>

<SbSection id="negotiate">

<SbHeading>Negotiation</SbHeading>

Your leverage determines your strategy. Three approaches based on how central your creative contribution is to the venture's value proposition.

<SbNeg tabsJson='["High Leverage", "Moderate", "Low Leverage"]'>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Demand 45–60% ownership** reflecting that your brand/creative vision is the primary value driver. "Without my name and creative direction, this is just another product line. With me, it's a destination brand."</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**Push for creative control clauses,** personal brand protection with 12-month wind-down, and shotgun buyout provision. Negotiate a design retainer ($50K+/year) on top of equity and distributions.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**Your brand drives customer acquisition.** Partner approached you specifically. Your audience or reputation is the venture's primary differentiator.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Accept 35–45% ownership** with strong protective provisions. Focus negotiation energy on governance rights, capital call protection, and buyout terms rather than pushing ownership above 50%.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**Lock in board representation** (at least 2 of 4–5 seats), unanimous consent for major decisions, and right of first refusal on expansion opportunities.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**Both contributions are essential** — your creative vision and their operations/capital are roughly balanced. Neither party could execute alone.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Accept 25–35% ownership** with a consulting retainer to maintain income during the build phase. Negotiate milestone equity grants that increase your stake if the venture hits revenue targets.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**Propose a trial phase** — 6-month consulting engagement before formalizing the JV. Prove your value, build relationship, then negotiate from a stronger position.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**Partner has multiple creative options.** Your contribution matters but isn't irreplaceable. The venture is capital-intensive and your creativity is one piece of a larger puzzle.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
</SbNeg>

<SbSection id="value">

<SbHeading>Example Value Calculation: Sofia's Furniture JV</SbHeading>

Sofia is a furniture designer with 8 years of experience and a growing Instagram following. A mid-size manufacturer with retail distribution approaches her about launching a co-branded furniture line. The manufacturer's initial offer: 8% royalty on wholesale revenue.

<SbChart title="4-Year Total Compensation — JV Ownership vs. Licensing Royalty">
  <SbChartRow label="JV (40% ownership)" value="$10.48M" pct="100" />
  <SbChartRow label="Licensing (8% royalty)" value="$1.08M" pct="10" />
</SbChart>

**Without the structure:** Sofia accepts the 8% royalty. Over 4 years, the line generates $13.5M wholesale. Her total: **$1.08M** in royalties. No ownership, no board seat, no exit value. She built a brand for someone else.

**With the structure:** Sofia counters with a JV — "Sofia Reyes Home, LLC" — at **40% ownership.** She contributes brand + design ($250K value), the manufacturer contributes $375K in tooling and capital. Sofia receives a $50K/year design retainer plus quarterly profit distributions. Year 4: the line is acquired for $25M. Her 40% = **$10M** exit payout. Total over 4 years: $10.48M — nearly **10x** the licensing alternative.

**What changed:** Sofia recognized that her name on the product was the primary value driver. Instead of renting her brand for 8%, she co-owned the venture it built. The manufacturer couldn't replicate her creative vision. She couldn't replicate their retail relationships. The JV combined both — and she captured the enterprise value her brand created.

<SbSection id="cases">

<SbHeading>Example Use Cases</SbHeading>

How three creatives at different career stages structured joint ventures — and what happened.

<SbCase label="Case 01 · Consumer / Home · Mid-Career" name="Sofia Reyes — Furniture Designer" tag="Consumer Goods" outcome="$10.48M over 4 years">
  <SbCaseText>Sofia, 8 years in custom furniture design ($200K/year income), was offered an 8% wholesale royalty to create a branded furniture line with a manufacturer. She calculated: 8% on wholesale = ~4% of retail. At $5M retail, she'd earn the same $200K she already made — with no ownership and no control.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The shift:** She proposed a JV instead. "Sofia Reyes Home, LLC" — **40/60 ownership split.** Her contribution: brand + design ($250K value). Their contribution: tooling + capital ($375K). Board: 4 members (2 each). She retained personal brand rights with 24-month wind-down. Year 1: $1.2M revenue. Year 4: acquired for $25M. Her 40% = $10M exit + $480K in retainer and distributions.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** Board representation was as important as the ownership percentage. In Year 2, the manufacturer wanted to reduce quality to cut costs — Sofia's board vote blocked it. That quality positioning is what made the line attractive to the acquirer.</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>

<SbCase label="Case 02 · Media / Content · Established" name="James Okafor — Documentary Filmmaker" tag="Media / Content" outcome="$2.13M over 3 years">
  <SbCaseText>James, a YouTube creator with 2M subscribers, was offered $100K talent fee + 5% net profit participation to host a documentary series for a streaming platform via an independent production company. He'd seen enough "net profit" statements to know that 5% of net = $0.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The shift:** He proposed a JV — "Okafor Studios, LLC" — at **50/50 ownership.** His contribution: personal brand ($500K value) + creative direction ($250K) + on-camera talent ($150K/series). Their contribution: production financing ($400K/series) + distribution relationships. He negotiated $75K/series talent fee + profit distributions. Three series, licensed to major streamers. **Total: $2.13M** vs. $300K on the original offer.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** IP ownership was the long-term play. Okafor Studios owns the series — they can re-license internationally, create spinoffs, and build a catalog. The talent deal would have left him with nothing after the checks cleared.</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>

<SbCase label="Case 03 · Food / Hospitality · Emerging" name="David Lim — Chef" tag="Hospitality" outcome="$7.13M over 5 years">
  <SbCaseText>David, 12 years experience as sous chef at a Michelin-starred restaurant, was offered $120K salary + 10% "restaurant profits" by a hospitality group. He recognized 10% of restaurant "profits" meant near-zero after overhead allocation, management fees, and depreciation were deducted.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The shift:** He proposed a JV — "Lim's Kitchen, LLC" — at **35/65 ownership.** His contribution: brand + recipes + creative direction ($350K value). Their contribution: $2.5M buildout + operations. He took $100K salary (below market, offset by equity). Michelin star Year 3. Expanded to 2 locations. Year 5: acquired for $18M. **Total: $7.13M** (exit + salary + distributions) vs. $650K on the original offer.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** Right of first refusal on expansion was the decisive clause. When the group opened Location 2, David's ROFR gave him 35% of the new entity. Without it, they'd have expanded his concept without him — and kept 100% of future locations.</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>
