<SbSection id="overview">

<SbHeading>Overview</SbHeading>

Subsidiary Rights Retention means licensing only your **primary rights** (one format, one territory, one term) while explicitly retaining ownership of everything else — sequels, adaptations, foreign editions, merchandise, audio, derivatives, and formats that don't exist yet. Instead of selling "all rights in perpetuity" for a single payment, you license the specific use the buyer needs and keep everything else for separate negotiations at market rates as your IP proves its value.

The math is dramatic. An author sells "all rights" for $100K. That same book generates $30K in audio rights, $150K in film options, $200K in foreign licensing, and $500K in sequel advances — but the author sees none of it. With subsidiary retention: $70K primary (30% less upfront) + $880K in separately negotiated subsidiaries = **$950K total** (9.5x the all-rights deal). The lower upfront payment funds a portfolio of retained rights that compounds as the work succeeds.

Your creative work can be exploited in dozens of ways across formats, territories, and time periods. Each exploitation should be **separately valued and negotiated.** When you sell film rights to your book, the buyer shouldn't automatically get sequel rights, streaming rights, merchandise, theme parks, and video games — especially when those weren't contemplated (or valued) in the original negotiation. Subsidiary retention ensures you capture value from future exploitations the buyer didn't imagine when signing the deal.

<SbHeading>When to Use This Structure</SbHeading>

<SbFlags>
  <SbFlagCol header="Use When">
    <SbFlag type="use">IP has multi-exploitation potential (franchise concepts, adaptable stories)</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="use">Characters, world-building, or concepts could support standalone works</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="use">Genre translates across formats (thriller, fantasy, sci-fi, horror)</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="use">Can afford 20–30% reduction in upfront payment</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="use">Willing to actively manage subsidiary licensing over 5–10+ years</SbFlag>
  </SbFlagCol>
  <SbFlagCol header="Avoid When">
    <SbFlag type="avoid">Work is commodity or work-for-hire (no franchise potential)</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="avoid">Desperately need maximum upfront cash (can't afford reduction)</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="avoid">No interest in managing IP long-term (want to create and move on)</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="avoid">Single buyer exists with no competition (zero leverage)</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="avoid">Legal costs ($5–25K) exceed potential subsidiary value</SbFlag>

<SbSection id="protect">

<SbHeading>Key Protections</SbHeading>

Five provisions that ensure subsidiary retention is enforceable and valuable.

<SbCards>
  <SbCard label="Explicit Retention Language (Enumerate Everything)">
    <SbCardText>"Author expressly retains all rights not explicitly granted herein, including: (a) sequel, prequel, and spin-off rights, (b) adaptation rights (film, TV, audio, stage, video game, graphic novel), (c) foreign language and territorial rights beyond [primary territory], (d) merchandise and commercial rights, (e) derivative works beyond Publisher's specific grant, (f) formats not existing as of Execution Date."</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Vague contracts lead to disputes. "All rights" gives buyers everything. Even "derivative works" is dangerously broad — buyers claim sequels are derivatives. Enumerate every retained right explicitly. If it's not listed as granted, it's retained. If it's not listed as retained, it's vulnerable.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="No Implied Rights / Future Formats Protection">
    <SbCardText>"Publisher acquires no implied rights beyond those explicitly granted. All rights not explicitly granted are retained by Author. 'Granted Rights' are limited to: [specific format], [specific territory], [specific term]. Author retains all rights to formats, distribution methods, and technologies not existing as of Execution Date."</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** E-books, streaming, AI training — none existed when many foundational contracts were written. Creators lost billions in formats they couldn't have anticipated. The "future formats" clause prevents buyers from exploiting your work in ways neither party could foresee.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Limited or No Right of First Refusal">
    <SbCardText>**Reject if possible:** "Author has no obligation to offer retained rights to Publisher." **Acceptable compromise (sequels only):** "Publisher has one-time ROFR on first sequel. 60-day exercise window. Must publish within 18 months at market rates. All other subsidiaries — no ROFR."</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** ROFR on all subsidiaries creates marketplace obstacles — third parties won't negotiate seriously if the buyer can match any offer. Limit ROFR to sequels (logical given primary publisher relationship) and eliminate it for all other subsidiary rights.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Separate Accounting (No Cross-Collateralization)">
    <SbCardText>"All subsidiary rights licensed by Author shall have separate accounting. Primary and subsidiary deals shall not be cross-collateralized. Each exploitation reports revenue independently. Subsidiary revenue shall never be applied against primary advance recoupment."</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Without separate accounting, your audio revenue goes toward recouping your book advance. Your $50K audio deal generates $0 because it's applied to an unrecouped $100K book advance. Each subsidiary must stand alone financially.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Creative Approval Over Subsidiary Exploitations">
    <SbCardText>"Author maintains final approval over storylines, character depictions, creative direction, and quality standards for all subsidiary exploitations. Subsidiary licensees must receive Author's written approval before production. If quality standards are violated, Author may terminate subsidiary license."</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** A bad adaptation or low-quality merchandise damages your primary work's value and brand. Creative approval ensures subsidiary exploitations enhance rather than dilute your IP. This is what separates strategic retention from passive licensing.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
</SbCards>

</SbSection>

<SbSection id="manipulations">

<SbHeading>Common Manipulations</SbHeading>

Four tactics buyers use to acquire subsidiary rights at primary rights prices.

<SbCards>
  <SbCard label="The 'Industry Standard' All-Rights Grab" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Buyer presents contract with "Publisher acquires all rights worldwide in perpetuity in all formats." Claims "this is standard" and "everyone accepts these terms." You sign, book becomes a hit, publisher licenses film rights for $500K — you get $0 or a token percentage.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** "Standard" doesn't mean fair. All-rights deals are standard because buyers train creators to accept them. Sophisticated creators with representation routinely retain subsidiaries. The buyer's "standard" benefits the buyer.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>I understand all-rights was standard 20 years ago, but the market has evolved. I'm licensing hardcover/paperback North America with a 7-year term. I retain subsidiaries. If you want all rights, price them separately — sequel rights are $X, film option is $Y, foreign is $Z. Otherwise, I retain them.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="The Vague 'Derivative Works' Expansion" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Contract says "Publisher acquires all rights to publish and exploit the Work, including derivative works." Later, buyer claims "derivative works" includes sequels (they derive from the original), film adaptations, audiobooks, merchandise — everything. Your retained rights evaporated through one vague phrase.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** "Derivative works" under U.S. Copyright Act is dangerously broad — it technically includes any work "based upon preexisting work." Never accept vague derivative works language. Enumerate exactly what's granted and what's retained.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>I need to replace "derivative works" with specific enumeration. Granted: annotated edition and study guide only. Retained: sequels, adaptations, merchandise, and all other works continuing characters or universe. "Derivative works" as a grant is too broad and too ambiguous — it needs to be specific.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Cross-Collateralization of Subsidiary Earnings" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Publisher acquires primary rights plus options on audio and foreign. Contract cross-collateralizes: all revenue applied to recoup primary advance first. Your $100K advance hasn't earned out. Audio generates $50K. Publisher applies audio revenue to recoup book advance — you receive $0 from your audio deal.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Cross-collateralization means your subsidiary successes subsidize the buyer's primary investment risk. Each subsidiary should have separate accounting — audio revenue is audio revenue, not book advance recoupment.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>Each exploitation must have separate accounting. Audio revenue cannot recoup the book advance — they're different rights with different economics. If you want joint accounting, reduce my book advance proportionally. Otherwise, separate accounting is non-negotiable.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="The 'Matching Rights' Blockade" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Primary buyer demands right to match any third-party offer for subsidiary rights. You negotiate a $250K film deal. Must offer to publisher. Publisher matches — not to produce a film, but to block a competitor from getting it. Film rights sit on shelf for years. You can't re-offer to other producers.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Matching rights on all subsidiaries chill third-party interest (why negotiate if the buyer can swoop in?) and give the buyer a blocking tool. Limit ROFR to one specific subsidiary (sequels) and eliminate it for everything else.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>I'll give you first refusal on the first sequel — that's fair. But matching rights on all subsidiaries creates marketplace obstacles and reduces their value. Film producers won't negotiate seriously if you can match. If you want ROFR on film, pay me for that option separately — $[X] for a 12-month exclusive negotiation window.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
</SbCards>

<SbSection id="negotiate">

<SbHeading>Negotiation</SbHeading>

Subsidiary retention negotiation centers on which rights to retain, which to grant, and what premium (or discount) applies.

<SbNeg tabsJson='["High Leverage", "Moderate", "Low Leverage"]'>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Comprehensive retention — license primary only.** "I'm licensing hardcover/paperback North America for 7 years. I retain everything else — sequel, adaptation, foreign, audio, merchandise. If you want any subsidiary, license it separately at market rates."</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**No ROFR on any subsidiary.** "I license retained rights without restrictions." If buyer insists, grant first-look (not matching right) on sequels only — 60-day exclusive negotiation window, at market rates. Reject all-subsidiaries ROFR.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**Multiple buyers competing, proven commercial IP, strong representation.** You can walk away. Subsidiaries have demonstrated value (prior works adapted, strong foreign sales). Bidding competition creates leverage.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Strategic retention — keep most valuable, share or grant others.** "I retain sequel, film/TV, and merchandise (non-negotiable). I'll share foreign rights 60/40 in my favor. I'll grant audio with improved royalty. This preserves my highest-value subsidiaries while giving you participation."</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**Milestone triggers for additional subsidiaries.** "If the book sells 100K copies, you get 90-day exclusive negotiation on foreign. If it sells 250K, you get first-look on audio adaptation. Milestones reward our joint success." Renegotiate at market rates, not original deal terms.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**Growing track record, moderate leverage.** Buyer wants your IP but has alternatives. Compromise on lower-value subsidiaries to preserve highest-value ones. Relationship-building matters.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Retain sequels and future formats — accept all-rights on rest.** "I need sequel and spin-off rights — those are non-negotiable. I also retain rights to formats not yet invented. Everything else, I'll grant for an increased advance of $[X] to compensate."</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**If forced to grant all rights, add strong reversion (#29).** "All rights, 7-year term, automatic reversion on any subsidiary not exploited within 24 months." Reversion ensures unexploited subsidiaries return to you.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**First deal or limited leverage.** Subsidiary retention may not be achievable, but sequel rights and future formats cost the buyer nothing (they weren't going to exploit them anyway). Always retain something.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
</SbNeg>

<SbSection id="value">

<SbHeading>Example Value Calculation: Marcus's Debut Thriller — All-Rights vs. Retention</SbHeading>

Marcus, 32, has a completed literary thriller generating offers from three publishers. He compares selling all rights vs. strategic subsidiary retention.

<SbChart title="5-Year Outcome — Subsidiary Retention vs. All-Rights Deal">
  <SbChartRow label="Subsidiary retention (retained film, audio, foreign)" value="$850K" pct="100" />
  <SbChartRow label="All-rights deal (maximum upfront)" value="$300K" pct="35" />
</SbChart>

**All-rights path:** $125K advance + $75K royalties + $100K subsidiary splits (25% of publisher's $400K in subsidiary licensing) = **$300K total over 5 years.**

**Subsidiary retention path:** $50K advance (60% less) + $100K royalties (higher rate) + $50K audio (self-licensed) + $90K film option (option + renewal) + $120K foreign (8 territories) + $200K film purchase + $150K backend + $100K sequel advance = **$850K over 5 years.**

**The gap:** Marcus sacrificed $75K upfront ($125K → $50K) to retain subsidiaries worth $710K over 5 years. His $75K "investment" returned **9.5x.** Even if only half the subsidiaries materialized, he'd earn $425K — still 42% more than the all-rights deal.

**The risk:** If the book flopped, subsidiaries would be worthless. Marcus would earn $50K vs. $125K — $75K less. But the asymmetry (lose $75K downside vs. gain $550K+ upside) made retention the rational bet.

<SbHeading>Example Use Cases</SbHeading>

How three creatives navigated subsidiary rights — and what retention (or its absence) meant for outcomes.

<SbCase label="Case 01 · Publishing · Emerging" name="Marcus Chen — Debut Novelist's Strategic Retention" tag="Publishing / Thriller" outcome="$850K (2.8x the all-rights alternative)">
  <SbCaseText>Marcus had three publisher offers. Publisher A: $75K, all rights. Publisher B: $125K, all rights with 10-year reversion. Publisher C: $50K, hardcover/paperback North America only — Marcus retains audio, film, foreign, sequel. He chose Publisher C despite the lowest advance.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The shift:** Year 1: Audio licensed to Audible ($25K). Foreign sold to 5 territories ($65K). Year 2: Film option to indie producer ($50K). 3 more foreign territories ($55K). Year 3: Film option renewed ($40K). Sequel deal with same publisher ($100K advance). Year 4: Film greenlit, purchase price $200K + 2.5% backend. Year 5: Film released, moderate success. **5-year total: ~$850K.** If he'd taken Publisher A's all-rights offer: ~$300K total. Publisher would have captured the $550K difference.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** "Choosing the lowest advance was terrifying. But I modeled the scenarios: even if only 2 of 5 subsidiaries materialized, I'd still beat the all-rights deal. The film option alone ($290K total) was more than double Publisher A's entire advance. My agent said: 'The advance is renting your work. Retention is owning it.' She was right."</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>

<SbCase label="Case 02 · Screenwriting · Established" name="Samantha Rodriguez — Fantasy Series Renegotiation" tag="Film / Fantasy" outcome="$12M+ (vs. $4.7M under original terms)">
  <SbCaseText>Samantha published 4 bestselling fantasy novels under all-rights deals ($200K/book advance). Publisher captured $1.5M+ in subsidiary licensing — Samantha received almost nothing from those deals. For Books 5–7 (series conclusion), she demanded subsidiary retention.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The shift:** Publisher offered $400K/book all-rights. Samantha countered: $325K/book, retain film, merchandise, video game, theme park, spin-off. Publisher resisted ("We made you successful"). Samantha held firm — she could take the series to a competitor. Final deal: $325K/book, publisher gets first-look on foreign/audio, Samantha retains everything else. **5-year results:** Film deal ($4M total), merchandise ($2.5M), video game ($750K), theme park option ($1M). **Total: $12M+** vs. $4.7M under original all-rights terms.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** "The publisher tried guilt: 'We invested in your career.' But I created the characters and world that generated $1.5M in subsidiaries on Books 1–4 and saw almost none of it. For Books 5–7, I captured $7.3M more by retaining rights. The key was being willing to walk. When they realized I'd actually take the series to a competitor, they negotiated."</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>

<SbCase label="Case 03 · Music · Emerging (Cautionary)" name="Elena Vasquez — The Missing Retention" tag="Music / Pop" outcome="$0 from master sale ($4.2M sold without her)">
  <SbCaseText>Elena signed a 5-album deal at age 22. No subsidiary retention. No reversion. No term limit on master ownership. She became successful: 5 albums generating $800K+/year in streaming. At contract end, she left — but the original label owned her masters permanently.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The result:** Label sold her masters for $4.2M. Elena received $0 from the sale. Her only recourse: Copyright Act Section 203 (reversion after 35 years — when she'd be 57). Meanwhile she earns 18% royalties while the label collects 82% in perpetuity. If her original contract had included "masters revert 10 years after release," she'd own $4.2M in assets today.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** "I was 22 and didn't know what subsidiary rights meant. My lawyer was the label's recommendation — a conflict of interest I didn't understand. One missing clause. That's the difference between owning $4.2M in assets and watching someone else sell them. I tell every young artist: get your own lawyer, and demand retention."</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>
