<SbSection id="overview">

<SbHeading>Overview</SbHeading>

Revenue Share Partnership pays you a percentage of **actual, verifiable gross revenue** — not manipulated "profits." When a product generates $1M in sales, your 15% revenue share means $150K, regardless of what the company claims about costs. Revenue is objective: either sales were $500K or they weren't. Your contract specifies X% of that number, calculated from transparent sources like payment processors, platform dashboards, or distributor reports. Minimal room for manipulation.

The fundamental advantage over net profit participation (#23): revenue sharing eliminates the accounting layer that studios, labels, and publishers exploit to show zero profit on commercially successful projects. You participate in **sales**, not the company's subjective interpretation of what's left after costs. This creates genuine alignment — you want the same thing the company wants: maximum revenue.

Revenue share is now the dominant model across the creator economy. YouTube pays creators 55% of ad revenue. Patreon charges 5–12% (creators keep 88–95%). Substack takes 10%. These platforms proved that transparent revenue sharing builds trust, encourages quality, and scales naturally. The structure works for individual product collaborations, platform partnerships, licensing relationships, and creator-brand deals — any context where revenue is measurable and your contribution drives sales.

<SbSection id="when-to-use">

<SbHeading>When to Use This Structure</SbHeading>

<SbFlags>
  <SbFlagCol header="Use When">
    <SbFlag type="use">Product has clear, verifiable revenue model (sales, subscriptions, licensing)</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="use">Revenue reported by third parties (platforms, distributors, payment processors)</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="use">Product will generate recurring revenue for years (not one-time event)</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="use">Your creative contribution directly impacts commercial success</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="use">Partner is transparent and willing to share detailed revenue data</SbFlag>
  </SbFlagCol>
  <SbFlagCol header="Avoid When">
    <SbFlag type="avoid">Revenue is difficult to verify (opaque B2B contracts, private enterprise sales)</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="avoid">Partner defines "net revenue" with heavy deductions (back to profit participation)</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="avoid">No audit rights or verification mechanism offered</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="avoid">Product is one-time event with no recurring revenue</SbFlag>
    <SbFlag type="avoid">You need predictable, stable income (revenue share fluctuates)</SbFlag>
  </SbFlagCol>
</SbFlags>

<SbSection id="protect">

<SbHeading>Key Protections</SbHeading>

Five provisions that ensure "revenue share" actually means sharing revenue — not profit participation in disguise.

<SbCards>
  <SbCard label="Gross Revenue Definition (Not 'Net')">
    <SbCardText>"Revenue Share calculated from Gross Revenue: all amounts received from Product sales, before ANY deductions except actual refunds and government-collected sales taxes. No deductions for distribution, marketing, platform fees, or any costs." If they insist on deducting platform/processing fees: cap at 5% of gross, verified by platform statements.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** "Net revenue" with deductions for distribution, marketing, and overhead is profit participation in disguise. A "20% net revenue share" after 85% in deductions = 3% of gross. If they call it revenue share, it should share revenue. Period.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Minimum Guarantee with Recoupment Cap">
    <SbCardText>"Creator receives minimum $60K annually, recoupable against revenue share. If revenue share does not exceed cumulative minimums within 24 months, Company waives recoupment and minimum converts to non-recoupable guarantee." This protects against slow launches without creating indefinite recoupment traps.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Without minimums, you bear 100% of product failure risk. With indefinite recoupment, a slow Year 1 creates "debt" that absorbs Years 2–3 revenue share. The 24-month recoupment cap ensures you aren't subsidizing failure forever.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Itemized Reporting + Audit Rights">
    <SbCardText>Quarterly revenue statements itemizing: each sale by date and amount, breakdown by channel (direct, distributor, platform), permitted deductions with documentation, revenue share calculation, and cumulative totals. Annual audit rights with fee recovery if under-reporting exceeds 5%.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** "Q1 revenue: $450K, your 15% = $67.5K" with no detail is unverifiable. Itemized reporting + audit rights with fee recovery creates transparency and deterrent. If accurate reporting costs nothing; if inaccurate, the partner bears the audit cost.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Acceleration on Change of Control">
    <SbCardText>"Upon sale of Company or Product, Creator's revenue share converts to lump sum: 4x trailing twelve-month revenue share, payable within 30 days of close." Alternative: "Revenue share obligation transfers to acquirer on identical terms."</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** If the partner sells the company or product, the new owner may not honor your revenue share. A buyout formula (3–5x trailing annual) ensures you're compensated fairly. Without this, acquisition can terminate your income stream overnight.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Anti-Revenue-Routing Clause">
    <SbCardText>"Revenue means amounts from end-customers, whether direct or through affiliates. For affiliate sales, Revenue calculated from retail price, not inter-company transfer price. Creator's share not reduced by routing through subsidiaries." Prevents the company from selling to its own distributor at artificially low prices.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Why it matters:** Vertically integrated companies can "sell" product to their own distribution arm at 50% of retail, then resell at market price. Your revenue share calculated on the low transfer price — cutting your payout in half without any change in actual market revenue.</SbCardText>
  </SbCard>
</SbCards>

<SbSection id="manipulations">

<SbHeading>Common Manipulations</SbHeading>

Four tactics that convert transparent revenue sharing into opaque profit participation.

<SbCards>
  <SbCard label="The 'Net Revenue' Bait-and-Switch" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Partner offers "20% revenue share" — sounds great. Contract defines it as "20% of net revenue after deductions": returns (15%), distribution fees (30%), marketing costs (25%), platform fees (10%), "other costs" (5%). Your 20% of "net" = 3% of gross. $1M in sales → $30K, not $200K.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** "Net revenue" with uncapped deductions is net profit participation wearing a revenue share mask. If you accept net definitions, demand the percentage be 2–3x higher to compensate: "15% of gross, or 35% of net — which do you prefer?"</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>When you say "20% revenue share," I need that to mean 20% of actual sales. If you're deducting costs first, that's profit sharing, not revenue sharing — and it needs a completely different percentage. I'll do 15% of gross or 35% of net with capped deductions. Which works for you?</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Revenue Routing Through Affiliates" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Company sells product to its own distribution subsidiary for $50/unit (artificially low). Subsidiary sells to market at $100/unit. Your 15% revenue share calculated on $50 = $7.50 per unit when it should be $15 on the $100 retail price.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Inter-company transfer pricing is the revenue share equivalent of overhead allocation in net profit deals. It artificially reduces the revenue base your percentage applies to. Require revenue calculated on end-customer price, not inter-company transfers.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>My revenue share must be calculated from end-customer price, not inter-company transfer pricing. If you sell through affiliated distributors, I need retail price as the revenue base. Otherwise you can halve my share by routing through a subsidiary at 50% of market rate.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="Delayed Revenue Recognition" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** Customer pays $100K for annual subscription in January. Company's accounting "recognizes" $8,333/month over 12 months. Your revenue share: calculated on $8,333 monthly, not $100K when paid. You wait 12 months for your full share on money the company already has.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Accrual accounting serves tax and GAAP purposes — not your revenue share. You should get paid when the company gets paid. Cash-basis revenue recognition ensures your revenue share tracks actual cash received.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>Revenue for my share must be recognized on a cash basis — when you receive payment, not when your accountants recognize it. You have the cash in January. I should get my share in January, not spread over 12 months of accrual accounting.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
  <SbCard label="The Minimum Guarantee Recoupment Trap" warn="true">
    <SbCardText>**How it works:** "Minimum $50K annually, recoupable against future revenue share indefinitely." Year 1: product underperforms, you receive $50K minimum but only "earned" $30K. You're $20K "in debt." Years 2–4: revenue share first recoups the minimum shortfall before you see a dollar above $50K. Indefinite recoupment can last the life of the deal.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardText>**Reality:** Indefinite recoupment turns your minimum guarantee into a loan. Cap recoupment at 24 months — if revenue share hasn't exceeded cumulative minimums by then, the shortfall is forgiven and future revenue share is paid in full.</SbCardText>
    <SbCardSub>Sample Response</SbCardSub>
    <SbScript>The minimum guarantee must have a 24-month recoupment cap. If revenue share doesn't exceed the minimum within 2 years, the shortfall is forgiven — I'm not carrying a perpetual debt for your product's slow start. After 24 months, every dollar of revenue share is mine.</SbScript>
  </SbCard>
</SbCards>

<SbSection id="negotiate">

<SbHeading>Negotiation</SbHeading>

Revenue share negotiation centers on three variables: percentage, duration, and definition. Three approaches based on leverage.

<SbNeg tabsJson='["High Leverage", "Moderate", "Low Leverage"]'>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Demand gross revenue share with minimum guarantee.** "I'm proposing 20% of gross product revenue with a $75K annual minimum for 2 years. My brand drives your sales — this structure aligns us around the revenue I'll help generate."</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**Perpetual revenue share or high buyout price.** Push for escalating percentage at revenue milestones (15% up to $1M, 20% above $1M). Monthly payments. Acceleration clause on acquisition (4x trailing annual). Detailed itemized reporting.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**Your involvement drives measurable revenue.** Unique brand, audience, or IP that the partner needs. Multiple partners competing for your participation. Product success is directly tied to your name or creative contribution.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Balance upfront fee with revenue share upside.** "I'll reduce my fee from $150K to $60K in exchange for 15% gross revenue share. The $90K fee reduction is my investment in the partnership — the minimum guarantee of $40K/year protects my downside."</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**Accept net revenue only with explicit caps:** total deductions under 10% of gross (refunds + processing only). Push for quarterly payments, audit rights, and 24-month recoupment cap on minimum guarantee.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**Valuable contributor with growing track record.** Partner wants your involvement but alternatives exist. Revenue share upside is meaningful but not guaranteed. Balanced negotiation appropriate.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
  <SbNegPanel>
    <SbNegRow label="Position">**Take majority in upfront fee, treat revenue share as bonus.** "My fee is $80K. I'll accept $50K upfront + 10% revenue share. The revenue share is bonus compensation — my upfront fee reflects fair market value regardless of product performance."</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Add">**Focus on transparency provisions** even at low leverage: itemized quarterly reporting, basic audit rights, and cash-basis revenue recognition. These cost the partner nothing and protect you.</SbNegRow>
    <SbNegRow label="Signal">**First revenue share deal or limited negotiating position.** Building portfolio experience. Better to accept fair terms with transparency provisions than fight for higher percentage you can't enforce.</SbNegRow>
  </SbNegPanel>
</SbNeg>

<SbSection id="value">

<SbHeading>Example Value Calculation: Amara's Consumer Product Partnership</SbHeading>

Amara is a product designer whose normal fee for consumer brand work is $150K. A DTC home goods company wants her to design a signature collection. She negotiates a revenue share partnership.

<SbChart title="5-Year Outcome — Revenue Share vs. Flat Fee">
  <SbChartRow label="Revenue share ($80K + 12% gross)" value="$1.41M" pct="100" />
  <SbChartRow label="Flat fee (no backend)" value="$150K" pct="11" />
</SbChart>

**The deal:** $80K upfront (reduced from $150K) + 12% of gross product revenue for 5 years. $60K annual minimum guarantee (recoupable for 24 months). Quarterly payments. Audit rights.

- **Year 1** (launch): $400K revenue → 12% = $48K. Minimum kicks in: Amara receives **$60K** (shortfall: $12K).
- **Year 2** (growth): $1.2M revenue → 12% = $144K. After recouping $12K: **$132K** paid.
- **Year 3** (momentum): $2.8M revenue → 12% = **$336K**.
- **Year 4** (maturity): $3.5M revenue → 12% = **$420K**.
- **Year 5** (plateau): $3.2M revenue → 12% = **$384K**.

**Total: $80K upfront + $1.332M revenue share = $1.41M** over 5 years. If she'd taken the $150K flat fee: $150K total. Revenue share returned **9.4x the flat fee alternative.** The $70K fee reduction generated $1.332M in revenue share — a **19x return** on foregone cash.

**What changed:** Two factors made this work: (1) gross revenue definition eliminated accounting manipulation, and (2) the product succeeded commercially. If the product had plateaued at $400K/year, Amara would have earned $300K over 5 years — still double the flat fee. Revenue share outperformed in both the upside and moderate scenarios.

<SbHeading>Example Use Cases</SbHeading>

How three creatives structured revenue share partnerships — and what drove the outcomes.

<SbCase label="Case 01 · Newsletter · Mid-Career" name="Claire Nakamura — Tech Journalist to Independent Publisher" tag="Media / Newsletter" outcome="$2.38M over 3 years (vs. $600K salaried)">
  <SbCaseText>Claire, 35, was a senior technology journalist at a major media company earning $200K/year. She had 50K Twitter followers, deep sourcing in Big Tech, and an engaged readership. She left for an independent newsletter platform offering 90/10 revenue share (90% to her, 10% to platform).</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The shift:** Priced newsletter at $10/month ($100/year). Year 1: 3,500 paid subscribers → $315K (her 90%). Year 2: 9,000 paid → $810K. Year 3: 14,000 paid → $1.26M. **3-year total: $2.38M** vs. $600K at her previous salary. When platform reached $1.2M+ annual revenue, she moved to a flat-fee platform ($200/month) — saving $120K+ annually in platform share.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** "Platform revenue share (90/10) was the perfect starting point — zero upfront costs, aligned incentives. But once I hit $1M+, that 10% became $120K/year. Moving to flat-fee was the natural evolution. Revenue share for early growth, fixed costs once you've scaled. The trajectory: salaried → platform revenue share → own infrastructure."</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>

<SbCase label="Case 02 · Skincare · Emerging" name="Hana Yoon — Creator-Brand Product Partnership" tag="Consumer / Beauty" outcome="$2.4M over 3 years (revenue share + equity hybrid)">
  <SbCaseText>Hana, 26, had 3M followers across TikTok and YouTube focused on skincare education. A beauty conglomerate offered a co-developed skincare line: $150K development fee + 12% gross product revenue share. She negotiated an addition: 15% equity in the brand entity.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The shift:** Year 1 (launch): $3.5M product revenue → $420K share. Year 2: $9M → $1.08M. Year 3: $7M → $840K (market saturation). **Revenue share total: $2.34M + $150K upfront = $2.49M** over 3 years. Equity component: brand entity valued at ~$25M by Year 3. Her 15% = $3.75M paper value. **Combined: $6.24M total value** from a partnership that cost her zero capital.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** "Revenue share provided immediate income; equity preserved long-term upside. If I'd taken a flat $750K licensing fee, I'd have left $5.5M+ on the table. But the hybrid only works with transparent partners — I see quarterly retail sales reports from every distributor, verified against my audit rights."</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>

<SbCase label="Case 03 · Gaming · Emerging (Cautionary)" name="Alex Petrov — Indie Game Developer Platform Choice" tag="Gaming / Indie" outcome="$38M self-published vs. ~$4.5M estimated with traditional publisher">
  <SbCaseText>Alex, a solo game developer, spent 2.5 years building a passion project funded by a $50K crowdfunding campaign. At launch, he had two paths: sign with a publisher (20–30% royalty after recoupment of $200K advance + costs) or self-publish on platforms taking 30% (70% to developer).</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**The shift:** Self-published across all major platforms. 3.5M copies at $12 average = $42M gross. After 30% platform fees: **$29.4M to Alex** over 3 years. Additional console ports: $8.6M net. **Total: ~$38M.** Publisher alternative estimate: $42M gross → publisher deducts $200K advance, $5M marketing, $12.6M overhead, $8M distribution → "net" of $16.2M → Alex's 25% royalty: **~$4M.** Platform revenue share delivered **9.5x** the publisher alternative.</SbCaseText>
  <SbCaseText>**Key insight:** "Platform revenue share (70/30) is fundamentally different from publisher royalties (25% of net). Both are 'revenue sharing' in name — but one shares actual revenue and the other shares what's left after manipulation. I chose transparent platforms where revenue is reported in real-time dashboards. The $34M difference between self-publishing and a publisher deal was the difference between gross revenue share and net profit participation."</SbCaseText>
</SbCase>
